<h1>Is there hope in Gerakan Harapan Baru?</h1>
<div class="clearfix"></div>
<div class="meta">
July 16, 2015 </div>
<div class="clearfix"></div>
<div class="storycontent-post">
<p class="summary">What is more important is whether its leaders will be able to convince Malaysians that they are going to move in the right direction.
</p><div class="clearfix"></div> <div class='storycontent-news-type'>COMMENT</div><div class="clearfix"></div>
<p><a href="http://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/harapan-baru_600.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-570178" alt="harapan-baru_600" src="http://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/harapan-baru_600.jpg" width="648" height="369" srcset="http://s3media.freemalaysiatoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/harapan-baru_600.jpg 648w, http://s3media.freemalaysiatoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/harapan-baru_600-300x170.jpg 300w, http://s3media.freemalaysiatoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/harapan-baru_600-105x60.jpg 105w, http://s3media.freemalaysiatoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/harapan-baru_600-218x124.jpg 218w" sizes="(max-width: 648px) 100vw, 648px"/></a></p>
<p><em><strong>By P Ramasamy</strong></em></p>
<p>Formation of political coalitions is not something new in Malaysia. The real question in the minds of the public is whether they are able to survive in the “divisive waters” of Malaysian politics.</p>
<p>Ethnic, religious, class and spatial barriers make it difficult for political coalitions to function and pro-long their activities in the country.</p>
<p>The ambitious launch of a new political party called Gerakan Harapan Baru (GHB) or New Hope Movement on July 13, 2015, by the progressive group that was purged from PAS, seems to rekindle fresh hope for the formation of a new political opposition coalition.</p>
<p>For all intents and purposes, the Pakatan Rakyat (PR) coalition informally formed before the 2008 general elections is dead and gone. The fact that the coalition lasted seven years in itself is no small achievement.</p>
<p>The formation of new political party might possibly lead to a new political coalition. Whether the new coalition would be termed as PR Baru or PR 2.0 remains to be seen.</p>
<p>Not surprisingly, the leader of this new political party is none other than the vociferous Mat Sabu, the former deputy president of PAS. The others who have rallied around Mat Sabu are Muhammed Faiz Fadzil, the son of PAS&#8217; former president; Muhammed Muhd Taib, the former Menteri Besar of Selangor; Ahmad Awang, the candidate who contested the post of president; Dzulkefly Ahmad; and others who have labelled themselves as the “Group of 18”.</p><div class='code-block code-block-1 ai-viewport-1' style='float: left; margin: 8px 8px 8px 0px;'>

<p>According to Mat Sabu, the new party, awaiting approval from the Registrar of Societies (RoS), will be all inclusive and progressive and that it would not repeat the mistakes made by PAS. It will try to embrace the aspirations of all Malaysians and offer membership at two levels&#8211;one for Muslims and the other for non-Muslims.</p>
<p>Although the controversial syariah law, Hudud, will be part of the new party&#8217;s agenda, its leaders have not fully elaborated as to how this will be implemented.</p>
<p>Mat Sabu and his friends are also aware that too much focus on Hudud will be suicidal for the new party. For this progressive group, some matters are best left untouched at least for the moment, until of course; they get formal approval from the RoS.</p>
<p>Forming a new party with the ultimate objective of forging a new and progressive coalition is something desirable especially when the country&#8217;s general election is two years away.</p>
<p>The formation of a new party also tells us that all is not well with PAS. PAS is no more the party that is united and cohesive. Internal politics is raging. Progressive members and those who have disagreed with the policies of the party have nowhere to go except to think of a new party.</p>
<p>There has been a mixed response to the new formation. DAP and PKR, as expected, have welcomed its formation. Both the parties tired and bored with the pro-Umno policies of PAS&#8217; leadership, are eagerly waiting to join hands with a new Malay-based opposition to confront Umno/BN. The Malaysian middle-class, tired of putting up with religious and racial sentiments, are looking forward to the new party so that a new coalition can be constituted to provide an alternative for Malaysians.</p>
<p>As soon as the new movement was announced, PAS leaders, not surprisingly, shot it down by saying it was formed by members who have close association with DAP, a Chinese dominated party. The core group behind the new party were labelled as &#8220;lackeys&#8221; of the DAP. One PAS leader went to the extent of saying the new party is &#8220;satanic&#8221; as its objectives were not Islamic at all.</p>
<p>Utusan Malaysia, the mouth-piece of Umno, condemned the formation of the new party as yet another attempt by Mat Sabu and group to split and weaken the Malays.</p>
<p>Forming political parties or political coalitions is not difficult. What is difficult is for them to negotiate through the rough waters of Malaysian politics. It is not enough to label the party as the “New Hope Movement”. It is more important to see if the leaders will be able to convince Malaysians that they are going to move in the right direction.</p>
<p><em>P Ramasamy is a DAP member, state assemblyman for Prai and deputy chief minister II of Penang.</em></p>
