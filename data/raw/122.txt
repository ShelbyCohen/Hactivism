<p>The <b>barracuda</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Ray-finned_fish" class="mw-redirect" title="Ray-finned fish">ray-finned fish</a> known for its large size, fearsome appearance and ferocious behaviour. The barracuda is a saltwater <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><b>Sphyraena</b></i>, the only genus in the family Sphyraenidae which was named by <a href="/wiki/Constantine_Samuel_Rafinesque" title="Constantine Samuel Rafinesque">Constantine Samuel Rafinesque</a> in 1815.<sup id="cite_ref-WoRMS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WoRMS-2">[2]</a></sup> and is found in tropical and subtropical oceans worldwide ranging from the Eastern border of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. They are found near the top of the water and near coral reefs and sea grasses.<sup id="cite_ref-pawnation1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pawnation1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barracuda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Barracuda_laban.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Photo of baracuda with coral swimming above coral" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Barracuda_laban.jpg/250px-Barracuda_laban.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Barracuda_laban.jpg/375px-Barracuda_laban.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Barracuda_laban.jpg/500px-Barracuda_laban.jpg 2x" data-file-width="999" data-file-height="750" /></a>

<p>Barracuda are snake-like in appearance, with prominent, sharp-edged, <a href="/wiki/Fang" title="Fang">fang</a>-like teeth, much like <a href="/wiki/Piranha" title="Piranha">piranha</a>, all of different sizes, set in <a href="/wiki/Tooth_socket" class="mw-redirect" title="Tooth socket">sockets</a> of their large jaws. They have large, pointed heads with an underbite in many species. Their gill covers have no spines and are covered with small <a href="/wiki/Scale_(zoology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Scale (zoology)">scales</a>. Their two <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fins</a> are widely separated, with the anterior fin having five spines, and the posterior fin having one spine and 9 soft rays. The posterior dorsal fin is similar in size to the <a href="/wiki/Anal_fin" class="mw-redirect" title="Anal fin">anal fin</a> and is situated above it. The <a href="/wiki/Lateral_line" title="Lateral line">lateral line</a> is prominent and extends straight from head to tail. The spinous dorsal fin is placed above the <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fins</a> and is normally retracted in a groove. The <a href="/wiki/Caudal_fin" class="mw-redirect" title="Caudal fin">caudal fin</a> is moderately forked with its posterior edged double-curved and is set at the end of a stout <a href="/wiki/Caudal_peduncle" class="mw-redirect" title="Caudal peduncle">peduncle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Pectoral_fins" class="mw-redirect" title="Pectoral fins">pectoral fins</a> are placed low on the sides. Its <a href="/wiki/Swim_bladder" title="Swim bladder">swim bladder</a> is large.</p>
<p>In most cases, a barracuda is dark gray, dark green, white, or blue on its upper body, with silvery sides and a chalky-white belly. Coloration varies somewhat between species. For some species, irregular and unorganized black spots or a row of darker cross-bars occur on each side. Their fins may be yellowish or dusky. Barracudas live primarily in oceans, but certain species, such as the great barracuda, live in brackish water.</p>
<p>Some species grow quite large (up to 165&#160;cm in length),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> such as <i>Sphyraena sphyraena</i>, found in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>; <i>Sphyraena picudilla</i>, ranging on the Atlantic coast of tropical America from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> to <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and reaching <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>. Other barracuda species are found around the world. Examples are <i>Sphyraena argentea</i>, found from <a href="/wiki/Puget_Sound" title="Puget Sound">Puget Sound</a> southwards to <a href="/wiki/Cabo_San_Lucas" title="Cabo San Lucas">Cabo San Lucas</a>, <i>Sphyraena jello</i>, from the seas of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a> and Archipelago.</p>

<p>Barracudas are ferocious, opportunistic <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predators</a>, relying on surprise and short bursts of speed, up to 27&#160;mph (43&#160;km/h),<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> to overtake their prey.</p>
<p>Adults of most species are more or less solitary, while young and half-grown fish frequently congregate. Barracudas prey primarily on fish (which may include some as large as themselves). They kill and consume larger prey by tearing chunks of flesh. Barracuda are competitive species and often are seen competing against <a href="/wiki/Mackerel" title="Mackerel">mackerel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Needle_fish" class="mw-redirect" title="Needle fish">needle fish</a> and sometimes even <a href="/wiki/Dolphins" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolphins">dolphins</a> for prey.<sup id="cite_ref-pawnation1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pawnation1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Barracuda feed on an array of prey including fish such as jacks, grunts, groupers, snappers, small <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tunas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mullet_(fish)" title="Mullet (fish)">mullets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Killifish" title="Killifish">killifishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herrings</a>, and anchovies by simply biting them in half.<sup id="cite_ref-atlanticpanic1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlanticpanic1-8">[8]</a></sup> They also seem to consume smaller species of sustenance that are in front of them.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interactions_with_humans">Interactions with humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barracuda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interactions with humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, some species of barracuda are reputed to be dangerous to swimmers. Barracudas are scavengers, and may mistake snorkellers for large predators, following them hoping to eat the remains of their prey. Swimmers have reported being bitten by barracuda, but such incidents are rare and possibly caused by poor visibility. Large barracudas can be encountered in muddy shallows on rare occasion. Barracudas may mistake things that glint and shine for prey.<sup id="cite_ref-flmnh_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flmnh-9">[9]</a></sup> One incident reported a barracuda jumping out of water and injuring a kayaker,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> but Jason Schratwieser, conservation director of the International Game Fish Association, said that the wound could have been caused by a <a href="/wiki/Houndfish" title="Houndfish">houndfish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Handfeeding or touching large barracudas in general is to be avoided. <a href="/wiki/Spearfishing" title="Spearfishing">Spearfishing</a> around barracudas can also be dangerous, as they are quite capable of ripping a chunk from a wounded fish thrashing on a spear, or out of the arm which is holding the spear. Humans are not on their preferred menu, but haste can lead to confusion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_food">As food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barracuda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Barracudas are popular both as food and game fish. They are most often eaten as fillets or steaks. Larger species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_barracuda" title="Great barracuda">great barracuda</a>, have been implicated in cases of <a href="/wiki/Ciguatera" title="Ciguatera">ciguatera</a> food poisoning.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Those who have been diagnosed with this type of food poisoning display symptoms of gastrointestinal discomfort, limb weakness, and an inability to differentiate hot from cold effectively.<sup id="cite_ref-atlanticpanic1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlanticpanic1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africans</a> <a href="/wiki/Smoking_(cooking)" title="Smoking (cooking)">smoke</a> them for use in <a href="/wiki/Soup" title="Soup">soups</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sauce" title="Sauce">sauces</a>. Smoking protects the soft flesh from disintegrating in the broth and gives it a smoky flavour.</p>
