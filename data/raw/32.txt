<p><b>Economy for the Common Good</b> is a social movement advocating for an alternative economic model. It calls for working towards the common good and cooperation instead of profit-orientation and competition<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which lead to greed and uncontrolled growth.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Christian Felber coined the term in his book <i>Die Gemeinwohl-Ökonomie - Das Wirtschaftsmodell der Zukunft</i>, published in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to Felber, it makes much more sense for companies to create a so-called "common good balance sheet" than a financial balance sheet. The common good balance sheet shows the extent to which a company abides by values like human dignity, solidarity and economic sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_for_the_Common_Good&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Economy for the Common Good calls for reevaluating economic relations by, for example, putting limits on financial speculation and encouraging companies to produce socially-responsible products.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_good_balance_sheet">Common good balance sheet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_for_the_Common_Good&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Common good balance sheet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over 1200 companies actively support the concept of Economy for the Common Good and a few hundred of them have committed to creating the common good balance sheet.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These include Sparda-Bank Munich,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> the Rhomberg Group and <a href="/wiki/VAUDE" title="VAUDE">Vaude</a> Outdoor.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> According to proponents of the movement, the success of a company should not be determined by how much profit it makes, but rather by the degree to which it contributes to the common good.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Companies receive more points in this balance sheet when, for example, employees are satisfied with their jobs or when the top managers do not receive exorbitantly more than the lowest paid worker.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
