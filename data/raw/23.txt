	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom firstpar"><strong><span class="srTitle">American civil rights movement</span></strong><strong>, </strong>mass protest movement against <a name="ref169162" id="ref169162" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/racial-segregation"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/488135/racial-segregation">racial segregation</span></a> and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/discrimination" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="discrimination">discrimination</a> in the southern <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/United-States"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/616563/United-States">United States</span></a> that came to national prominence during the mid-1950s. This movement had its roots in the centuries-long efforts of African <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Slave-people"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/548206/Slave">slaves</span></a> and their descendants to resist <a name="ref169165" id="ref169165" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/racism"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/488187/racism">racial oppression</span></a> and abolish the institution of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/slavery-sociology"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/548305/slavery">slavery</span></a>. Although American slaves were emancipated as a result of the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/American-Civil-War"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/19407/American-Civil-War">Civil War</span></a> and were then granted basic civil rights through the passage of the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Fourteenth-Amendment"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/215201/Fourteenth-Amendment">Fourteenth</span></a> and <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Fifteenth-Amendment"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/206459/Fifteenth-Amendment">Fifteenth amendment</span></a>s to the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Constitution-of-the-United-States-of-America"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/134197/Constitution-of-the-United-States-of-America">U.S. Constitution</span></a>, struggles to secure federal protection of these rights continued during the next century. Through nonviolent protest, the civil rights movement of the 1950s and ’60s broke the pattern of public facilities’ being segregated by <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/race-human"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/488030/race">“race”</span></a> in the <a name="ref169163" id="ref169163" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/the-South-region"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/555542/the-South">South</span></a> and achieved the most important breakthrough in equal-rights legislation for <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/African-American"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/67474/African-Americans">African Americans</span></a> since the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Reconstruction-United-States-history"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/493722/Reconstruction">Reconstruction</span></a> period (1865–77). Although the passage in 1964 and 1965 of major civil rights legislation was victorious for the movement, by then militant black activists had begun to see their struggle as a freedom or liberation movement not just seeking civil rights reforms but instead confronting the enduring economic, political, and cultural consequences of past racial oppression.</p><!--[END-1ST-PARA]--><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="9456" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/46/7546-004-7F54297C.jpg" alt="Martin Luther King, Jr. (centre), with other civil rights supporters at the March on Washington, D.C., in August 1963." data-width="550" data-height="379"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Martin Luther King, Jr. (centre), with other civil rights supporters at the March on Washington, …</div><div class="credit"><em>UPI/Bettmann/Corbis</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li></ul></div></section><section class="gutter-bottom" data-level="1" id="toc288196"><h2 class="h2">Abolitionism to Jim Crow</h2><a href="/place/United-States/The-Kennedy-and-Johnson-administrations#ref1133848" class="major-reference md-content-module-left grid-md-shrink visible-lg visible-md">

	</a><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">American history has been marked by persistent and determined efforts to expand the scope and inclusiveness of civil rights. Although <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/equality-human-rights"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/275667/equality">equal rights</span></a> for all were affirmed in the founding documents of the United States, many of the new country’s inhabitants were denied essential rights. African slaves and indentured servants did not have the inalienable right to “life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness” that British colonists asserted to justify their <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Declaration-of-Independence"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/285012/Declaration-of-Independence">Declaration of Independence</span></a>. Nor were they included among the “People of the United States” who established the Constitution in order to “promote the general Welfare, and secure the Blessings of Liberty to ourselves and our Posterity.” Instead, the Constitution protected slavery by allowing the importation of slaves until 1808 and providing for the return of slaves who had escaped to other states.</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="94617" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/42/97342-004-F74CD325.jpg" alt="Image of the Declaration of Independence (1776) taken from an engraving made by printer William J. 	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">As the United States expanded its boundaries, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Native-American"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1357826/Native-American">Native American</span></a> peoples resisted conquest and absorption. Individual states, which determined most of the rights of American <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/citizenship"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/118828/citizenship">citizens</span></a>, generally limited <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/suffrage"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/571785/suffrage">voting rights</span></a> to white property-owning males, and other rights—such as the right to own land or serve on juries—were often denied on the basis of racial or gender distinctions. A small proportion of African Americans lived outside the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Slave-people"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/548206/Slave">slave</span></a> system, but those so-called “free blacks” endured racial discrimination and enforced <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/racial-segregation"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/488135/racial-segregation">segregation</span></a>. Although some slaves violently rebelled against their enslavement (<em>see</em><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/slave-rebellions"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/548231/slave-rebellions"> slave rebellions</span></a>), African Americans and other subordinated groups mainly used nonviolent means—protests, legal challenges, pleas and petitions addressed to government officials, as well as sustained and massive civil rights movements—to achieve gradual improvements in their status.</p><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">During the first half of the 19th century, movements to extend voting rights to non-property-owning white male labourers resulted in the elimination of most property qualifications for voting, but this expansion of suffrage was accompanied by brutal suppression of American Indians and increasing restrictions on free blacks. Slave owners in the South reacted to the 1831 <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Nat-Turner"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/610295/Nat-Turner">Nat Turner</span></a> slave revolt in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Virginia-state"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/629964/Virginia">Virginia</span></a> by passing laws to discourage antislavery activism and prevent the teaching of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Slave-people"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/548206/Slave">slaves</span></a> to read and write. Despite this repression, a growing number of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/African-American"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/67474/African-Americans">African Americans</span></a> freed themselves from slavery by escaping or negotiating agreements to purchase their freedom through wage labour. By the 1830s, free black <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="communities">communities</a> in the Northern states had become sufficiently large and organized to hold regular national conventions, where black leaders gathered to discuss <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternative" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="alternative">alternative</a> strategies of racial advancement. In 1833 a small minority of whites joined with black antislavery activists to form the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/American-Anti-Slavery-Society"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/19269/American-Anti-Slavery-Society">American Anti-Slavery Society</span></a> under the leadership of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/William-Lloyd-Garrison"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/226208/William-Lloyd-Garrison">William Lloyd Garrison</span></a>.</p><div 	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom"><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Frederick-Douglass"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/170246/Frederick-Douglass">Frederick Douglass</span></a> became the most famous of the ex-slaves who joined the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/abolitionism-European-and-American-social-movement"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1448/abolitionism">abolition movement</span></a>. His autobiography—one of many <a class="md-crosslink" href="/art/slave-narrative"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/548224/slave-narrative">slave narratives</span></a>—and his stirring orations heightened public awareness of the horrors of slavery. Although black leaders became increasingly militant in their attacks against slavery and other forms of racial oppression, their efforts to secure equal rights received a major setback in 1857, when the U.S. <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Supreme-Court-of-the-United-States"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/574815/Supreme-Court-of-the-United-States">Supreme Court</span></a> rejected African American <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/citizenship"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/118828/citizenship">citizenship</span></a> claims. The <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Dred-Scott-decision"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/171273/Dred-Scott-decision">Dred Scott decision</span></a> stated that the country’s founders had viewed blacks as so inferior that they had “no rights which the white man was bound to respect.” This ruling—by declaring unconstitutional the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Missouri-Compromise"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/385744/Missouri-Compromise">Missouri Compromise</span></a> (1820), through which <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Congress-of-the-United-States"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/132602/Congress-of-the-United-States">Congress</span></a> had limited the expansion of slavery into western territories—ironically strengthened the antislavery movement, because it angered many <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Caucasian-peoples"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/100262/Caucasian-peoples">whites</span></a> who did not own slaves. The inability of the country’s political leaders to resolve that dispute fueled the successful presidential campaign of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Abraham-Lincoln"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/341682/Abraham-Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</span></a>, the candidate of the antislavery <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Republican-Party"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/498842/Republican-Party">Republican Party</span></a>. Lincoln’s victory in turn prompted the Southern slave states to secede and form the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Confederate-States-of-America"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/131803/Confederate-States-of-America">Confederate States of America</span></a> in 1860–61.</p><a href="/quiz/history-buff-quiz" class="test-your-knowledge md-content-module-left grid-md-shrink visible-lg visible-md">
		<div class="subhead">Test Your Knowledge</div>
		<div class="image-wrapper">
			












	
	
		<img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/19/153419-118-F5EF4ED0.jpg" alt="Ruins of statues at Karnak, Egypt." />
	
<div class="title">History Buff Quiz</div>
		</div>
	</a><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Although Lincoln did not initially seek to abolish slavery, his determination to punish the rebellious states and his increasing reliance on black soldiers in the Union army prompted him to issue the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Emancipation-Proclamation"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/185468/Emancipation-Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation </span></a> (1863) to deprive the Confederacy of its slave <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/property-legal-concept"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/479008/property">property</span></a>. After the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/American-Civil-War"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/19407/American-Civil-War">American Civil War</span></a> ended, Republican leaders cemented the Union victory by gaining the ratification of <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutional" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="constitutional">constitutional</a> <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/amendments" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="amendments">amendments</a> to abolish slavery (<a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Thirteenth-Amendment"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/592556/Thirteenth-Amendment">Thirteenth Amendment</span></a>) and to protect the legal equality of ex-slaves (<a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Fourteenth-Amendment"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/215201/Fourteenth-Amendment">Fourteenth Amendment</span></a>) and the voting rights of male ex-slaves (<a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Fifteenth-Amendment"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/206459/Fifteenth-Amendment">Fifteenth Amendment</span></a>). Despite those constitutional guarantees of rights, almost a century of civil rights agitation and litigation would be required to bring about consistent federal enforcement of those rights in the former Confederate states. Moreover, after federal military forces were removed from the South at the end of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Reconstruction-United-States-history"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/493722/Reconstruction">Reconstruction</span></a>, white leaders in the region enacted new laws to strengthen the “<a name="ref1224308" id="ref1224308" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Jim-Crow-law"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/303897/Jim-Crow-law">Jim Crow</span></a>” system of racial segregation and discrimination. In its <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Plessy-v-Ferguson"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/464679/Plessy-v-Ferguson"><em>Plessy</em> v. <em>Ferguson</em></span></a> decision (1896), the Supreme Court ruled that “separate but equal” facilities for African Americans did not violate the Fourteenth <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Amendment" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="Amendment">Amendment</a>, ignoring evidence that the facilities for blacks were inferior to those intended for whites.</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="12624" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/43/2143-004-E21119B2.jpg" alt="Emancipation Proclamation, 1863." data-width="277" data-height="450"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Emancipation Proclamation, 1863.</div><div class="credit"><em>The Granger Collection, New York</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li></ul></div><div class="connect md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300 text-center ">
