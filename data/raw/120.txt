<p><i><b>Jurassic Park III</b></i> is a 2001 American <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">science-fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure film</a> and the third installment in the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park" title="Jurassic Park"><i>Jurassic Park</i> film series</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Sam_Neill" title="Sam Neill">Sam Neill</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_H._Macy" title="William H. Macy">William H. Macy</a>, <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A9a_Leoni" title="Téa Leoni">Téa Leoni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Nivola" title="Alessandro Nivola">Alessandro Nivola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Morgan_(actor)" title="Trevor Morgan (actor)">Trevor Morgan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jeter" title="Michael Jeter">Michael Jeter</a>. It is the first film in the series not to have been directed by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>, nor based on a book by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a> (though numerous scenes in the film were ultimately taken from Crichton's novels <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(novel)" title="Jurassic Park (novel)">Jurassic Park</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(Crichton_novel)" title="The Lost World (Crichton novel)">The Lost World</a></i>). The film takes place on Isla Sorna, off <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>'s Pacific coast, the island featured in the <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">second film</a>, where a divorced couple has tricked <a href="/wiki/Alan_Grant_(Jurassic_Park_character)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alan Grant (Jurassic Park character)">Dr. Alan Grant</a> into going in order to help them find their son.</p>
<p>After the success of Spielberg's <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Johnston" title="Joe Johnston">Joe Johnston</a> expressed interest in directing a sequel. Spielberg instead gave Johnston permission to direct a third film in the series, if there were to be one. Production of <i>Jurassic Park III</i> began on August 30, 2000. Despite mixed reviews from critics, the film was successful at the box office, grossing $368 million worldwide. A sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_World" title="Jurassic World">Jurassic World</a></i>, was released on June 12, 2015.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ben Hildebrand and 12-year-old Eric Kirby go <a href="/wiki/Parasailing" title="Parasailing">parasailing</a> around the waters of Isla Sorna. An unknown creature attacks and kills the boat crew, forcing Ben to detach the line; he and Eric drift towards the island. On the mainland, Dr. Alan Grant has become famous after his involvement at <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a>, while Ellie Sattler is married and has two children. Grant discusses with Sattler how raptors are far more intelligent than they had previously believed. At a dig site, Grant's assistant, Billy Brennan, demonstrates how he can use a <a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printer</a> to replicate a <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Larynx" title="Larynx">larynx</a>.</p>
<p>Paul and Amanda Kirby, a seemingly wealthy couple, offer Grant funding for his research if he will give them an aerial tour of Isla Sorna. Although initially suspicious of their true intentions, Grant is desperate for research support and thus reluctantly agrees. He flies there along with Paul, Amanda, Billy, and the Kirbys' <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenary</a> associates, Udesky, Cooper, and their pilot Nash. On the plane, Grant learns that the Kirbys actually plan to land on the island. When Grant opposes, he is knocked out by Cooper and wakes to the sound of Amanda using a <a href="/wiki/Megaphone" title="Megaphone">megaphone</a>. This attracts a <i><a href="/wiki/Spinosaurus" title="Spinosaurus">Spinosaurus</a></i>, which devours Cooper and Nash. The creature's obstructing of the runway causes the plane to crash into the forest. Fleeing, the survivors briefly lose the <i>Spinosaurus</i>, only to encounter a <i><a href="/wiki/Tyrannosaurus" title="Tyrannosaurus">Tyrannosaurus</a></i>. The <i>Spinosaurus</i> returns, but the group escapes while the two titans fight each other. The <i>Spinosaurus</i> overpowers the <i>T. rex</i> and kills it.</p>
<p>Grant, demanding the truth from the Kirbys, learns they are actually a middle-class divorced couple, who are looking for their son Eric, and Amanda's boyfriend Ben, who have been missing on the island for eight weeks. Later, the group finds the parasail with Ben's corpse attached. The group takes the parasail, and then encounter raptor nests. They find an abandoned InGen compound, where Amanda is ambushed by a raptor. The group manages to trap it, but it escapes and contacts the rest of its pack. The group flees into a herd of <i><a href="/wiki/Corythosaurus" title="Corythosaurus">Corythosaurus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Parasaurolophus" title="Parasaurolophus">Parasaurolophus</a></i>, causing a stampede, separating Grant and Udesky from the others. Grant retrieves Billy's satchel, while Udesky is left on the ground barely alive by the raptors, who set a trap in an attempt to lure the others out of the tree they had climbed into. Amanda is nearly killed by the raptors when she tries to descend to help Udesky, and upon the failure of their trap, the raptors kill Udesky before departing.</p>
<p>Grant suspects that the raptors are searching for something while observing two of them communicating. He becomes ambushed and cornered by the raptors, but is rescued by Eric, who managed to survive in an overturned water truck. The next day, Grant and Eric hear Paul's satellite phone ringing and are reunited with the Kirbys and Billy. Paul explains that he gave the phone to Nash before he was devoured, before the group is attacked by the <i>Spinosaurus</i>. After the group escapes, Grant discovers Billy took two raptor eggs to use for funding, which provoked the raptor attacks. He decides to keep the eggs to ensure the group's survival. The group unknowingly enters a large <a href="/wiki/Aviary" title="Aviary">aviary</a> used to house <i><a href="/wiki/Pteranodon" title="Pteranodon">Pteranodons</a></i>, which attack the group and fly away with Eric. Billy rescues Eric by using Ben's parasail, but is then attacked and seemingly killed by the <i>Pteranodons</i>. The rest of the group escapes the aviary, unintentionally leaving the door unlocked. They make their way downriver using a boat.</p>
<p>That night, the group hears the phone ringing buried in the feces of the <i>Spinosaurus</i> and retrieves it. As rain falls, Grant tries to contact Sattler, but the <i>Spinosaurus</i> attacks the boat. Grant and Paul scare it off by setting the boat's fuel on fire. The next day, the group makes their way towards the shoreline, but are surrounded by the raptors once again. The eggs are surrendered to the raptors, while Grant uses the replicated raptor larynx to confuse the pack, who run off with the eggs. The group flees to the coast and find that Sattler had called in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> to rescue them. They discover that Billy, while seriously injured, is still alive. As they leave the island, they see the <i>Pteranodons</i> flying free, and Grant muses they are "looking for new nesting grounds."</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creatures_on_screen">Creatures on screen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Creatures on screen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cloned_animals_in_Jurassic_Park" title="List of cloned animals in Jurassic Park">List of cloned animals in Jurassic Park</a></div>
<p>In a deviation from the previous films, the <i><a href="/wiki/Spinosaurus" title="Spinosaurus">Spinosaurus</a></i> is considered the primary antagonist:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Johnston stated, "A lot of dinosaurs have a very similar silhouette to the <i>T-Rex</i> ... and we wanted the audience to instantly recognize this as something else."<sup id="cite_ref-prodnotes_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prodnotes-4">[4]</a></sup> The silhouette of the <i>Spinosaurus</i> is also on the poster behind the <i>Pteranodon</i>, taking the place of the <i>Tyrannosaurus</i> which had been used in the previous films' posters. <i><a href="/wiki/Baryonyx" title="Baryonyx">Baryonyx</a></i> was originally considered to be the "<a href="/wiki/Big_bad" class="mw-redirect" title="Big bad">big bad</a>" before <i>Spinosaurus</i> was chosen, and early concept posters reflected this. Within film dialogue, Billy interprets the animal encountered as a <i>Baryonyx</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Suchomimus" title="Suchomimus">Suchomimus</a></i>, but Dr. Grant corrects his analysis based on its size and its sail.<sup id="cite_ref-making_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-making-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to new discoveries and theories in the field of paleontology, the portrayal of several dinosaurs differed from that of the previous films. Discoveries suggesting that <i>Velociraptor</i> were feathered prompted the addition of quill-like structures on the head and neck of the males in the film. "We've found evidence that <i>Velociraptors</i> had feathers, or feather-like structures, and we've incorporated that into the new look of the raptor", said paleontologist <a href="/wiki/Jack_Horner_(paleontologist)" title="Jack Horner (paleontologist)">Jack Horner</a>, the film's technical adviser.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spielberg insisted to Johnston that he include <i>Pteranodons</i> in the film, after they had been cut from the previous films for budget reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-Jul01_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jul01-7">[7]</a></sup> An aquatic reptile was featured in the first draft, but was ultimately removed from the final script.<sup id="cite_ref-Jul01_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jul01-7">[7]</a></sup> The special effects used for the creatures were a mixture of animatronics and <a href="/wiki/Computer_generated_imagery" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer generated imagery">CGI</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-prodnotes_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prodnotes-4">[4]</a></sup> The following creatures appear in the film:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development">Early development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joe_Johnston" title="Joe Johnston">Joe Johnston</a> had been interested in directing the sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> and approached Spielberg, a friend of his, about the project. While Spielberg wanted to direct the first sequel, he agreed that if there was ever a third film, Johnston could direct.<sup id="cite_ref-making_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-making-5">[5]</a></sup> The second film, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">The Lost World: Jurassic Park</a></i>, included the scene of the <i>Tyrannosaurus</i> rampaging through <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>. Spielberg had initially wanted the scene to be saved for a third film, but later decided to add it into the second film after realizing that he would probably not direct another film in the series.<sup id="cite_ref-time_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time-8">[8]</a></sup> After the release of the second film in May 1997, Spielberg was busy with other projects and was asked about the possibility of a third <i>Jurassic Park</i> film; he responded, "It would give me a tremendous <a href="/wiki/Ibuprofen" title="Ibuprofen">Advil</a> headache just to think about it."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-production">Pre-production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pre-production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Universal Pictures announced the film on June 29, 1998, with Spielberg acting as a producer. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a> was reportedly going to collaborate with Spielberg to create a storyline and write a script. The film was set for release in summer 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Spielberg initially devised a story idea that involved Dr. Alan Grant, who was discovered to have been living on one of <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park#InGen" title="Jurassic Park">InGen</a>'s islands. According to Johnston, "He'd snuck in, after not being allowed in to research the dinosaurs, and was living in a tree like <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe" title="Robinson Crusoe">Robinson Crusoe</a>. But I couldn't imagine this guy wanting to get back on any island that had dinosaurs in it after the first movie."<sup id="cite_ref-Jul01_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jul01-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 1999, Craig Rosenberg began writing the first draft of the script, which involved teenagers who get marooned on Isla Sorna.<sup id="cite_ref-about_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup> Johnston was announced as the film's director in August 1999, with Rosenberg still attached. Production was expected to begin in early 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Rosenberg's draft about teenagers on Isla Sorna was rejected in September 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup> Although Johnston felt that it was "not a badly written script,"<sup id="cite_ref-about_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup> he also said, "It read like a bad episode of <a href="/wiki/Friends" title="Friends">Friends</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup> By December 1999, new writers had been hired to devise a better story for the film.<sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film's second script involved <i><a href="/wiki/Pteranodon" title="Pteranodon">Pteranodon</a></i> escaping from Isla Sorna and causing a spate of mysterious killings on the mainland, which was to be investigated by Alan Grant and a number of other characters including Billy Brennan, a naturalist named Simone, a tough <a href="/wiki/Military_attach%C3%A9" title="Military attaché">military attaché</a>, wealthy Paul Roby, and Roby's teenage son Miles. Grant's group crash-lands on the island, while a parallel investigation is being carried out on the mainland. The aviary sequence and laboratory set piece were initially much longer and more complex, including <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i> stealthily entering the hatchery as the team spends the night there. Sets, costumes, and props were built for this version.<sup id="cite_ref-making_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-making-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-about_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2000, filming was reportedly set to begin in <a href="/wiki/Fiordland,_New_Zealand" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiordland, New Zealand">Fiordland, New Zealand</a> around the end of the month.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Scenes were originally planned to be filmed there for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park">The Lost World: Jurassic Park</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In March 2000, <a href="/wiki/Maui,_Hawaii" class="mw-redirect" title="Maui, Hawaii">Maui, Hawaii</a> was reportedly chosen instead of New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Sam Neill signed on to the project in June 2000, with 18 weeks of filming expected to begin by August 2000, for a release in July 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Macy originally turned down his role due to scheduling conflicts.<sup id="cite_ref-July20_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-July20-20">[20]</a></sup> Trevor Morgan and Téa Leoni were cast in August 2000,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> with Utah's <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_National_Monument" title="Dinosaur National Monument">Dinosaur National Monument</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Oahu" title="Oahu">Oahu</a> military base being considered as possible filming locations.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> During the pre-production phase, concept artists created advertising for the film using a number of working titles including <i>Jurassic Park: Extinction</i> and <i>Jurassic Park: Breakout</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five weeks before filming began,<sup id="cite_ref-making_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-making-5">[5]</a></sup> Johnston and Spielberg rejected the entire script as they were dissatisfied with it; $18 million had already been spent on the film at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Johnston felt that the script's story was too complicated.<sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup> The simpler "rescue mission" plot, which had been suggested by <a href="/wiki/David_Koepp" title="David Koepp">David Koepp</a>, was used for the film instead.<sup id="cite_ref-making_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-making-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup> Alexander Payne and Jim Taylor began rewriting the script in July 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-July20_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-July20-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Johnston said that the script was never finished during production: "We shot pages that eventually went into the final script but we didn't have a document".<sup id="cite_ref-about_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup> Principal photography began on August 30, 2000, at <a href="/wiki/Dillingham_Airfield" title="Dillingham Airfield">Dillingham Airfield</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mokul%C4%93ia,_Hawaii" title="Mokulēia, Hawaii">Mokulēia, Hawaii</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bulletin_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bulletin-26">[26]</a></sup> Macy, commenting on the slow pace of filming the script, said "we would do a quarter-page--some days, an eighth of a page. And that would be a full 12-hour day."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Filming continued on <a href="/wiki/Oahu" title="Oahu">Oahu</a> until September 9.<sup id="cite_ref-advertiser_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advertiser-28">[28]</a></sup> Aerial footage of <a href="/wiki/Molokai" title="Molokai">Molokai</a>'s North Shore cliffs was then shot over the next two days, followed by a week of filming in <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kauai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bulletin_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bulletin-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Filming concluded in Hawaii on September 20, 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-advertiser_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advertiser-28">[28]</a></sup> Production then moved to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_August" title="John August">John August</a> was hired to do uncredited work on the script in September 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Scenes were filmed at <a href="/wiki/Occidental_College" title="Occidental College">Occidental College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> on October 10, 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scenes were filmed at Center Bay Studios in Los Angeles at the end of October.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Other filming locations in California included <a href="/wiki/South_Pasadena,_California" title="South Pasadena, California">South Pasadena</a> and a rock quarry in <a href="/wiki/Irwindale" class="mw-redirect" title="Irwindale">Irwindale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Filming also took place at Universal Studios' backlot in Los Angeles.<sup id="cite_ref-IGN_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGN-31">[31]</a></sup> Production returned to Hawaii in January 2001, to film the movie's ending, which had yet to be written during the previous Hawaiian shoot. The ending was filmed on <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kauai</a>'s Pila'a Beach.<sup id="cite_ref-about_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storyline contains minor scenes from Crichton's <i>Jurassic Park</i> and <i>The Lost World</i> novels that were ultimately not featured in the film versions, such as the <i>Pteranodon</i> aviary and the use of the boat. <a href="/wiki/Laura_Dern" title="Laura Dern">Laura Dern</a>'s cameo was shot in a day.<sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup> In an earlier draft, Neill and Dern's characters were a couple in the process of splitting up.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Johnston said, "I didn't want to see them as a couple anymore. For one thing, I don't think they look like a couple. It would be uncomfortable to still see them together. And Laura Dern doesn't look like she's aged for the past fifteen years!"<sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup> The film's longest rough cut was approximately 96 minutes, without <a href="/wiki/Motion_picture_credits" title="Motion picture credits">credits</a>. According to Johnston, "We lost maybe 8 minutes, so it was never really that long."<sup id="cite_ref-DVD_14-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DVD-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_III_(film_score)" title="Jurassic Park III (film score)">Jurassic Park III (film score)</a></div>
<p>Given <a href="/wiki/John_Williams" title="John Williams">John Williams</a> was busy writing the music for Spielberg's own <i><a href="/wiki/A.I._Artificial_Intelligence" title="A.I. Artificial Intelligence">A.I. Artificial Intelligence</a></i>, he recommended <a href="/wiki/Don_Davis_(composer)" title="Don Davis (composer)">Don Davis</a> to write the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_III_(film_score)" title="Jurassic Park III (film score)"><i>Jurassic Park III</i> score</a>. Williams' original themes were integrated into the score as well as several new ones, such as one for the <i>Spinosaurus</i> that focused on low sounds, with tubas, trombones and timpani. The fight between the <i>Spinosaurus</i> and the <i>Tyrannosaurus</i>, compared by Davis to <a href="/wiki/King_Kong" title="King Kong">King Kong</a> fighting a dinosaur in <a href="/wiki/King_Kong_(1933_film)" title="King Kong (1933 film)">the 1933 film</a>, had a juxtaposition of the <i>Spinosaurus</i> theme with the one Williams wrote for the <i>T. rex</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In addition, "Big Hat, No Cattle", a song by <a href="/wiki/Randy_Newman" title="Randy Newman">Randy Newman</a>, was used in a restaurant scene.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Jurassic Park III</i> premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Amphitheater" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Amphitheater">Universal Amphitheater</a> in Los Angeles, California, on July 16, 2001,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> followed two days later by a release in the United States and other countries. The film earned $181,171,875 in the United States and $368,780,809 worldwide, making it the eighth-highest-grossing film of the year worldwide<sup id="cite_ref-BOM_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-2">[2]</a></sup> but it is the lowest grossing <i>Jurassic Park</i> film in the entire series. The film was released on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> in December 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It was re-released with both sequels in December 2001<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> as the <i>Jurassic Park Trilogy</i>, and as the <i>Jurassic Park Adventure Pack</i> in November 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The film has also been released as a two-disc DVD set alongside <i><a href="/wiki/Hulk_(film)" title="Hulk (film)">Hulk</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2011, the film was released on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" class="mw-redirect" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a> as part of the <i>Jurassic Park Ultimate Trilogy</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_III_(film_score)" title="Jurassic Park III (film score)">soundtrack</a> was released in July 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Critics"></span><span id="Critical_response"></span> <i>Jurassic Park III</i> has received a mostly mixed response from critics and fans alike. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gives the film a 50% rating based on 163 reviews, with an average rating of 5.2/10. The site's consensus states: "<i>Jurassic Park III</i> is darker and faster than its predecessors, but that doesn't quite compensate for the franchise's continuing creative decline."<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> It also has a 42 out of 100 on <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, indicating "mixed or average reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> On both sites, it is the lowest rated film out of the <i>Jurassic Park</i> franchise.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Owen_Gleiberman" title="Owen Gleiberman">Owen Gleiberman</a>, who praised both the previous <i>Jurassic Park</i> films, awarded the third film only a C grade, writing "<i>Jurassic Park III</i> has no pretensions to be anything more than a goose-bumpy fantasy theme-park ride for kids, but it's such a routine ride. Spielberg's wizardry is gone, and his balletic light touch as well, and that gives too much of this 90-minute movie over to the duller-than-dull characters."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Derek Elley of <i>Variety Reviews</i> felt likewise, calling the film "an all-action, helter-skelter, don't-forget-to-buy-the-computer-game ride that makes the two previous installments look like models of classic filmmaking".<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Ben Varkontine of <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> called it "not as good a ride as the first", but "better than the second."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Much of the criticism was leveled at the plot as simply a chase movie with no character development; <i>Apollo Movie Guide</i> panned the film as being "almost the same as the first movie" with "no need for new ideas or even a script".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire</a></i> magazine gave the film 3 stars out of 5, commenting that it was "Short, scrappy and intermittently scary" and that the film ultimately "skews young".<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>On <i><a href="/wiki/Ebert_and_Roeper" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebert and Roeper">Ebert and Roeper</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Roeper" title="Richard Roeper">Richard Roeper</a> gave it a Thumbs Down, while <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> awarded a Thumbs Up.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In a subsequent review, Ebert called it "the best blockbuster of the Summer".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In his written review, Ebert gave the film three stars and wrote that while the film was not as awe-inspiring as the first film or as elaborate as the second, "it's a nice little thrill machine. [...] I can't praise it for its art, but I must not neglect its craft..."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early pioneer of the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur-bird_connection" class="mw-redirect" title="Dinosaur-bird connection">dinosaur-bird connection</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_T._Bakker" title="Robert T. Bakker">Robert T. Bakker</a> has quipped that the <a href="/wiki/Feathery" class="mw-redirect" title="Feathery">feathery</a> quills added to the <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i> for <i>Jurassic Park III</i> "looked like a <a href="/wiki/Roadrunner%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Roadrunner's">roadrunner's</a> <a href="/wiki/Toupee" class="mw-redirect" title="Toupee">toupee</a>." However, he conceded that feathers are difficult subjects for computer animation and speculated that <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_World" title="Jurassic World">Jurassic Park IV</a>'</i>s raptors would have more realistic thorough plumage.<sup id="cite_ref-bakker-hitchcock_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bakker-hitchcock-53">[53]</a></sup> As of 2002, Crichton said he had not seen the film.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_and_merchandise">Marketing and merchandise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Marketing and merchandise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A teaser trailer was released online in September 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Universal avoided excessive early marketing to prevent possible backlash, as it believed that awareness of the film was high enough.<sup id="cite_ref-may25_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-may25-57">[57]</a></sup> Marketing began in April 2001, three months before the film's release.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The first footage from the film was aired during the second-season finale of <i><a href="/wiki/Survivor_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Survivor (U.S. TV series)">Survivor</a></i> in May 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Promotional partners included <a href="/wiki/Kodak" title="Kodak">Kodak</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">The Coca-Cola Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> No fast-food promotions took place in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-may25_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-may25-57">[57]</a></sup> although <a href="/wiki/Kids%27_meal" title="Kids' meal">kids' meal</a> toys based on the film were offered in Canadian <a href="/wiki/Burger_King" title="Burger King">Burger Kings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_video_games#Jurassic_Park_III_.282001.29" class="mw-redirect" title="Jurassic Park video games">Seven video games</a><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> were released to coincide with the film, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Novelization" title="Novelization">novelization</a> by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Ciencin" title="Scott Ciencin">Scott Ciencin</a> that was aimed at young children.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Ciencin also wrote three children's books to tie in with the film's events: <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_Adventures:_Survivor" title="Jurassic Park Adventures: Survivor">Jurassic Park Adventures: Survivor</a></i>, the first book, detailed the eight weeks Eric spent alone on Isla Sorna;<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_Adventures:_Prey" title="Jurassic Park Adventures: Prey">Jurassic Park Adventures: Prey</a></i> had Eric and Alan returning to Isla Sorna to rescue a group of teenage filmmakers;<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_Adventures:_Flyers" title="Jurassic Park Adventures: Flyers">Jurassic Park Adventures: Flyers</a></i> involved Eric and Alan leading the <i>Pteranodon</i> home after they nest in a Universal Studios theme park.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hasbro released a line of 3.75" action figures in the spring of 2001 to coincide with the release, including electronic dinosaurs, humans, and vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-JPT_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JPT-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The figures were scaled down from the original Kenner action figures from the pre-<i>Jurassic Park III</i> toy lines.<sup id="cite_ref-JPT_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JPT-68">[68]</a></sup> A line of toys were also released through the <a href="/wiki/Lego_Studios" title="Lego Studios">Lego Studios</a> brand.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Playskool" title="Playskool">Playskool</a> released a line of toys aimed at younger children,<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> called Jurassic Park Junior.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> A smaller die-cast line of toys was also produced, along with clothes. In November 2001, to promote the film's upcoming home media release, Universal launched a <a href="/wiki/Viral_marketing" title="Viral marketing">viral marketing</a> website for Isla Travel, a fictional Isla Sorna travel agency.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_phone_promotion">Cell phone promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic_Park_III&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cell phone promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the film's home media release, Universal and cell-phone company Hop-On partnered to produce "the world's first-disposable cell phone", available through an in-package offer upon purchase of the film.<sup id="cite_ref-variety_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-variety-75">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The phones were to be delivered for free to customers who responded to a winning promotional card that came with select copies of the film.<sup id="cite_ref-variety_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-variety-75">[75]</a></sup> Approximately 5,000 copies of the film contained a winning promotional card; approximately 1,000 of them were redeemed.<sup id="cite_ref-hmm_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hmm-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the promotion was cancelled when the cell phones could not be finished on time.<sup id="cite_ref-hmm_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hmm-77">[77]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sfc_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-78">[78]</a></sup> An investigation by the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> revealed that sample versions of Hop-On's cell phones were actually modified <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> cell phones, as Hop-On was having problems with its own design.<sup id="cite_ref-sfc_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-78">[78]</a></sup> Customers who were to receive the cell phones instead received a $30 check and a free DVD.<sup id="cite_ref-hmm_77-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hmm-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
