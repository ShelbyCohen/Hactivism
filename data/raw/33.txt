			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Effective altruism</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Effective altruism</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">social movement</a> that applies evidence and reason to determine the most effective ways to benefit others.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Effective altruism encourages individuals to consider all causes and actions and to act in the way that brings about the greatest positive impact, based upon their values.<sup id="cite_ref-vox_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vox-2">[2]</a></sup> It is the broad, evidence-based approach that distinguishes effective altruism from traditional <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">altruism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Charity_(practice)" title="Charity (practice)">charity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bcg_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bcg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>While a substantial proportion of effective altruists have focused on the <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonprofit sector">nonprofit sector</a>, the philosophy of effective altruism applies more broadly to prioritizing the scientific projects, companies, and policy initiatives which can be estimated to save lives, help people, or otherwise have the biggest benefit.<sup id="cite_ref-doing-good-better_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doing-good-better-4">[4]</a></sup> Notable people associated with the movement include philosopher <a href="/wiki/Peter_Singer" title="Peter Singer">Peter Singer</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-blog.ted.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blog.ted.com-5">[5]</a></sup> Facebook cofounder <a href="/wiki/Dustin_Moskovitz" title="Dustin Moskovitz">Dustin Moskovitz</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Washington_Post_on_Dustin_Moskovitz_and_Cari_Tuna_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Washington_Post_on_Dustin_Moskovitz_and_Cari_Tuna-6">[6]</a></sup> Oxford based philosopher <a href="/wiki/William_MacAskill" title="William MacAskill">William MacAskill</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-The_Atlantic_article_The_Greatest_Good_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Atlantic_article_The_Greatest_Good-7">[7]</a></sup> researcher <a href="/wiki/Toby_Ord" title="Toby Ord">Toby Ord</a><sup id="cite_ref-Peter_Singer_The_Most_Good_You_Can_Do_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peter_Singer_The_Most_Good_You_Can_Do-8">[8]</a></sup> and professional poker player <a href="/wiki/Liv_Boeree" title="Liv Boeree">Liv Boeree</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_Singer_MIT_Veritas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Peter_Singer_MIT_Veritas.jpg/220px-Peter_Singer_MIT_Veritas.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Peter_Singer_MIT_Veritas.jpg/330px-Peter_Singer_MIT_Veritas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Peter_Singer_MIT_Veritas.jpg/440px-Peter_Singer_MIT_Veritas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1956" data-file-height="1956" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_Singer_MIT_Veritas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Peter_Singer" title="Peter Singer">Peter Singer</a> is a prominent advocate of effective altruism.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Effective altruism differs from other philanthropic practices because of its emphasis on quantitatively comparing charitable causes and interventions with the goal of maximizing certain moral values. In this way it is similar to <a href="/wiki/Consequentialism" title="Consequentialism">consequentialism</a>, which some leaders of the movement explicitly endorse.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The views of the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Peter_Singer" title="Peter Singer">Peter Singer</a> in particular helped give rise to the effective altruist movement.<sup id="cite_ref-sep-impartiality_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sep-impartiality-12">[12]</a></sup> Singer's book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_You_Can_Save" title="The Life You Can Save">The Life You Can Save</a></i> argued for the basic philosophy of effective giving, claiming that people have a moral imperative to donate more because of the existence of extreme poverty. In the book, Singer argued that people should use <a href="/wiki/Charity_evaluators" class="mw-redirect" title="Charity evaluators">charity evaluators</a> to determine how to make their donations most effective. Singer personally gives a third of his income to charity.<sup id="cite_ref-kristof_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kristof-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause_prioritization">Cause prioritization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cause prioritization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although there is a growing emphasis on effectiveness and evidence among nonprofits, this is usually done with a single cause in mind, such as education or climate change. Effective altruists, however, seek to compare the relative importance of different causes.<sup id="cite_ref-gabriel_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gabriel-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oliver_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oliver-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-practical-ethics_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-practical-ethics-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Effective altruists choose the highest priority causes based on whether activities in each cause area could efficiently advance broad goals, such as increasing human or animal welfare. They then focus their attention on interventions in high priority areas. Several organizations are performing cause prioritization research.<sup id="cite_ref-vox_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vox-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some priorities of effective altruists include poverty in the <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing world</a>, the suffering of animals in <a href="/wiki/Factory_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Factory farming">factory farms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Risks_to_civilization,_humans_and_planet_Earth" class="mw-redirect" title="Risks to civilization, humans and planet Earth">risks to civilization, humans and planet Earth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-doing-good-better_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doing-good-better-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gabriel_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gabriel-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-practical-ethics_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-practical-ethics-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost-effectiveness">Cost-effectiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cost-effectiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Effective altruist organizations claim that some charities are far more effective than others, either because some do not achieve their goals or because of variability in the cost of achieving those goals.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dollar-overseas_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dollar-overseas-20">[20]</a></sup> When possible, they seek to identify charities that are highly <a href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness_analysis" title="Cost-effectiveness analysis">cost-effective</a>, meaning that they achieve a large benefit for a given amount of money.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Atlantic_article_The_Greatest_Good_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Atlantic_article_The_Greatest_Good-7">[7]</a></sup> For example, they select health interventions on the basis of their impact as measured by lives saved per dollar, <a href="/wiki/Quality-adjusted_life_year" title="Quality-adjusted life year">quality-adjusted life years</a> (QALY) saved per dollar, or <a href="/wiki/Disability-adjusted_life_year" title="Disability-adjusted life year">disability-adjusted life years</a> (DALY) averted per dollar. This measure of disease burden is expressed as the number of years lost due to ill-health, disability or early death.</p>
<p>Effective altruism organizations use <a href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" title="Randomized controlled trial">randomized controlled trials</a> as a primary form of evidence,<sup id="cite_ref-The_Atlantic_article_The_Greatest_Good_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Atlantic_article_The_Greatest_Good-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-skelton_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skelton-21">[21]</a></sup> as they are often considered to be at the highest level of strong evidence in healthcare research.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> They also make philanthropic recommendations for charities on the basis of their current funding need rather than merely by evaluating the value of the work itself.<sup id="cite_ref-givewell-interview-huffpo_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-givewell-interview-huffpo-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SSIR-holden_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSIR-holden-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impartiality">Impartiality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impartiality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Effective altruists reject the view that some lives are intrinsically more valuable than others. For example, they believe that a person in a developing country has equal value to a person in one's own community.<sup id="cite_ref-sep-impartiality_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sep-impartiality-12">[12]</a></sup> In the 1972 essay '<a href="/wiki/Famine,_Affluence,_and_Morality" title="Famine, Affluence, and Morality">Famine, Affluence, and Morality</a>', Peter Singer wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It makes no difference whether the person I can help is a neighbor's child ten yards away from me or a Bengali whose name I shall never know, ten thousand miles away. [...] The moral point of view requires us to look beyond the interests of our own society. Previously [...], this may hardly have been feasible, but it is quite feasible now. From the moral point of view, the prevention of the starvation of millions of people outside our society must be considered at least as pressing as the upholding of property norms within our society.<sup id="cite_ref-singer1972_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singer1972-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In addition, many effective altruists think that future generations have equal moral value to currently existing people, so they focus on reducing <a href="/wiki/Risks_to_civilization,_humans,_and_planet_Earth" class="mw-redirect" title="Risks to civilization, humans, and planet Earth">existential risks to humanity</a>. Others believe that the interests of <a href="/wiki/Personhood" title="Personhood">non-human animals</a> should be accorded the same moral weight as similar interests of humans and work to prevent the suffering of animals,<sup id="cite_ref-skelton_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skelton-21">[21]</a></sup> such as those raised in factory farms.<sup id="cite_ref-huffpost-ea-global_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffpost-ea-global-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Counterfactual_reasoning">Counterfactual reasoning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Counterfactual reasoning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Effective altruists argue that <a href="/wiki/Counterfactual_conditional" title="Counterfactual conditional">counterfactual</a> reasoning is important to determine which course of action maximizes positive impact. Many people assume that the best way to help people is through direct methods, such as working for a charity or providing social services,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> but since charities and social-service providers usually can find people willing to work for them, effective altruists compare the amount of good somebody does in a conventional altruistic career to how much good would have been done had the next-best candidate been hired for the position. According to this reasoning, the impact of a career may be smaller than it appears.<sup id="cite_ref-todd-career-paper_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-todd-career-paper-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Donation">Donation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Donation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Effective altruism encourages significant charitable donation. Some believe it is a moral duty to alleviate suffering through donations if the purchases that one forgoes to donate do not cause comparable suffering to oneself,<sup id="cite_ref-singer1972_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singer1972-25">[25]</a></sup> leading some of them to lead a frugal lifestyle in order to give substantially more than is typical in their society.<sup id="cite_ref-kaufman-wise_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kaufman-wise-30">[30]</a></sup> Advocacy focuses on increasing the amount that people donate or identifying nonprofits that best meet the criteria of effective altruism.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Giving_What_We_Can" title="Giving What We Can">Giving What We Can</a> (GWWC) is an organization which hosts a community of individuals who have pledged to donate at least 10% of their income for the remainder of their working lives to the causes that they believe are the most effective. GWWC was founded in November 2009 by <a href="/wiki/Toby_Ord" title="Toby Ord">Toby Ord</a>, a moral philosopher at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford University</a>, who lives on £18,000 ($27,000) per year and donates the remainder of his income to charity.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> As of 2015, more than 1,000 individuals took the pledge.<sup id="cite_ref-ox-gwwc-pledges_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ox-gwwc-pledges-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Founders_Pledge" title="Founders Pledge">Founders Pledge</a> is a similar system run by the nonprofit Founders Forum for Good where startup founders make a legally binding commitment to donate at least 2% of their personal proceeds to charity in the event that they sell their business.<sup id="cite_ref-founders-pledge-80k-interview_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-founders-pledge-80k-interview-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-founders-pledge-forbes_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-founders-pledge-forbes-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-techcrunch_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techcrunch-35">[35]</a></sup> By May 2016, one year after launch, 430 entrepreneurs had pledged, for an estimated total value of $134 million based on the founders' equity and the companies' valuation.<sup id="cite_ref-founders-pledge-anniversary_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-founders-pledge-anniversary-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_selection">Career selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Career selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Effective altruists argue that selection of one's career is an important determinant of the amount of good one does,<sup id="cite_ref-oliver_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oliver-15">[15]</a></sup> both directly (through the services one provides to the world) and indirectly (through the ways one directs the money earned based on the career).<sup id="cite_ref-macaskill-replaceability-paper_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macaskill-replaceability-paper-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/80,000_Hours" title="80,000 Hours">80,000 Hours</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>-based organization in the effective altruism movement that writes articles and conducts one-on-one coaching to help people find careers with a positive social impact.<sup id="cite_ref-yc-techcrunch_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yc-techcrunch-38">[38]</a></sup> It considers indirect methods of altruistic employment, such as earning a high salary in a conventional career and donating a portion of it, as well as direct practices, such as scientific research. It was co-founded by <a href="/wiki/William_MacAskill" title="William MacAskill">William MacAskill</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-fastcoexist_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fastcoexist-39">[39]</a></sup> who is also its current president.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Earning_to_give" title="Earning to give">earning to give</a> strategy has been proposed as a possible strategy for effective altruists. This strategy involves choosing to work in high-paying careers with the explicit goal of donating large sums of money to charity.<sup id="cite_ref-join-ws-save-world_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-join-ws-save-world-41">[41]</a></sup> Benjamin Todd and William MacAskill have argued that the marginal impact of one's potentially unethical actions in such a lucrative career would be small since someone else would have done them regardless, while the impact of donations would be large.<sup id="cite_ref-macaskill-replaceability-paper_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macaskill-replaceability-paper-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>This is a practice which has attracted controversy. <a href="/wiki/David_Brooks_(journalist)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Brooks (journalist)">David Brooks</a>, a columnist for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, criticized effective altruists who adopt the strategy. He wrote that most people who work in finance and other high-paying industries value money for selfish reasons and that being surrounded by these people will cause effective altruists to become less altruistic.<sup id="cite_ref-brooks2013_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brooks2013-42">[42]</a></sup> Some effective altruists acknowledge this possibility and aim to reduce the risk through online communities, public pledges, and donations through <a href="/wiki/Donor-advised_fund" title="Donor-advised fund">donor-advised funds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Week" title="The Week">The Week</a></i>, Pascal-Emmanuel Gobry argued that taking an "unethical" job is fundamentally immoral, no matter the reason.<sup id="cite_ref-gobry_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gobry-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause_priorities">Cause priorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cause priorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Effective altruism is in principle open to helping in whichever areas will do the most good.<sup id="cite_ref-practical-ethics_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-practical-ethics-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kissel_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kissel-45">[45]</a></sup> In practice, people in the effective altruist movement have prioritized the following three focus areas:<sup id="cite_ref-vox_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vox-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-practical-ethics_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-practical-ethics-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-skelton_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skelton-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_poverty_alleviation">Global poverty alleviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Global poverty alleviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Global <a href="/wiki/Poverty_alleviation" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty alleviation">poverty alleviation</a> has been a focus of some of the earliest and most prominent organizations associated with effective altruism.</p>
<p>Charity evaluator <a href="/wiki/GiveWell" title="GiveWell">GiveWell</a> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Holden_Karnofsky" title="Holden Karnofsky">Holden Karnofsky</a> and Elie Hassenfeld in 2007 to address poverty and is currently a part of the effective altruism movement.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-givewell-opt-in_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-givewell-opt-in-47">[47]</a></sup> Givewell has argued that the value of donations is greatest for international poverty alleviation and developing world health issues,<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-givewell_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-givewell-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dollar-overseas_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dollar-overseas-20">[20]</a></sup> and its leading recommendations have been in these domains<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-huffpo-elie_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffpo-elie-50">[50]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Against_Malaria_Foundation" title="Against Malaria Foundation">Against Malaria Foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schistosomiasis_Control_Initiative" title="Schistosomiasis Control Initiative">Schistosomiasis Control Initiative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deworm_the_World_Initiative" title="Deworm the World Initiative">Deworm the World Initiative</a>, and (earlier) <a href="/wiki/VillageReach" title="VillageReach">VillageReach</a> in global health, and <a href="/wiki/GiveDirectly" title="GiveDirectly">GiveDirectly</a> for direct unconditional cash transfers).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Giving_What_We_Can" title="Giving What We Can">Giving What We Can</a> is focused on causes related to the alleviation of global poverty<sup id="cite_ref-wsj2011_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj2011-51">[51]</a></sup> and does in-house research evaluating causes and charities, but largely relies on research by other organizations such as <a href="/wiki/GiveWell" title="GiveWell">GiveWell</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gwwc-sources_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gwwc-sources-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The organization The Life You Can Save, which originated from the <a href="/wiki/The_Life_You_Can_Save" title="The Life You Can Save">book by the same name</a>, also focuses on global poverty.</p>
<p>While much of the initial focus of effective altruism was on direct strategies such as health interventions and cash transfers, there has also been interest in more systematic social, economic, and political reform that would facilitate larger long-term poverty reduction.<sup id="cite_ref-scott-weathers_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scott-weathers-53">[53]</a></sup> In September 2011, GiveWell announced GiveWell Labs,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> which was later renamed as the "<a href="/wiki/Open_Philanthropy_Project" class="mw-redirect" title="Open Philanthropy Project">Open Philanthropy Project</a>", for exploration of more speculative causes such as policy reform. It is a collaboration between GiveWell and <a href="/wiki/Good_Ventures" title="Good Ventures">Good Ventures</a>, a philanthropic foundation founded by <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> co-founder <a href="/wiki/Dustin_Moskovitz" title="Dustin Moskovitz">Dustin Moskovitz</a> and his wife Cari Tuna.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Moses_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moses-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_welfare">Animal welfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Animal welfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many effective altruists believe that reducing <a href="/wiki/Animal_suffering" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal suffering">animal suffering</a> should be a major priority and that, at the current margin, there are cost-effective ways of accomplishing this.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Peter Singer quotes an activist estimate that over a hundred million chickens literally "suffer to death" each year in factory farms and argues that effective animal welfare altruists should prioritize factory farming over more overfunded popular causes such as pet welfare. Singer also argues that, if farms animals such as chickens are assigned even a modicum of consciousness, efforts to reduce factory farming (for example, by reducing global meat consumption) could be an even more underfunded and cost-effective way of reducing current global suffering than human poverty reduction.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESinger2015138.2C_146.E2.80.93147_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESinger2015138.2C_146.E2.80.93147-59">[59]</a></sup> Philosophically, <a href="/wiki/Wild_animal_suffering" title="Wild animal suffering">wild animal suffering</a> may be an additional moral concern for effective altruists.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Animal Charity Evaluators (ACE, formerly called Effective Animal Activism) is an organization connected with the movement that evaluates and compares various animal charities based on their cost-effectiveness and transparency, particularly those that are tackling <a href="/wiki/Factory_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Factory farming">factory farming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESinger2015139_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESinger2015139-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Far_future_and_global_catastrophic_risks">Far future and global catastrophic risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Far future and global catastrophic risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some effective altruists believe that the far future is extremely important. Specifically they believe that the total value of any meaningful metric (wealth, potential for suffering, potential for happiness, etc.) summed up over future generations, far exceeds the value for people living today.<sup id="cite_ref-vox_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vox-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eaf-far-future_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eaf-far-future-63">[63]</a></sup> In particular, the importance of addressing <a href="/wiki/Existential_risk" class="mw-redirect" title="Existential risk">existential risks</a> such as dangers associated with <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Existential_risk_from_advanced_artificial_intelligence" class="mw-redirect" title="Existential risk from advanced artificial intelligence">advanced artificial intelligence</a> is often highlighted and the subject of active research.</p>
<p>Some organizations that work actively on research and advocacy for improving the far future, and have connections with the effective altruist movement, are the <a href="/wiki/Future_of_Humanity_Institute" title="Future of Humanity Institute">Future of Humanity Institute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_the_Study_of_Existential_Risk" title="Centre for the Study of Existential Risk">Centre for the Study of Existential Risk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Future_of_Life_Institute" title="Future of Life Institute">Future of Life Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-guan-op-ed_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guan-op-ed-64">[64]</a></sup> In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Machine_Intelligence_Research_Institute" title="Machine Intelligence Research Institute">Machine Intelligence Research Institute</a> is focused on the more narrow mission of aligning advanced artificial intelligence.<sup id="cite_ref-washpost-ai_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washpost-ai-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-matthews-ea-global_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matthews-ea-global-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_as_a_social_movement">History as a social movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History as a social movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ideas behind effective altruism, such as <a href="/wiki/Consequentialism" title="Consequentialism">consequentialism</a>, have been present in practical ethics for a long time and have been reflected in the writings of philosophers such as Peter Singer<sup id="cite_ref-sep-impartiality_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sep-impartiality-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Unger" title="Peter Unger">Peter Unger</a>. A basic argument for altruism was defined in Singer's 1972 paper "<a href="/wiki/Famine,_Affluence,_and_Morality" title="Famine, Affluence, and Morality">Famine, Affluence, and Morality</a>", in which he argued that people have an obligation to help those in need:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>If it is in our power to prevent something bad from happening, without thereby sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, then we ought, morally, to do it.<sup id="cite_ref-singer1972_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singer1972-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>However, the movement identifying with the name 'effective altruism' itself only came into being in the late 2000s,<sup id="cite_ref-tmgycd_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tmgycd-67">[67]</a></sup> centered around organizations such as Giving What We Can.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/EA_Global" class="mw-redirect" title="EA Global">Effective altruism conferences</a> have been held since 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-huffpost-ea-global_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffpost-ea-global-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> In 2015, Peter Singer published <i><a href="/wiki/The_Most_Good_You_Can_Do" title="The Most Good You Can Do">The Most Good You Can Do</a></i>, a book on effective altruism. The book describes the philosophy and social movement of effective altruism and argues in favor of it.<sup id="cite_ref-kristof_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kristof-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_altruism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>David Brooks has questioned whether children in distant countries should be treated as having equal moral value to nearby children. He claims that morality should be "internally ennobling".<sup id="cite_ref-brooks2013_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brooks2013-42">[42]</a></sup> Pascal-Emmanuel Gobry warns about the "measurement problem", stating in some areas, such as medical research, or helping to reform third-world governance "one grinding step at a time", are hard to measure with controlled cost-effectiveness experiments and risk being undervalued by the effective altruism movement.<sup id="cite_ref-gobry_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gobry-44">[44]</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/Stanford_Social_Innovation_Review" title="Stanford Social Innovation Review">Stanford Social Innovation Review</a></i>, Ken Berger and Robert Penna of <a href="/wiki/Charity_Navigator" title="Charity Navigator">Charity Navigator</a> condemned effective altruism's practice of "weighing causes and beneficiaries against one another", calling this "moralistic, in the worst sense of the word".<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Jacobin_(magazine)" title="Jacobin (magazine)">Jacobin</a></i> magazine, Mathew Snow argues that effective altruism "implores individuals to use their money to procure necessities for those who desperately need them, but says nothing about the system that determines how those necessities are produced and distributed in the first place".<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> However, Joshua Kissel argues that anti-capitalism is compatible with effective altruism in theory, while adding that effective altruists and anti-capitalists have reason to be more sympathetic to each other.<sup id="cite_ref-kissel_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kissel-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
