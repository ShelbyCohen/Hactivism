			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Food Not Bombs</h1>

<p><b>Food Not Bombs</b> is a loose-knit group of independent <a href="/wiki/Collectives" class="mw-redirect" title="Collectives">collectives</a>, serving free <a href="/wiki/Vegan" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegan">vegan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vegetarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegetarian">vegetarian</a> food to others. Food Not Bombs' ideology is that myriad <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> priorities are skewed to allow <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hunger</a> to persist in the midst of abundance. To demonstrate this (and to reduce costs), a large amount of the food served by the group is <a href="/wiki/Food_Salvage" class="mw-redirect" title="Food Salvage">surplus food</a> from grocery stores, bakeries and markets that would otherwise go to waste. This group exhibits a form of <a href="/wiki/Franchise_activism" title="Franchise activism">franchise activism</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Food_not_bombs_2.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Food_not_bombs_2.JPG/170px-Food_not_bombs_2.JPG" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Food_not_bombs_2.JPG/255px-Food_not_bombs_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Food_not_bombs_2.JPG/340px-Food_not_bombs_2.JPG 2x" data-file-width="468" data-file-height="701" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Food_not_bombs_2.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The group serves free meals.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Food Not Bombs is an all-volunteer global movement that shares free <a href="/wiki/Vegan" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegan">vegan</a> meals as a protest to <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>. Each chapter collects surplus food that would otherwise go to waste from grocery stores, bakeries and markets, as well as donations from local farmers, then prepares community meals which are served for free to anyone who is hungry. The central beliefs of the group are:<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Always <a href="/wiki/Vegan" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegan">vegan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vegetarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegetarian">vegetarian</a> and free to everyone.</li>
<li>Each chapter is independent and autonomous and makes decisions using the <a href="/wiki/Consensus" class="mw-redirect" title="Consensus">consensus</a> process.</li>
<li>Food Not Bombs is dedicated to nonviolence.</li>
</ul>
<p>Food Not Bombs works to call attention to <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homelessness" title="Homelessness">homelessness</a> in society by sharing food in public places and facilitating community gatherings of hungry people.</p>
<p>Anyone who wants to cook may cook, and anyone who wants to eat may eat. Food Not Bombs strives to include everyone.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Food Not Bombs was founded in 1980 in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>, Massachusetts by <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement" title="Anti-nuclear movement">anti-nuclear activists</a> Keith McHenry,<sup id="cite_ref-KeithBio_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KeithBio-3">[3]</a></sup> Jo Swanson, Mira Brown, Susan Eaton, Brian Feigenbaum, C.T. Lawrence Butler,<sup id="cite_ref-CTBio_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTBio-4">[4]</a></sup> Jessie Constable and Amy Rothstien. Co-founder, Keith McHenry has volunteered for 35 years and can be found sharing food almost every week in various cities including some Santa Cruz, California and Taos, New Mexico. The members' activities included providing food and marching and protesting. Their protests were against such things as nuclear power, United States' involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Salvadoran_Civil_War" title="Salvadoran Civil War">Salvadoran Civil War</a>, and discrimination against the homeless.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first arrests for sharing free food were on August 15, 1988 at the entrance to Golden Gate Park in San Francisco, California. Nine people were arrested that day, including McHenry. The city went on to make over 1,000 arrests, and Amnesty International declared these volunteers 'prisoners of conscience'.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s:_Further_development">1990s: Further development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1990s: Further development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Food Not Bombs grew throughout the 1990s, and held four international gatherings: in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> in 1992 and 1995, in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> in 1996, and in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> in 2005. The 1995 International Food Not Bombs Gathering took place in and around <a href="/wiki/Civic_Center,_San_Francisco,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Civic Center, San Francisco, California">United Nations Plaza</a> in San Francisco at the same time the world was celebrating the 50th anniversary of the founding of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> (at a historic conference in San Francisco).</p>
<p>Chapters of Food Not Bombs were involved in the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-globalization">Anti-Globalization Movement</a> in the late 1990s, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific_Economic_Cooperation" title="Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation">APEC</a> resistance in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> in 1997; the June 18, 1999, International Carnival Against Capitalism; and the so-called <a href="/wiki/WTO_Ministerial_Conference_of_1999_protest_activity" class="mw-redirect" title="WTO Ministerial Conference of 1999 protest activity">“Battle of Seattle”</a> later that year, which shut down the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> meetings. Food Not Bombs helped start the Low Watt FM Free Radio, the October 22nd No Police Brutality Day, and <a href="/wiki/Homes_Not_Jails" title="Homes Not Jails">Homes Not Jails</a> during the San Francisco days.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s:_Anti-war_activism">2000s: Anti-war activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000s: Anti-war activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Food Not Bombs supported the actions against the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a> by providing meals at protests all over the world. During a presentation to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a> in 2006, an <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a> <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">counter-terrorism</a> official labeled Food Not Bombs and <a href="/wiki/Independent_Media_Center" title="Independent Media Center">Indymedia</a> as having possible terrorist connections.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the summer of 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale</a> Food Not Bombs chapter began to receive systematic harassment by local law enforcement until an ultimatum made by the Fort Lauderdale Police for the arrest of those conducting the feedings was met with 100s of supporters the next week and subsequent relenting by local law enforcement until the 2010s.<sup id="cite_ref-BrowardPalmBeach.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrowardPalmBeach.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando</a> enacted an ordinance prohibiting serving food to more than a certain number of people without a permit.<sup id="cite_ref-SchluebSentinel_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchluebSentinel-10">[10]</a></sup> In the fall of 2007, Eric Montanez of Orlando's Food Not Bombs was charged with violating a city ordinance by feeding more people in a public park at one time than the law allows without a permit. On October 10, 2007, Montanez was acquitted by a jury.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Food Not Bombs and a church for the homeless called First Vagabonds Church of God sued the city<sup id="cite_ref-SchluebSentinel_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchluebSentinel-10">[10]</a></sup> on the grounds that serving food is first amendment-protected political speech and religious activity. The groups won and the city ordinance was overturned; Orlando appealed to the 11th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals and won.<sup id="cite_ref-SchluebSentinel_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchluebSentinel-10">[10]</a></sup> On August 31, 2010, the 11th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals threw out the decision, barring Orlando from enforcing the ordinance until another hearing before a 10-judge panel takes place.<sup id="cite_ref-SchluebSentinel_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchluebSentinel-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2008, local business owners attempted to stop the <a href="/wiki/Kitchener,_Ontario" title="Kitchener, Ontario">Kitchener, Ontario</a>, Food Not Bombs from serving in a highly visible downtown location,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (January 2014)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> describing the group as supporting meat-free diets, anti-capitalism, and an end to Canada's military intervention in Afghanistan.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (January 2014)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In April 2009, the city of <a href="/wiki/Middletown,_Connecticut" title="Middletown, Connecticut">Middletown, Connecticut</a>, issued a cease-and-desist order to the local chapter of Food Not Bombs. Prior to the order, the City Health Inspector had cited the organization for distributing food without a license. In August 2009 the chapter began operating out of a licensed kitchen provided by the Middletown First Church of Christ Congregational as state hearings into the matter were held.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>As of October 2011, there were more than 400 chapters of Food Not Bombs listed on the organization's website,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> with about half the chapters located outside the United States. Food Not Bombs has a loose structure: every chapter of Food Not Bombs embraces a few basic principles, and carries out the same sort of action, but every chapter is free to make its own decisions, based on the needs of its community. Likewise, every chapter of Food Not Bombs operates on <a href="/wiki/Consensus_decision-making" title="Consensus decision-making">consensus</a>. Besides collecting and distributing food for free, many chapters of Food Not Bombs are involved in community <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">anti-poverty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-war" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-war">anti-war</a>, and pro-immigrant organizing, as well as other political causes related to social justice.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance_to_restrictions_on_food_sharing">Resistance to restrictions on food sharing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Resistance to restrictions on food sharing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most widely publicized restrictions on food sharing involving Food Not Bombs were the 2011 feeding bans in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. Similar laws have been enacted in other jurisdictions, including Philadelphia<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and Houston.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_Florida_feeding_bans">2011 Florida feeding bans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2011 Florida feeding bans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On April 20, 2011, an en banc panel of the 11th Circuit Court of Appeals upheld the Orlando ordinance as a valid "time, place and manner" regulation,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> reversing the initial ruling of First Vagabonds Church of God, An Unincorporated Association, Brian Nichols v. City of Orlando, Florida and removing the permanent injunction against the Orlando ordinance that was first attempted in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 18<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the 30-day stay ended and the ordinance would soon be enforced on June 1 resulting in the arrest of co-founder <a href="/wiki/Keith_McHenry" title="Keith McHenry">Keith McHenry</a> and Orlando FNB volunteer Ben Markeson. Each successive sharing saw arrests, with four arrests on June 6, five on June 8, three on June 13, and six on June 21. That same week the lawyer for Orlando FNB issued a cease and desist to the city,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> saying that violating the ordinance was not an arrestable offense, and hackers claiming to be affiliated with <a href="/wiki/Anonymous_(group)" title="Anonymous (group)">Anonymous</a> began issuing threats to the city of Orlando. Orlando Mayor Buddy Dyer has also received heavy criticism for referring to Food Not Bombs activists as "food terrorists."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Monday, June 20, no arrests were made at Food Not Bombs' breakfast in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eola_Park" title="Lake Eola Park">Lake Eola Park</a>, however Ben Markeson was cited for holding a sign without a permit, with much confusion among city officials about procedure and the violations of civil rights. The city later issued a statement reversing their interpretation of the sign regulations. On the same day hackers carried through with their threats and took down the Orlando Chamber of Commerce site and a Universal Studios website in "Operation Orlando," issuing a video statement later declaring a 48-hour cease fire on the condition that the city arrest no one for feeding the homeless, presumably on June 22.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 22 more arrests took place including a second arrest of McHenry, resulting in a 17-day stay in jail. "Operation Orlando" soon went into full attack resulting in many websites going down in subsequent days. Orlando and Dyer were soon inundated with national and international attention and outcry. On July 1, OFNB took the Mayor up on his offer to move sharings to City Hall, which stopped arrests and resulted in a new, stable arrangement for OFNB.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Homeless hacktivist Christopher Doyon, also known as "Commander X", was eventually arrested for "Operation Orlando" and other activity. Soon after his arraignment he held a press statement where he admitted to all charges, but argued that the <a href="/wiki/Distributed_denial_of_service_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Distributed denial of service attack">distributed denial of service</a> attacks constituted acts of cyber-<a href="/wiki/Civil_disobedience" title="Civil disobedience">civil disobedience</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale</a> has been pondering a feeding ban for some time. In 2011 FNB activists complained about unjust surveillance and arrest and claimed to be victims of an unwarranted police raid due to their home having their electricity cut off; they were harassed by police asking if they were "terrorists." Activists have also been arrested while playing a game of capture the flag.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinellas County is not only trying to ban feeding but is also banning sleeping in public. This means that homeless people in the St Pete area must either move into the "Safe Harbor" homeless facility or get out of the town.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>An ordinance in Sarasota currently requires gatherings of 75 or more people to obtain a special event permit. Local residents are currently petitioning to lower that number to 12, as well as to require feeders to obtain the same permit necessary for people who sell goods in public places (a $150 fee). There have been numerous other ordinances in recent months targeting the homeless, including the banning of smoking and removing park benches.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Since 2009, homeless shelters in Gainesville could feed only 130 people at a time, leading to the formation of the Coalition To End The Meal Limit.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Two years later, due to pressure from the local Democratic Party, the meal limit and other rules were significantly changed, resulting in a victory for the Coalition to End The Meal Limit.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 19, 2011, Orlando Mayor Buddy Dyer held a press conference to announce that charges against food sharers arrested in Lake Eola Park, Orlando, were dropped, resulting in a new state of compromise between Buddy Dyer's administration and Orlando Food Not Bombs.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_Fort_Lauderdale_sharing_ban">2014 Fort Lauderdale sharing ban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2014 Fort Lauderdale sharing ban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In November 2014, Fort Lauderdale finally enacted a sharing ban, drawing a similar flurry of media attention as in Orlando.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Several Food Not Bombs activists were arrested sharing food and other acts of civil disobedience, for which they received "Civil Liberties Arrest" medals from the Broward County ACLU.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Other FNB activists went on hunger strike against enforcement of the law.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A court injunction stopped enforcement of the sharing ban in early December 2014 pending several court cases. In late December the injunction was extended until February.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> On January 29, 2015, Food Not Bombs filed a 29-page federal lawsuit against the City of Fort Lauderdale to strike down the sharing ban ordinances as unconstitutional.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FNB.27s_involvement_in_the_Occupy_Wall_Street_movement">FNB's involvement in the Occupy Wall Street movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: FNB's involvement in the Occupy Wall Street movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Food Not Bombs groups have been heavily involved in supporting occupation camps across the US during the <a href="/wiki/Occupy_Wall_Street" title="Occupy Wall Street">Occupy Wall Street</a> movement. The use of consensus, supporting urban homeless communities, and mass feedings through donations are all specialties of Food Not Bombs that has now seen an unheralded demand.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a case of history repeating itself, a Food Not Bombs kitchen was removed in a late night police confrontation with <a href="/wiki/Occupy_San_Francisco" title="Occupy San Francisco">Occupy San Francisco</a> in mid-October.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Co-founder <a href="/wiki/C.T._Lawrence_Butler" class="mw-redirect" title="C.T. Lawrence Butler">C.T. Lawrence Butler</a> was recently inspired to come back to the Boston activism scene to join Occupy Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Co-founder Keith McHenry, who spent much of the year encouraging the advent of American occupation camps during his touring, has been an enthusiastic participant in many camps<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> even as he has released a new Food Not Bombs handbook.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>A Food Not Bombs World Gathering took place August 20–26, 2012, in Tampa, Florida - the week before the Republican National Convention.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In conjunction with Occupy Tampa and many other organizations, Food Not Bombs activists collected and prepared food for hundreds of RNC protesters and offered workshops, cultural events, and protest activities from August 20–30.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupy_Sandy">Occupy Sandy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Occupy Sandy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Near the end of 2012, Food Not Bombs activists, in particular Long Island Food Not Bombs, fed countless thousands of people in the wake of Superstorm Sandy alongside "Occupy Sandy." <sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The outpouring of food going to waste and support for disaster stricken, impoverished communities culminated in the "Largest Food Not Bombs Ever" at the "Hempstead Food Share Bonanza" on Nov. 18th.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Food_Not_Bombs_Free_Skool">The Food Not Bombs Free Skool</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_Not_Bombs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The Food Not Bombs Free Skool">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Keith McHenry and other long-time Food Not Bombs activists announced in 2012 the opening of the FNB Free Skool in <a href="/wiki/Taos,_New_Mexico" title="Taos, New Mexico">Taos, New Mexico</a>, part of the United States of America. The first year of classes started in summer of 2013. Topics covered by the course are analysis of current social issues, community organizing, nonviolent social change, cultural events which support social change and sustainable future for communities.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
