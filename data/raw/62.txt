            <h1>Gay rights movement</h1><div class="topic-identifier subhead">political and social movement</div>

	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom firstpar"><strong><span class="srTitle">Gay rights movement</span></strong><strong>, </strong>also called <span class="alternate">homosexual rights movement</span> or <span class="alternate">gay liberation movement</span>,  <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/civil-rights"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/119317/civil-rights">civil rights</span></a> movement that advocates <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/equality-human-rights"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/275667/equality">equal rights</span></a> for gay men, lesbians, bisexuals, and transsexuals; seeks to eliminate <a name="ref750908" id="ref750908" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/sodomy-law"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/552330/sodomy-law">sodomy laws</span></a> barring homosexual acts between consenting adults; and calls for an end to <a name="ref750909" id="ref750909" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/discrimination-society"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/165494/discrimination">discrimination</span></a> against gay men and lesbians in employment, credit lending, housing, public accommodations, and other areas of life.</p><!--[END-1ST-PARA]--><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="126350" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/30/130030-004-86EF2695.jpg" alt="Several hundred thousand people celebrating Gay Pride, Rio de Janeiro, 2007. " data-width="413" data-height="600"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Several hundred thousand people celebrating Gay Pride, Rio de Janeiro, 2007.</div><div class="credit"><em>Luisa De Paola—AFP/Getty Images</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li></ul></div></section><section class="gutter-bottom" data-level="1" id="toc281888"><h2 class="h2">Gay rights prior to the 20th century</h2><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Religious <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/admonitions" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="admonitions">admonitions</a> against sexual relations between same-sex individuals (particularly men) long stigmatized such behaviour, but most legal codes in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/history-of-Europe"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/195896/history-of-Europe">Europe</span></a> were silent on the subject of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/homosexuality"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/270637/homosexuality">homosexuality</span></a>. The judicial systems of many predominantly Muslim countries <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/invoked" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="invoked">invoked</a> Islamic law (<a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Shariah"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/538793/Shariah">Sharīʿah</span></a>) in a wide range of <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contexts" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="contexts">contexts</a>, and many sexual or quasi-sexual acts including same-sex intimacy were criminalized in those countries with severe penalties, including execution.</p><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Beginning in the 16th century, lawmakers in Britain began to categorize homosexual behaviour as criminal rather than simply immoral. In the 1530s, during the reign of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Henry-VIII-king-of-England"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/261947/Henry-VIII">Henry VIII</span></a>, <a name="ref1077996" id="ref1077996" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/England"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/700965/England">England</span></a> passed the <a name="ref1077994" id="ref1077994" class="idxref"></a>Buggery Act, which made sexual relations between men a criminal offense punishable by death. In Britain <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/sodomy"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/552329/sodomy">sodomy</span></a> remained a capital offense punishable by hanging until 1861. Two decades later, in 1885, Parliament passed an <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/amendment" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="amendment">amendment</a> sponsored by <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Henry-Du-Pre-Labouchere"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/326785/Henry-Du-Pre-Labouchere">Henry Du Pré Labouchere</span></a>, which created the offense of “gross indecency” for same-sex male sexual relations, enabling any form of sexual behaviour between men to be prosecuted (lesbian sexual relations—because they were unimaginable by male legislators—were not subject to the law). Likewise, in <a name="ref1077995" id="ref1077995" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Germany"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/231186/Germany">Germany</span></a> in the early 1870s, when the country was <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrating" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="integrating">integrating</a> the civil codes of various <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disparate" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="disparate">disparate</a> kingdoms, the final German penal code included <a name="ref1077998" id="ref1077998" class="idxref"></a>Paragraph 175, which criminalized same-sex male relations with punishment including prison and a loss of civil rights.</p></section><section class="gutter-bottom" data-level="1" id="toc281889"><h2 class="h2">The beginning of the gay rights movement</h2><div class="similar-topics md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300">

	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Before the end of the 19th century there were scarcely any “movements” for gay rights. Indeed, in his 1890s poem “<q>Two Loves,</q>” <a name="ref1077997" id="ref1077997" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Lord-Alfred-Douglas"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/170140/Lord-Alfred-Douglas">Lord Alfred (“Bosie”) Douglas</span></a>, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Oscar-Wilde"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/643631/Oscar-Wilde">Oscar Wilde</span></a>’s lover, declared “I [homosexuality] am the love that dare not speak its name.” Homosexual men and women were given voice in 1897 with the founding of the <a name="ref239592" id="ref239592" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Scientific-Humanitarian-Committee"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/528924/Scientific-Humanitarian-Committee">Scientific-Humanitarian Committee</span></a> (Wissenschaftlich-humanitäres Komitee; WhK) in <a name="ref1077999" id="ref1077999" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Berlin"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/62055/Berlin">Berlin</span></a>. Their first activity was a petition to call for the repeal of Paragraph 175 of the Imperial Penal Code (submitted 1898, 1922, and 1925). The committee published emancipation literature, sponsored rallies, and campaigned for legal reform throughout Germany, as well as in The Netherlands and Austria, developing some 25 local chapters by 1922. Its founder was <a name="ref239593" id="ref239593" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Magnus-Hirschfeld"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/266859/Magnus-Hirschfeld">Magnus Hirschfeld</span></a>, who in 1919 opened the Institute for Sexual Science (Institut für Sexualwissenschaft), which anticipated by decades other scientific centres (such as the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Kinsey-Institute-for-Research-in-Sex-Gender-and-Reproduction"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/537030/Kinsey-Institute-for-Research-in-Sex-Gender-and-Reproduction">Kinsey Institute for Research in Sex, Gender, and Reproduction,</span></a> in the <a name="ref1078001" id="ref1078001" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/United-States"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/616563/United-States">United States</span></a>) that specialized in sex research. He also helped sponsor the <a name="ref239594" id="ref239594" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/World-League-for-Sexual-Reform"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/648557/World-League-for-Sexual-Reform">World League of Sexual Reform</span></a>, which was established in 1928 at a conference in Copenhagen. Despite Paragraph 175 and the failure of the WhK to win its repeal, homosexual men and women experienced a certain amount of freedom in Germany, particularly during the Weimar period, between the end of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/World-War-I"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/648646/World-War-I">World War I</span></a> and the Nazi seizure of power. In many larger German cities, gay nightlife became tolerated, and the number of gay publications increased; indeed, according to some historians, the number of gay bars and periodicals in Berlin in the 1920s exceeded that in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/New-York-City"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/412352/New-York-City">New York City</span></a> six decades later. <a name="ref1078004" id="ref1078004" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Adolf-Hitler"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/267992/Adolf-Hitler">Adolf Hitler</span></a>’s seizure of power ended this relatively liberal period. He ordered the reinvigorated enforcement of Paragraph 175, and on May 6, 1933, German student athletes raided and ransacked Hirschfeld’s archives and burned the institute’s materials in a public square.</p><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Outside Germany, other organizations were also created. For example, in 1914 the <a name="ref239595" id="ref239595" class="idxref"></a>British Society for the Study of Sex Psychology was founded by <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Edward-Carpenter"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/96730/Edward-Carpenter">Edward Carpenter</span></a> and <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Havelock-Ellis"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/185133/Havelock-Ellis">Havelock Ellis</span></a> for both promotional and educational purposes, and in the United States in 1924 Henry Gerber, an immigrant from Germany, founded the Society for Human Rights, which was chartered by the state of Illinois.</p><div class="md-sidebar-news-features md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300">

	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Despite the formation of such groups, political activity by homosexuals was generally not very visible. Indeed, gays were often harassed by the police wherever they congregated. <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/World-War-II"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/648813/World-War-II">World War II</span></a> and its aftermath began to change that. The war brought many young people to cities and brought visibility to the gay <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="community">community</a>. In the United States this greater visibility brought some backlash, particularly from the government and police; civil servants were often fired, the military attempted to purge its ranks of gay soldiers (a policy enacted during World War II), and police vice squads frequently raided gay bars and arrested their <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/clientele" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="clientele">clientele</a>. However, there was greater political activity as well, aimed in large measure at decriminalizing sodomy.</p></section><section class="gutter-bottom" data-level="1" id="toc281890"><h2 class="h2">The gay rights movement since the mid-20th century</h2><a href="/quiz/hip-hop-quiz" class="test-your-knowledge md-content-module-left grid-md-shrink visible-lg visible-md">
		<div class="subhead">Test Your Knowledge</div>
		<div class="image-wrapper">
			












	
	
		<img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/13/175913-118-9510C3E3.jpg" alt="Art. Graffiti. Spray paint. New York City. Graffiti and trash in Manhattan, 1986." />
	
<div class="title">Hip-Hop Quiz</div>
		</div>
	</a><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Beginning in the mid-20th century, an increasing number of organizations were formed. The Cultuur en Ontspannings Centrum (“<a name="ref239596" id="ref239596" class="idxref"></a><a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Culture" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="Culture">Culture</a> and Recreation Centre”), or COC, was founded in 1946 in Amsterdam. In the United States the first major male organization, founded in 1950–51 by Harry Hay in Los Angeles, was the <a name="ref239597" id="ref239597" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Mattachine-Society"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/369619/Mattachine-Society">Mattachine Society</span></a> (its name reputedly derived from a <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="medieval">medieval</a> French society of masked players, the Société Mattachine, to represent the public “masking” of homosexuality), while the <a name="ref239598" id="ref239598" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Daughters-of-Bilitis"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/152374/Daughters-of-Bilitis-DOB">Daughters of Bilitis</span></a> (named after the Sapphic love poems of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Pierre-Louys"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/349430/Pierre-Louys">Pierre Louÿs</span></a>, <em>Chansons de Bilitis</em>), founded in 1955 by Phyllis Lyon and <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Del-Martin"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1473046/Del-Martin">Del Martin</span></a> in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/San-Francisco-California"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/521129/San-Francisco">San Francisco</span></a>, was a leading group for women. In addition, the United States saw the publication of a national gay periodical, <i>One</i>, which in 1958 won a U.S. Supreme Court ruling that enabled it to mail the magazine through the postal service. In Britain a commission chaired by Sir John Wolfenden issued a groundbreaking report (<em>see</em> <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Wolfenden-Report"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/646566/Wolfenden-Report">Wolfenden Report</span></a>) in 1957, which recommended that private homosexual <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/liaisons" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="liaisons">liaisons</a> between consenting adults be removed from the domain of criminal law; a decade later the recommendation was <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implemented" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="implemented">implemented</a> by Parliament in the Sexual Offences Act, effectively decriminalizing homosexual relations for men age 21 or older (further legislation lowered the age of consent first to 18 [1994] and then to 16 [2001], the latter of which equalized the age of sexual consent for same-sex and opposite-sex partners).</p><div class="quiz-list-module md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300">

</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">The gay rights movement was beginning to win victories for legal reform, particularly in western Europe, but perhaps the single defining event of gay activism occurred in the United States. In the early morning hours of June 28, 1969, the <a name="ref239599" id="ref239599" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Stonewall-riots"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1386501/Stonewall-riots">Stonewall Inn</span></a>, a gay bar in New York City’s <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Greenwich-Village"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/245431/Greenwich-Village">Greenwich Village</span></a>, was raided by the police. Nearly 400 people joined a <a name="ref963313" id="ref963313" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Stonewall-riots"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1386501/Stonewall-riots">riot</span></a> that lasted 45 minutes and resumed on succeeding nights. “Stonewall” came to be <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/commemorated" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="commemorated">commemorated</a> annually in June with <a name="ref239601" id="ref239601" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Gay-Pride"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/227405/Gay-Pride">Gay Pride</span></a> celebrations, not only in U.S. cities but also in several other countries (Gay Pride is also held at other times of the year in some countries).</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="126352" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/57/129957-004-7DA86A6F.jpg" alt="The Stonewall Inn in New York City’s Greenwich Village days after the Stonewall riots, which began on June 28, 1969. " data-width="550" data-height="366"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">A large crowd gathering along the Amsterdam canals to celebrate Gay Pride, Aug. 2, 2008.</div><div class="credit"><em>© PHOTOGRAPHER/Shutterstock.com</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li><li><figure><span data-id="126628" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/14/130114-004-7D369D88.jpg" alt="Participants celebrating at GayFest in Bucharest, Romania, May 23, 2009. Groups such as the Human Rights Campaign, the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/National-LGBTQ-Task-Force"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/2057096/National-LGBTQ-Task-Force">National Gay and Lesbian Task Force</span></a>, and <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/ACT-UP"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/766488/ACT-UP">ACT UP</span></a> (AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power) in the United States and Stonewall and Outrage! in the United Kingdom—and dozens and dozens of similar organizations in Europe and elsewhere—began agitating for legal and social reforms. In addition, the transnational <a name="ref750912" id="ref750912" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/International-Lesbian-Gay-Bisexual-Trans-and-Intersex-Association"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/766396/International-Lesbian-Gay-Bisexual-Trans-and-Intersex-Association-ILGA">International Lesbian and Gay Association</span></a> was founded in Coventry, England, in 1978. Now headquartered in Brussels, it plays a significant role in coordinating international efforts to promote <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/human-rights"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/275840/human-rights">human rights</span></a> and fight <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/discrimination-psychology"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/165484/discrimination">discrimination</span></a> against lesbian, gay, bisexual, and <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/transgender"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/2033198/transgender">transgender</span></a> persons.</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="126354" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/47/129947-004-D4574394.jpg" alt="Gay rights demonstration at the Democratic National Convention, New York City, July 1976." data-width="550" data-height="361"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Gay rights demonstration at the Democratic National Convention, New York City, July 1976.</div><div class="credit"><em>Warren K. </div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">In the United States, gay activists won support from the Democratic Party in 1980, when the party added to its platform nondiscrimination clause a plank including <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/sexuality"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/537209/sexuality">sexual orientation</span></a>. This support, along with campaigns by gay activists urging gay men and women to “come out of the closet” (indeed, in the late 1980s, National Coming Out Day was established and is now celebrated on October 11 in most countries), encouraged gay men and women to enter the political arena as candidates. The first openly gay government officials in the United States were Jerry DeGrieck and Nancy Wechsler, in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Ann-Arbor"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/26041/Ann-Arbor">Ann Arbor</span></a>, Michigan. DeGrieck and Wechsler both were elected in 1972 and came out while serving on the city council; Wechsler was replaced on the council by Kathy Kozachenko, who ran openly as a lesbian, in 1974—thus becoming the first openly gay person to win office after first coming out. In 1977 American gay rights activist <a name="ref1078008" id="ref1078008" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Harvey-Milk"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/895071/Harvey-Milk">Harvey Milk</span></a> was elected to the San Francisco Board of Supervisors; Milk was assassinated the following year. In 1983 Gerry Studds, a sitting representative from Massachusetts, became the first member of the United States Congress to announce his homosexuality. <a name="ref1078009" id="ref1078009" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Barney-Frank"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1675962/Barney-Frank">Barney Frank</span></a>, also a member of the U.S. House of Representatives from Massachusetts, also came out while serving in Congress in the 1980s; Frank was a powerful member of that body and within the Democratic Party into the 21st century. <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Tammy-Baldwin"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/2029693/Tammy-Baldwin">Tammy Baldwin</span></a>, from Wisconsin, became the first openly gay politician to be elected to both the U.S. House of Representatives (1998) and the U.S. Senate (2012). In 2009 <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Annise-Parker"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1660429/Annise-Parker">Annise Parker</span></a> was elected mayor of Houston, America’s fourth largest city, making it the largest U.S. city to elect an openly gay politician as mayor.</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="110671" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/25/114925-004-617EA44F.jpg" alt="Harvey Milk posing in front of his camera shop in San Francisco, 1977." data-width="501" data-height="450"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Harvey Milk posing in front of his camera shop in San Francisco, 1977.</div><div class="credit"><em>AP</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li></ul></div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Outside the United States, openly gay politicians also scored successes. In Canada in 1998 Glen Murray became the mayor of Winnipeg, Manitoba—the first openly gay politician to lead a large city. Large cities in Europe also were fertile grounds for success for openly gay politicians—for example, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Bertrand-Delanoe"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/760474/Bertrand-Delanoe">Bertrand Delanoë</span></a> in Paris and Klaus Wowereit in Berlin, both elected mayor in 2001. At the local and national levels, the number of openly gay politicians increased dramatically during the 1990s and 2000s, and in 2009 <a name="ref1078010" id="ref1078010" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Johanna-Sigurdardottir"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1511694/Johanna-Sigurdardottir">Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir</span></a> became <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/prime-minister"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/476331/prime-minister">prime minister</span></a> of Iceland—the world’s first openly gay head of government. She was followed by Elio Di Rupo, who became prime minister of Belgium in 2011. In Africa, Asia, and <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Latin-America"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/331694/history-of-Latin-America">Latin America</span></a>, openly gay politicians have had only limited success in winning office; notable elections to national legislatures include Patria Jiménez Flores in Mexico (1997), Mike Waters in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/South-Africa"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/555568/South-Africa">South Africa</span></a> (1999), and Clodovil Hernandez in Brazil (2006).</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="123653" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/72/128472-004-53C0A8FD.jpg" alt="Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir, a member of the Social Democratic Alliance, became Iceland’s first female prime minister in 2009." data-width="299" data-height="450"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir, 2009.</div><div class="credit"><em>Icelandic Ministry of Social Affairs and Social Security</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li></ul></div><div class="trending-topics md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300">

	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">The issues that gay rights groups emphasized have varied since the 1970s by time and place, with different national organizations promoting policies specifically tailored to their country’s <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/milieu" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="milieu">milieu</a>. For example, whereas in some countries, particularly in Scandinavia, antisodomy statutes never existed or were struck down relatively early, in other countries the situation was more complex. In the United States, with its strong federal tradition, the battle for the repeal of sodomy laws initially was fought at the state level. In 1986 the U.S. Supreme Court upheld Georgia’s antisodomy law in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Bowers-v-Hardwick"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/675313/Bowers-v-Hardwick"><em>Bowers</em> v. <em>Hardwick</em></span></a>; 17 years later, however, in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Lawrence-v-Texas"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/909944/Lawrence-v-Texas"><em>Lawrence</em> v. <em>Texas</em></span></a>, the Supreme Court reversed itself, effectively overturning the antisodomy law in Texas and in 12 other states.</p><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Other issues of primary importance for the gay rights movement since the 1970s include combating the HIV/<a class="md-crosslink" href="/science/AIDS"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/10414/AIDS">AIDS</span></a> <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/epidemic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="epidemic">epidemic</a> and promoting disease prevention and funding for research; <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/lobbying"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/345407/lobbying">lobbying</span></a> government for nondiscriminatory policies in employment, housing, and other aspects of civil society; ending bans on military service for gay individuals; expanding <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/hate-crime"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/914040/hate-crime">hate crimes</span></a> legislation to include protection for gay, lesbian, and transgender individuals; and securing marriage rights for gay and lesbian couples (<em>see</em> <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/same-sex-marriage"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/753687/same-sex-marriage">same-sex marriage</span></a>).</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="95035" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/57/93057-004-D570C036.jpg" alt="Same-sex couple exchanging rings during their civil marriage ceremony.
