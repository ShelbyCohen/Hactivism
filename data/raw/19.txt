<p>The <b>Shepherding Movement</b> (sometimes called the "Discipleship Movement") was an influential and controversial movement within some British, Australian and American <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Charismatic movement">charismatic churches</a>. It emerged in the 1970s and early 1980s. The doctrine of the movement emphasized the "one another" passages of the New Testament, and the mentoring relationship described in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Epistle_to_Timothy" title="Second Epistle to Timothy">Second Epistle to Timothy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherding_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It began when four well-known Charismatic teachers, Bob Mumford, <a href="/wiki/Derek_Prince" title="Derek Prince">Derek Prince</a>, Charles Simpson, and <a href="/wiki/Don_Basham" title="Don Basham">Don Basham</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Ern_Baxter" title="Ern Baxter">Ern Baxter</a> and John Poole, formed the organization that would be 'the center of one of the most violent controversies (i.e., the Discipleship/Shepherding controversy) in Protestant charismatic history,' Christian Growth Ministries (CGM), headquartered in Ft. Lauderdale, Florida."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Their doctrine of a vertical, descending, "chain-of-command" established:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"a pyramid-shaped, multi-tiered organizational structure, which had at the top echelon of the pyramid (it just so happened) none other than the Fab Five themselves, who claimed (conveniently) to be in "submission" to each other, which arrangement, they purported, acted as a fail-safe "checks and balance" system to totally preclude them from falling prey to the corruptive properties of absolute power to which, historically, so many others (albeit, less spiritual than they, of course) succumbed."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>At the zenith of the pseudo-movement, "They had a national network of followers who formed pyramids of sheep and shepherds. Down through the pyramid went the orders, it was alleged, while up the same pyramid went the tithes."<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter-2">[2]</a></sup> The relationships that were formed became known theologically as "covenant relationships." A network of <a href="/wiki/Cell_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="Cell groups">cell groups</a> were formed. Members had to be submitted to a "shepherd", who in turn was submitted to the Five or their subordinates. "...large numbers of charismatic pastors began to be shepherded by the CGM leaders, a development that went uncharted but not unnoticed. It was uncharted because these relationships were personal and not institutional, so there were never any published lists of pastors and congregations being shepherded by CGM leaders...."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other movements influenced by the Shepherding doctrine were the Shiloh houses scattered across the USA (some of them transitioned into Calvary Chapels when they abandoned the shepherding movement ideas), <a href="/wiki/International_Churches_of_Christ" title="International Churches of Christ">International Churches of Christ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maranatha_Campus_Ministries" title="Maranatha Campus Ministries">Maranatha Campus Ministries</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Jesus_People_USA" title="Jesus People USA">Jesus People USA</a> (in or around '91 were adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Covenant_Church" title="Evangelical Covenant Church">Evangelical Covenant Church</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Great_Commission_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Commission International">Great Commission International</a> (today known as <a href="/wiki/Great_Commission_Ministries" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Commission Ministries">Great Commission Ministries</a>/<a href="/wiki/Great_Commission_Association_of_Churches" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Commission Association of Churches">Great Commission Association of Churches</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_controversy">Criticism and controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherding_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticism and controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Shepherding Movement became controversial:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"The heat of the controversy can be captured by reading an open letter, dated June 27, 1975, from Pat Robertson to Bob Mumford. Robertson said that in a recent visit to Louisville, Kentucky, he found cultish language like "submission" rather than churches, "shepherds" not pastors, and "relationships" but not Jesus. Robertson traveled to ORU and found a twenty-year-old "shepherd" who drew tithes from fellow students as part of their submission. Robertson, drawing from Juan Carlos Ortiz's Call to Discipleship, charged the leaders with placing personal revelations (rhema) on par with Scripture. He quoted a devotee as saying, "If God Almighty spoke to me, and I knew for a certainty that it was God speaking, and if my shepherd told me to do the opposite, I would obey my shepherd."<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Figures within the charismatic movement (such as <a href="/wiki/Pat_Robertson" title="Pat Robertson">Pat Robertson</a>) denounced the Shepherding Movement:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"<a href="/wiki/Pat_Robertson" title="Pat Robertson">Pat Robertson</a> banned the CGM leaders and erased all tapes that included them. Robertson used CBN to pronounce the shepherding teaching 'witchcraft' and said the only difference between the discipleship group and <a href="/wiki/Jonestown" title="Jonestown">Jonestown</a> was '<a href="/wiki/Drinking_the_Kool-Aid" title="Drinking the Kool-Aid">Kool-Aid</a>.' <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Kuhlman" title="Kathryn Kuhlman">Kathryn Kuhlman</a> refused to appear together with Bob Mumford at the 1975 Conference on the Holy Spirit in Jerusalem. <a href="/wiki/Demos_Shakarian" title="Demos Shakarian">Demos Shakarian</a> the founder and director of FGBMFI declared the CGM leaders <i>persona non grata</i>. The number of voices swelled as criticism came from <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Bennett_(priest)" title="Dennis Bennett (priest)">Dennis Bennett</a>, Ken Sumrall, Thomas F. Zimmerman (General Superintendent of the Assemblies of God), and David duPlessis."<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The Fort Lauderdale Five eventually parted company. Derek Prince and Bob Mumford both publicly distanced themselves from the teachings. Derek Prince withdrew in 1983, stating his belief that "we were guilty of the Galatian error: having begun in the Spirit, we quickly degenerated into the flesh."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Bob Mumford issued a "Formal Repentance Statement to the Body of Christ" in November 1989 and was quoted as saying, "I repent. I ask forgiveness."<sup id="cite_ref-Mumford_repents_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mumford_repents-8">[8]</a></sup> In the same article, Mumford also acknowledged abuses that had occurred because of his teaching on submission:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Mumford decided that he needed to publicly 'repent' of his responsibility in setting up a system where so many people were hurt by <i>misuses of authority</i>. 'Some families were split up and lives turned upside down,' says Mumford. 'Some of these families are still not back together.'"</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This emphasis resulted in "perverse and unbiblical obedience" to leaders, Mumford said.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"In his statement, Mumford admitted that he had not heeded earlier warnings about doctrinal error from Hayford and two others. "While it was not my intent to be willful," he said, "I ignored their input to my own hurt and the injury of others." ...He admitted that there had been an "unhealthy submission resulting in perverse and unbiblical obedience to human leaders." He took personal responsibility for these abuses, saying that many of them happened under his sphere of leadership."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mumford_repents_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mumford_repents-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_controversy">Australian controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherding_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Australian controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Shepherding movement within Australia was represented by the <a href="/wiki/Logos_Foundation_(Australia)" title="Logos Foundation (Australia)">Logos Foundation</a> under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Howard_Carter_(evangelist)" title="Howard Carter (evangelist)">Howard Carter</a>. It came to a sudden end when Carter was found to be immoral, although it did persist beyond the peak of the North American movement. Relocating from the Blue Mountains of NSW in 1987, its headquarters were in the Queensland city of <a href="/wiki/Toowoomba" title="Toowoomba">Toowoomba</a>, providing a conservative and sympathetic demographic. The Australian expression of the movement evidenced a measure of authoritarian characteristics, negatively effecting many of its constituents. In Australia it gained public attention when it initiated a campaign to resist the Australian Labor Party's attempt to introduce their version of a Bill of Rights in 1985 and in 1989 also engaged in the Queensland state election campaign. Prior to this, Logos Foundation (Australia) had morphed from being an organisation servicing the Charismatic Renewal across the mainline denominations into a church movement and activist group addressing socio-political issues informed by historic Reformed theology and, in particular, the Christian Reconstruction Movement in the USA. The election campaign backfired dramatically when the combined leaders of mainstream churches and a number of politicians all publicly denounced the Foundation and distanced themselves from it. During the election campaign, Carter, under the banner of Logos Foundation, placed extravagant advertisements in the media that were anti-abortion, anti-pornography and advocated for re-introduction of the death penalty. Logos Foundation ceased to operate from the early 1990s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shepherding_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The degree to which the Shepherding Movement still exists today is unclear. While both Charles Simpson and Bob Mumford have made public statements disavowing the movement, or at least distancing themselves from it, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.csmpublishing.org/ab_bio.php">Simpson's biography</a> on the website of Charles Simpson Ministries highlights his co-founding of <i>New Wine</i> Magazine and specifically mentions Baxter, Mumford, and Prince as "notable Bible teachers" associated with the magazine. The website <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.csmpublishing.org/ab_linkministries.php">also lists</a> Derek Prince Ministries and Lifechangers by Bob Mumford as "ministry allies."</p>
<p>David Moore's 2004 book <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> on the Shepherding Movement takes an impartial, scholarly look at the movement, which includes interviews with all living primary and many secondary individuals.</p>
<p>The Shepherding Movement ideologies live on today in some groups, most notably the Korean group called <a href="/wiki/University_Bible_Fellowship" title="University Bible Fellowship">University Bible Fellowship</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> who is currently active on over 70 US college campuses. John Bevere's <i>Under Cover</i> and Watchman Nee's <i>Spiritual Authority</i> teach unquestioning submission to pastors as a "covering," and are responsible for spiritual abuse in non-denominational charismatic churches.</p>
