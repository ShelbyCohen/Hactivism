<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious">Religious</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Christian_movements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Religious">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The modern <a href="/wiki/24-7_Prayer_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="24-7 Prayer Movement">24-7 Prayer Movement</a>: a movement spanning denominations focusing on the pursuit of <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> as the focus of one's life. The <a href="/wiki/International_House_of_Prayer" title="International House of Prayer">International House of Prayer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_MO" class="mw-redirect" title="Kansas City, MO">Kansas City, MO</a> is a visible example of this concept.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_Israelism" title="British Israelism">British Israelism</a> or "Anglo-Israelism": The Christian belief that many modern descendants of British and European heritage are descended from the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Lost_Tribes" title="Ten Lost Tribes">Ten Lost Tribes</a> or directly from the <a href="/wiki/Tribe_of_Judah" title="Tribe of Judah">Tribe of Judah</a> and thereby the heirs of the covenants with <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a> <a href="/wiki/Isaac" title="Isaac">Isaac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charismatic_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Charismatic movement">Charismatic movement</a> or "Neo-Pentecostalism": <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostal</a> beliefs and practices spread to churches outside of the Holiness tradition.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charismatic_Restorationism" class="mw-redirect" title="Charismatic Restorationism">Charismatic Restorationism</a>: Pentecostalism beliefs and practices together with <a href="/wiki/Restorationism_(Christian_primitivism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Restorationism (Christian primitivism)">restorationist</a> elements that reject <a href="/wiki/Denominationalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Denominationalism">denominationalism</a>. Closely related to <a href="/wiki/Latter_Rain_(post-World_War_II_movement)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latter Rain (post-World War II movement)">Latter Rain Movement</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_ecumenism" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian ecumenism">Christian ecumenism</a>: The promotion of unity or cooperation between distinct religious groups or denominations of the Christian religion.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Family_Movement" title="Christian Family Movement">Christian Family Movement</a>: a U.S. movement of parish and small groups of families that meet to reinforce Christian values.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_fundamentalism" title="Christian fundamentalism">Christian fundamentalism</a>: sought to assert a minimal set of traditional Christian beliefs against the influences of Modernist Christianity; became a movement of separation from the "mainline" Protestant churches.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Identity" title="Christian Identity">Christian Identity</a>: A label applied to a wide variety of loosely affiliated groups and churches with <a href="/wiki/White_supremacy" title="White supremacy">white supremacist</a> beliefs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_naturism" title="Christian naturism">Christian naturism</a>: A movement which believes that God never intended for people to be ashamed of their <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">bodies</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Torah-submission" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Torah-submission">Christian Torah-submission</a>: A movement of Christians that pursue lifestyles that are both fully dedicated to Jesus Christ and also obedient to God's commands found in the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Zionism" title="Christian Zionism">Christian Zionism</a> (called "Christian Restorationism" until the mid-twentieth century): The belief that the return of the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a> and the establishment of the State of Israel is in accordance with Biblical prophecy and is a necessary precondition for the <a href="/wiki/Second_coming" class="mw-redirect" title="Second coming">return of Jesus</a> to reign on Earth.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Confessing_Movement" title="Confessing Movement">Confessing Movement</a>: a neo-Evangelical movement within several mainline Protestant churches to return those churches to what members see as greater theological orthodoxy.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conservative_Evangelicalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservative Evangelicalism">Conservative Evangelicalism</a>: a division of evangelicalism characterised by reformed theology.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Convergence_Movement" title="Convergence Movement">Convergence Movement</a>: a move among evangelical and charismatic churches in the United States to blend charismatic worship with liturgies from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a> and other liturgical sources.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Creationism" title="Creationism">Creationism</a>: There are several schools of creationist thought, but all include some belief in the divine creation of human beings over a short period of time (distinguishing them from <a href="/wiki/Theistic_evolution" title="Theistic evolution">theistic evolutionists</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholicism</a>: A movement on the part of some particular Eastern churches to join in visible communion with the Bishop of Rome after the <a href="/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_Schism" title="East–West Schism">East–West Schism</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emerging_church_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Emerging church movement">Emerging church movement</a>: a transdenominational movement that seeks to reshape Christian epistemology, doctrines, and practices to fit into a postmodern mold.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelicalism</a>: A world-wide <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> movement maintaining that the essence of <a href="/wiki/The_gospel" title="The gospel">the gospel</a> consists in the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Faith" title="Faith">faith</a> in Christ's <a href="/wiki/Atonement_in_Christianity" title="Atonement in Christianity">atonement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxf_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxf-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Free_Grace_theology" title="Free Grace theology">Free Grace Movement</a>: Originally a reaction against Reformed soteriology making inroads in <a href="/wiki/Dispensationalism" title="Dispensationalism">Dispensationalism</a>, it has since developed away from some dispensational soteriology, like its understanding of <a href="/wiki/Repentance" title="Repentance">repentance</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Focolare_Movement" title="Focolare Movement">Focolare Movement</a>: an international organization that promotes the ideals of unity and universal brotherhood.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grace_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Grace Movement">Grace Movement</a>: A movement beginning in the 1930s embracing the Mid-Acts position Dispensational system of Bible interpretation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Roots" title="Hebrew Roots">Hebrew Roots</a> movement: Emphasizes the Jewish roots of Christianity and understanding Jesus and the New Testament in the light of Old Testament observances and Jewish tradition.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Holiness_movement" title="Holiness movement">Holiness movement</a>: A Wesleyan movement beginning in the 19th century which emphasized a personal experience of holiness, and which gave rise to Pentecostalism and the Charismatic movement.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/House_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="House Church">House Church</a> or <a href="/wiki/Simple_church" title="Simple church">Simple Church</a> movement is a worldwide shift of Christian expression in small groups rather than in formal institutionalized buildings.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lesbian_and_Gay_Christian_Movement" title="Lesbian and Gay Christian Movement">Lesbian and Gay Christian Movement</a>: officially, a British movement seeking a more inclusive church; unofficially, part of a larger number of <a href="/wiki/LGBT-welcoming_church_programs" class="mw-redirect" title="LGBT-welcoming church programs">LGBT-welcoming church programs</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liberal_Christianity" title="Liberal Christianity">Liberal Christianity</a> (Protestant) or <a href="/wiki/Modernism_(Roman_Catholicism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernism (Roman Catholicism)">Modernism (Catholicism)</a>: school of Christian thought which rose as a direct challenge to more conservative traditional Christian orthodoxy.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Missional_living" title="Missional living">Missional Movement</a>: A modern movement of Christianity that seeks to emphasize the call of the church towards a missions type of lifestyle focused on themes like <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inculturation" title="Inculturation">inculturation</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neo-orthodoxy" title="Neo-orthodoxy">Neo-orthodoxy</a>: emphasis on the <a href="/wiki/Transcendence_(religion)" title="Transcendence (religion)">transcendence</a> of God, the reality of sin, and an existentialist encounter with the word of God.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_Thought_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="New Thought Movement">New Thought Movement</a>: belief in <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> interpretation of the Bible. <a href="/wiki/Phineas_Quimby" title="Phineas Quimby">Phineas Quimby</a> is generally considered the founder of New Thought. His influence on the New Thought movement can be traced through <a href="/wiki/Unity_Church" title="Unity Church">Unity Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Divine_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Divine Science">Divine Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious_Science" title="Religious Science">Religious Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Understanding_Principles_for_Better_Living_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Understanding Principles for Better Living Church">Understanding Principles for Better Living Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seicho-No-Ie" class="mw-redirect" title="Seicho-No-Ie">Seicho-No-Ie</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oxford_Movement" title="Oxford Movement">Oxford Movement</a>: A nineteenth century movement to more closely align Anglicanism with its Roman Catholic heritage; part of <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Catholicism" title="Anglo-Catholicism">Anglo-Catholicism</a>, a movement that continues into the 21st century.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paleo-Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleo-Orthodoxy">Paleo-Orthodoxy</a>: evaluating later theology in light of the writings of the early Church.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostalism</a>: the gifts of the Holy Spirit are a normal part of the "Full Gospel".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prosperity_Theology" class="mw-redirect" title="Prosperity Theology">Prosperity Theology</a>: (sometimes referred to as the prosperity gospel, the health and wealth gospel, or the gospel of success) is a Christian religious doctrine that financial blessing is the will of God for Christians, and that faith, positive speech, and donations to Christian ministries will always increase one's material wealth.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Positive_Christianity" title="Positive Christianity">Positive Christianity</a>: a movement within <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> which blended ideas of <a href="/wiki/Racial_purity" class="mw-redirect" title="Racial purity">racial purity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nazi_ideology" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi ideology">Nazi ideology</a> with elements of Christianity.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Postmodern_Christianity" title="Postmodern Christianity">Postmodern Christianity</a>: an understanding of Christianity that has been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">continental philosophy</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Restoration_Movement" title="Restoration Movement">Restoration Movement</a>, also known as the "Stone-Campbell movement": a group of religious reform movements that arose during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Great_Awakening" title="Second Great Awakening">Second Great Awakening</a> and sought to renew the whole Christian church "after the New Testament pattern", in contrast to divided Christendom, of Catholicism and Protestantism.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Restorationism_(Christian_primitivism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Restorationism (Christian primitivism)">Restorationism (Christian primitivism)</a>: the belief that a purer form of Christianity should be restored using the <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">early church</a> as a model.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political">Political</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Christian_movements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Political_theology" title="Political theology">Political theology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christianity_and_politics" title="Christianity and politics">Christianity and politics</a></div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_anarchism" title="Christian anarchism">Christian anarchism</a>: the rejection of all authority and power other than God, sometimes even including the organized church. Christian anarchists believe that Jesus of Nazareth was an anarchist, and that his movement was reversed by strong Judaist and Roman statist influences.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_communism" title="Christian communism">Christian communism</a>: is a form of <a href="/wiki/Religious_communism" title="Religious communism">religious communism</a> based on the teachings of Jesus and the way of life of the Apostles and first Christians.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Democracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Democracy">Christian Democracy</a>: is a political ideology, born at the end of the 19th century, largely as a result of the papal encyclical Rerum Novarum of Pope Leo XIII, in which the Vatican recognizes workers' misery and agrees that something should be done about it, in reaction to the rise of the socialist and trade-union movements. The <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democrats" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Democrats">Christian Democrats</a> came out of this movement.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_left" title="Christian left">Christian left</a>: those who hold a strong Christian belief and share left-wing or liberal ideals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_libertarianism" title="Christian libertarianism">Christian libertarianism</a>: those who are committed to <a href="/wiki/Non-aggression_principle" title="Non-aggression principle">non-aggression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Property_rights_(economics)" title="Property rights (economics)">property rights</a>, strongly opposed to State coercion and (military, social, and economic) <a href="/wiki/Economic_interventionism" title="Economic interventionism">interventionism</a> as unjustifiable on Christian ethical grounds, advocate the promotion of virtue by persuasion only and either <a href="/wiki/Minarchism" title="Minarchism">minimal government</a> or no government (see Christian anarchism).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_right" title="Christian right">Christian right</a>: encompasses a spectrum of conservative Christian political and social movements and organizations characterized by their strong support of social values they deem traditional in the United States and other western countries.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_socialism" title="Christian socialism">Christian socialism</a>: those on the Christian left whose politics are both Christian and socialist, broadly including <a href="/wiki/Liberation_theology" title="Liberation theology">Liberation theology</a> and the doctrine of the social gospel.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dominionism" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominionism">Dominionism</a>: a movement among socially conservative Christians to gain influence or control over secular civil government through political action — seeking either a nation governed by Christians or a nation governed by a Christian understanding of biblical law.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Evangelical_left" title="Evangelical left">Evangelical left</a>: part of the Christian evangelical movement but who generally function on the left wing of that movement, either politically or theologically, or both.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Green_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Christianity">Green Christianity</a>: Christian-based opposition to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and other environmental problems</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liberation_theology" title="Liberation theology">Liberation theology</a>: an important and controversial school movement in the <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_theological_praxis" title="Christian theological praxis">praxis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a>, it has been officially condemned. It had broad influence in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and explores the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">Christian theology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_activism" class="mw-redirect" title="Political activism">political activism</a>, particularly in areas of <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>. It gave priority to the economically poor and oppressed of the human community. See also <a href="/wiki/Black_theology" title="Black theology">Black theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dalit_theology" title="Dalit theology">Dalit theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feminist_theology" title="Feminist theology">Feminist theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minjung_theology" title="Minjung theology">Minjung theology</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Queer_theology" title="Queer theology">Queer theology</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Progressive_Christianity" title="Progressive Christianity">Progressive Christianity</a>: focuses on the biblical injunctions that God's people live correctly, that they promote social justice and act to fight poverty, racism, and other forms of injustice.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rexism" class="mw-redirect" title="Rexism">Rexism</a> A Belgian <a href="/wiki/Fascist" class="mw-redirect" title="Fascist">fascist</a> movement derived from the Roman Catholic social teachings concerning <a href="/wiki/Christus_Rex" class="mw-redirect" title="Christus Rex">Christus Rex</a>, and it was also the title of a conservative Catholic journal</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Social_Gospel" title="Social Gospel">Social Gospel</a> movement: a Protestant Christian intellectual movement that was most prominent in the late 19th century and early 20th century. The movement applies Christian principles to social problems, especially poverty, liquor, drugs, crime, racial tensions, slums, bad hygiene, poor schools, and the danger of war. Theologically the Social Gospel leaders were overwhelmingly post-<a href="/wiki/Millenarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Millenarian">Millenarian</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophical">Philosophical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Christian_movements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Philosophical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_theology" title="Philosophical theology">Philosophical theology</a></div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">Christian asceticism</a>: a life which is characterised by refraining from worldly pleasures and luxuries, such as wealth, private possessions, and alcohol.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_atheism" title="Christian atheism">Christian atheism</a>: position in which the belief in the God of Christianity is rejected, but the moral teachings of Jesus are valued.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_existentialism" title="Christian existentialism">Christian existentialism</a>: a school of thought founded by the 19th-century Danish philosopher and father of <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existentialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard" title="Søren Kierkegaard">Søren Kierkegaard</a>, which emphasizes subjectivity and deep reflection on purpose, the apparent <a href="/wiki/Absurdity" title="Absurdity">absurdity</a> of life and the cosmos, the inevitable despair of an awakened existence, and finding authenticity of self by faith in God.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_vegetarianism" title="Christian vegetarianism">Christian vegetarianism</a>: the dietary practice of <a href="/wiki/Vegetarianism" title="Vegetarianism">vegetarianism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Veganism" title="Veganism">veganism</a> based on the idea that Jesus, the twelve apostles and the early <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Christian" title="Jewish Christian">Jewish followers</a> of Jesus (the <a href="/wiki/Nazarene_(sect)" title="Nazarene (sect)">Nazarenes</a>) were vegetarians.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_pacifism" title="Christian pacifism">Christian pacifism</a>: Christian churches, groups or communities teaching that Jesus was himself a pacifist who taught and practiced total nonviolence, and that his followers must do likewise.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Postmodern_Christianity" title="Postmodern Christianity">Postmodern Christianity</a>: an understanding of Christianity that has been influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodern</a> trend in 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Continental_philosophy" title="Continental philosophy">continental philosophy</a>, associated with literary <a href="/wiki/Deconstruction" title="Deconstruction">deconstruction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Postliberal_theology" title="Postliberal theology">postliberal</a> or narrative theology, and the <a href="/wiki/Emerging_church" title="Emerging church">Emerging church</a> movement.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Weak_theology" class="mw-redirect" title="Weak theology">Weak theology</a>: a form of postmodern Christianity that emphasizes the idea of the weakness of God.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quiverfull" title="Quiverfull">Quiverfull</a>: considers childbearing in marriage a Christian duty, emphasizes the continual role of Providence in controlling whether or not a woman conceives, and eschews all forms of human-mediated <a href="/wiki/Contraception" class="mw-redirect" title="Contraception">contraception</a>. Generally involves complete submission of the wife to the husband; women generally don't work and children are homeschooled.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wedding_of_the_Weddings" title="Wedding of the Weddings">Wedding of the Weddings</a> in Poland: considers the wedding celebration as a deeply religious acting that should not be distorted by alcohol consumption ("Jesus should enter the wedding house and not be driven away by alcohol")</li>
</ul>
