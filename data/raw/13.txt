<p><b>Alicia Garza</b> (born January 4, 1981) is an African American activist and editorial writer who lives in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a>. She is of <a href="/wiki/African-American" class="mw-redirect" title="African-American">African-American</a> descent. She has organized around the issues of health, student services and rights, rights for domestic workers, ending <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">police brutality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-racism" title="Anti-racism">anti-racism</a>, and violence against <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">trans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gender_non-conforming" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender non-conforming">gender non-conforming</a> people of color. Her editorial writing has been published by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Feminist Wire,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Rolling Stone, Huffington Post and truthout.org. She currently directs Special Projects at the National Domestic Workers Alliance. She also co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Black_Lives_Matter" title="Black Lives Matter">Black Lives Matter</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activism">Activism</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_Lives_Matter">Black Lives Matter</span></h3>
<p>Garza, with <a href="/wiki/Opal_Tometi" title="Opal Tometi">Opal Tometi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patrisse_Cullors" title="Patrisse Cullors">Patrisse Cullors</a>, birthed the Black Lives Matters movement. Garza is credited with inspiring the slogan when, after the July 2013 acquittal of <a href="/wiki/George_Zimmerman" title="George Zimmerman">George Zimmerman</a>, she posted on Facebook: "Black people. I love you. I love us. Our lives matter, Black Lives Matter" which Cullors then shared with the hashtag #BlackLivesMatter. Garza's organization Black Lives Matter was spurred on by the deaths of black people by police in recent media and racial disparities within the U.S. criminal justice system. She was also struck by the similarities of <a href="/wiki/Trayvon_Martin" title="Trayvon Martin">Trayvon Martin</a> to her younger brother, feeling that it could have been him killed instead.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Garza led the 2015 <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Ride" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom Ride">Freedom Ride</a> to Ferguson, organized by Cullors and <a href="/wiki/Darnell_Moore" class="mw-redirect" title="Darnell Moore">Darnell Moore</a> that launched the building of BlackLivesMatter chapters across the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Garza self-identifies as a <a href="/wiki/Queer" title="Queer">queer</a> woman, and her spouse is biracial and transgender; Garza draws on all of these experiences in her organizing and activism.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span></h3>
<p>Previously, Garza had served as the director of People Organized to Win Employment Rights in the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a>. During her time in the position, she won the right for youth to use public transportation for free in San Francisco and also fought <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a> and exposing police brutality in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Garza is on the board of directors of <a href="/wiki/Forward_Together" class="mw-redirect" title="Forward Together">Forward Together</a>'s Oakland California branch and is also involved with Black Organizing for Leadership and Dignity.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Act_of_protest">Act of protest</span></h3>
<p>Garza participated in an attempt to stop a <a href="/wiki/Bay_Area_Rapid_Transit" title="Bay Area Rapid Transit">Bay Area Rapid Transit</a> train for four and a half hours, a time chosen to reflect the time that <a href="/wiki/Shooting_of_Michael_Brown" title="Shooting of Michael Brown">Michael Brown</a>'s body was left in the street after he was killed. The protesters stopped the train for an hour and a half by chaining themselves both to the inside of the train and the outside, making it impossible for the door to close. The event ended when police removed the protestors by dismantling part of the train.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_Presidential_Race">2016 Presidential Race</span></h3>
<p>While Garza has been critical of <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> she has also been critical of <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> She declined to vote for Clinton, claiming that "The Clintons use black people for votes, but then don't do anything for black communities after they're elected. They use us for photo ops".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> She voted for <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> in the California Democratic Primary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition_and_awards">Recognition and awards</span></h2>
<p>Garza was recognized on the Root 100 list of African American Achievers between the ages of 25 and 45. She was also recognized on the Politico50 2015 guide to Thinkers, Doers, and Visionaries along with Cullors and Tometi.</p>
<p>Garza has received the Local Hero award from the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Guardian" title="San Francisco Bay Guardian">San Francisco Bay Guardian</a>. She has been twice awarded by the <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Milk_Democratic_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvey Milk Democratic Club">Harvey Milk Democratic Club</a> the Bayard Rustin Community Activist Award for her work fighting racism and gentrification in San Francisco. She has also been awarded the Jeanne Gauna Communicate Justice Award from the Centre for Media Justice.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Garza, Patrisse Cullors, and Opal Tometi (as "The Women of #BlackLivesMatter") were among the nine runners-up for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Advocate" title="The Advocate">The Advocate</a>'</i>s Person of the Year.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
