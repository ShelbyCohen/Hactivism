<p style="text-align: justify;">Tall palms cast shadows over the broad central square of Oaxaca, the capital city of the Mexican state of the same name. Their shade is welcomed both by those of the city’s 250,000 residents that find themselves on foot in the strong mountain sun, and by the many foreign tourists attracted to Oaxaca by a rich history and famed gastronomical traditions.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The scene, taking in souvenir sellers plying their trade to unhurried visitors, and local brass bands blowing melodies alongside the stone walls of the colonial cathedral, does little to give indication of a series of events that took place in the grand plaza over a six-month period just under a decade ago.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The second half of 2006 saw Oaxaca placed in the ever-wandering international spotlight, as images were broadcast of a quickly developing popular movement that brought thousands of the city’s residents into the streets under the banner of the newly formed Popular Assembly of the Peoples of Oaxaca (Asamblea Popular de los Pueblos de Oaxaca &#8211; APPO). They moved in united protest against a state governor whose mandate they considered illegitimate, and used their large numbers to take control of the city away from authorities &#8211; officials and law enforcement bodies were effectively banished, as Oaxacans pushed for the political changes that they deemed essential for the state.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">This process of transformation, that saw central Oaxaca converted from the heart of the highly important tourist industry (tourism is the state’s largest source of revenue) into the site of mass sit-ins and activism, has its origins in protests undertaken in May 2006 by some 40,000 members of Section 22 (Sección 22) &#8211; the Oaxacan branch of the National Education Workers Union (Síndicato Nacional de Trabajadores de la Educación &#8211; SNTE).</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Section 22 has long been considered the most radical branch of the SNTE &#8211; which with 1.5 million members is the largest union in Latin America &#8211; since it became highly politicised in the early 1980s. At that time, teachers began to campaign strongly, as they continue to do to the present day, for increased salaries and improved educational resources.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Oaxaca, the second poorest of Mexico’s 31 states, is consistently ranked as having the country’s worst schooling system. Primary and secondary schools in the many isolated populations often do not have such basic resources as permanent roofing and bathrooms. Furthermore, teachers consistently report having to use their own salaries to provide educational equipment for pupils. There is also a widely held sentiment that the educational needs of the two thirds of Oaxaca’s 3.6 million inhabitants that belong to indigenous groups are not met, most specifically mother-tongue education in the state’s 16 autochthonous languages.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Section 22 teachers have established a tradition of yearly strikes and sit-ins to maintain pressure on authorities to take notice of educational hardships in the state. In May 2006 teachers from across the state of Oaxaca congregated in its capital’s central square to participate in the 26th anual strike. They aimed to pressure Governor Ulises Ruíz into negotiating a rise in salaries and improved classroom conditions. Furthermore, Section 22 was critical of Díaz’s controversial spending of state funds on modernising historical parts of the city, as well as the questionable circumstances in which he had been elected two years earlier.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The teachers union&#8217;s occupation of the central square was met by mixed responses from Oaxacans. Though certain sectors of society supported the annual strike, others saw the time that teachers were spent away from their pupils as highly damaging to young Oaxacans. Many also held the belief that teachers already enjoyed salaries far higher than many others in the workforce, and that they only employed industrial action to seek increasingly disproportionate wages. As a result, local newspapers often wrote critically of the teachers’ continued presence in the central square as the strike extended through May into June.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">However, events that took place on 14th June  would largely bring this resentment to an end. Of the 40,000 teachers that had arrived to begin the sit-in, only 10,000 remained, however, they continued to push to negotiate their demands with Governor Ruíz. The politician refused to engage in dialogues, and instead sought to end the strike by force. On June 14 police units were deployed to storm the teachers’ camps to disband the sit-in, whilst helicopters hovering overhead dropped tear gas on the teaching professionals. Beatings and rape were reported during this official operation.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">A change in the attitude of Oaxacans came about following these events. No longer ambivalent or critical, 300,000 residents took to the streets in outrage at the perceived abuse of power that Ruíz had shown upon ordering the forceful disbandment of the Section 22 strike. The swollen numbers quickly retook the city’s main square, and with calls of “Ulises out!” (“¡Fuera Ulises!”) demanded that the governor step down.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Within days, the Oaxacan popular uprising, which incorporated many sectors of society, adopted a formal shape. Followingly the lead of the numerically vital Section 22, the Popular Assembly of the Peoples of Oaxaca (Asamblea Popular de Pueblos de Oaxaca &#8211; APPO) was formed. The group unified more than 350 local organisations that were opposed to the governor, and began to maintain constant sit-ins in the main square and outside all public buildings. APPO’s unignorable presence was to extend until November.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">It must be noted that the radical change observed in Oaxacans did not represent sudden approval of the frequent Section 22 strikes and demands for increased salaries for teachers. Rather, the people of Oaxaca shared teachers’ criticisms of the governor, and were appalled by his aggressive use of force against an entirely legal, though controversial, union movement. The events of June 14 merely acted to unite the many Oaxacans that already held anger towards the Ruíz administration, subsequently allowing a city-wide popular movement to emerge.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Animosity towards Governor Ruíz had initially arisen after he gained election in 2004 amidst circumstances that many considered fraudulent. Subsequently, in the first two years of his the governor’s administration, accusations of human rights violations, wasteful spending, nepotism and corruption had been frequent and prevalent. People felt indignation at the failure of the federal system, specifically the Federal Electoral Court (Tribunal Federal Electoral), to respond to these worries.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Gustavo Esteva, a Oaxacan intellectual and key figure in the APPO movement, wrote during APPO&#8217;s apogee that the organisation considered itself to have risen against ‘an oppressive power structure’. He wrote that a political class had spent 25 years effectively dismantling Mexico’s state apparatus, acting in its own restricted interests whilst disregarding the welfare of the population. As such, APPO did not merely wish to take power in order to position its own representatives within the existing system, but rather, pushed for a profound restructuring of Mexican politics. APPO aimed to strengthen formal democracy, and to provide raised levels of honesty, transparency and civil involvement in Oaxacan, and Mexican, governance.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Local and state police were no longer willing to obey Ruíz’s commands to break up the protests. Respectful of the vast numbers of their fellow citizens on the streets of the city centre, police were barely to be seen in areas ‘held’ by APPO until events came to a head in late October. Despite this lack of official law enforcement, a human rights organisation reported that during the months that APPO operated in the city centre, there were fewer incidents of civil violence that in any other period in the preceding 10 years. This comparative calm was even maintained during the eight ‘mega marches’, each boasting more than 100,000 participants, that were held over the period of the uprising.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">However, mainstream reporting of happenings in central Oaxaca often did not communicate the tendency towards pacifism that APPO proclaimed and largely achieved. APPO members complained that local radio and television stations were used as a tool by Governor Ruíz and other authorities to produce propaganda, displaying the activists&#8217; movement as illegitimate, anarchic and violent.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The lack of correspondence between the lived experiences of APPO members and the information they received from media outlets produced increasing frustration. Protesters’ disillusionment with the press culminated on August 1st, when between 2,500 and 5,000 women participated in what was dubbed the ‘March of the Pots and Pans’ (La Marcha de las Cacerolas) to protest Ruíz’s continued refusal to renounce power. During the march a number of women made their way to the city’s public-service radio station, and forcefully took control of the building and its equipment. Despite having no knowledge of broadcasting, they quickly gained a grasp of the necessary techniques and began to use the station, which was renamed ‘Radio Cacerola’, to divulge the experiences of APPO members that mainstream media did not touch upon.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">When Governor Ruíz had the station’s signal blocked several weeks later, APPO protesters retaliated by taking control of all of the city’s radio and television stations. This mass occupation lasted for several days, after which control was returned to the owners of all but one of the media outlets. APPO’s heavy focus on media resources displayed a keen awareness of the vital role held by local and national press in shaping the perception and success of popular movements.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The presence of activists in central Oaxaca was further felt when more than 1,000 road barricades were established. These blocks were put in place at 11pm each evening, leaving streets intransitable for vehicles till they were removed at 6am. This drastic measure was deemed necessary after a series of attacks on APPO members participating in sit-ins. ‘Mercenaries’ loyal to Ruíz had circulated the city in a convoy of 4x4s, firing into the air above protesters heads in an attempt to disband the movement. However, APPO’s use of overwhelming numbers effectively locked official forces out of the city centre, and increasingly distanced Ruíz from the power that he claimed; the governor was forced to secretly hold meetings with his staff on private property throughout the period of the uprising.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The predominant response of the federal government to events unfurling in Oaxaca was inaction. Politicians from the ruling National Action Party (Partido Acción Nacional &#8211; PAN), and the highly influential Institutional Revolutionary Party (Partido Revolucionario Institucional &#8211; PRI), declared their support for Ruíz, refusing to consider the step of declaring the state ‘without government’, which would have implicated the need to impose an interim governor.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">President Vicente Fox was pressured by those with large vested interests in the Oaxacan economy to ‘put an end’ to the APPO movement. Tourism, which is vital to the state’s economy, had been all but eradicated following the uprising. Foreign governments, including that of the United States, issued warnings to their citizens to discourage travel to the state. Furthermore, the ​<em>Guelagetza</em> &#8211; a yearly festival held to showcase the traditions of Oaxaca’s culturally diverse population &#8211; was cancelled by Governor Ruíz, resulting in an estimated loss of many millions of dollars for the local economy. APPO responded by organising a ​&#8217;Popular Guelagetza&#8217;​; a free event that aimed to give the many Oaxaca previously excluded from the official Guelagetza, due to high prices, an opportunity to form part of the state-wide celebration.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Oaxacans aimed to pressure the federal government to act in their favour and remove Ruíz from power. On September 21st 5,000 APPO members left Oaxaca on foot in direction of Mexico City, as part of an effort to bring their state’s plight to the nation’s capital. Throughout the journey of 17 days &#8211; the group arrived in Mexico City on October 8th &#8211; it is reported that they received broad support from fellow Mexicans through whose states they passed.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Days before the arrival of the 5,000 in Mexico City, the Minister of the Interior had convened a meeting of 100 prominent Oaxacans in the capital in order to negotiate a solution to the impasse. However, those present at the talks did not reach an agreement, and important artists, intellectuals and indigenous leaders walked out of the discussions in protest at the scant representation of Oaxacan’s indigenous majority.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">On October 10th, following months of inaction, the Mexican Senate decided to finally turn its attention to the issue of Oaxaca, and on the 19th October  declared that a “disappearance of government” should be called. This would implicate the removal of Ruíz from office, due to a lack of functioning authority in Oaxaca. However, legal complexities meant that the Federal Governement never formally moved to end the governor’s mandate, despite a further petition being released on 29th October by the Senate and the Chamber of Representatives calling for Ruíz’s renunciation. The Oaxacan governor’s response to these federal measures was to appeal to the Supreme Court, and to state that he would never resign from his post.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">APPO members continued to insist that their actions had not disrupted the rule of law in the city, given that it had never existed. They argued that the political class had stripped official authority of all its credibility through a disregard for the rights and duties declared in Mexican law. They stated that the Federal Government’s failure to act conclusively and effectively to remove Ruíz was a further example of a failed political system controlled by a class that acted to protect its own, without regard for popular, democratic will.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Nonetheless, progress in negotiations between APPO and the government seemed to appear in late October when members of Section 22 &#8211; the teachers’ union &#8211; agreed to return to their classrooms. In return, the government agreed to meet original demands for improved salaries and conditions, as well as to grant freedom to Section 22 members imprisoned during the strike. Other sectors of APPO reacted to this development by accusing the teachers of having ‘sold out’ by ending their strike without demanding the resignation of Governor Ruíz. The inevitable result was internal fracturing within APPO &#8211; the organisation’s dependency on the large teachers’ union meant that Section 22’s departure represented a strong blow to the movement’s strength.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Any hope of further progress in negotiations between APPO and the government was dashed on 27th October . A group composed of police officers faithful to the governor and paramilitaries attacked the barricades maintained by APPO in the city centre. During the clash Brad Will, an American journalist working for Indymedia, was shot and killed. The death of the foreign national brought the turmoil in Oaxaca firmly into the view of the American mainstream media, and provided President Fox with ample justification to deploy 4,000 members of the Federal Preventative Police (Policía Preventativa Federal &#8211; PFP) to ‘restore law and order’.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The PFP arrived in Oaxaca on October 28th and began their task of taking control of the city. APPO members retreated to the campus of the Benito Juárez Autonomous University of Oaxaca (Universidad Autónoma Benito Juárez de Oaxaca &#8211; UABJO), in order to protect the university’s radio station, which had been used as the protesters’ main media output. Although APPO leaders had declared that they were firmly determined to maintain a policy of nonviolence throughout the intervention by federal police, as the day drew to a close three members of the organisation had died, alongside many injured, and many ‘disappeared’. Fears for the human rights of protesters were rife, as APPO members were captured in their homes and at the barricades.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Despite the pressure imposed by the intervening police force, on 29th October  APPO organised three large marches to display their opposition to the PFP’s occupation. On 2nd November the federal police attacked UABJO grounds, provoking what has been described as the most brutal clash between civilians and police in Mexico’s recent history. The highly superior force of the police could not be long resisted by APPO.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">On 6th November, an interim report was released stating that during the violence of late October and early November 17 people had died, and 138 had been injured. In the months that followed, and with the arrival of Felipe Calderón to the Mexican presidency, also a member of the PAN, hundreds of APPO leaders and supporters were imprisoned. As a result, the structure and strength of the organisation was highly damaged, and possibilities of further efforts to achieve the removal of Ruíz were crippled. The governor continued in power, completing his term.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">From the overwhelming 300,000 people that intitially took to the streets in the days following the attack on the teachers’ sit-in in the main square in mid-June, Ronald Waterbury writes that barely 4,000 emerged to show their support for APPO during a march organised on January 11 2007. The harsh blows received from the federal government broke the internal strength of APPO, largely ending the cohesion of the varied groups that had originally united to form the assembly. Likewise, the ideological differences held by the many factions within APPO, most notably evidenced by the early departure of Section 22, weakened the movement. The organisation had brought together the great ideological and cultural diversity of the Oaxacan population, but was unable to gain the wider national backing that may have seen it achieve its principal goal of ousting Governor Ruíz.</p>
