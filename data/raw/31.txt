				 				<strong><a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/author/nikole-brown/" title="Posts by Nikole Brown" rel="author">Nikole Brown</a></strong>
				
 				<div class="hr"></div><h4>Tags:</h4><span class="tag-links"><a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/tag/andy-smith/" rel="tag">Andy Smith</a>  <a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/tag/carol-adams/" rel="tag">Carol Adams</a>  <a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/tag/ecofeminism/" rel="tag">ecofeminism</a>  <a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/tag/greta-gaard/" rel="tag">Greta Gaard</a>  <a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/tag/judith-plant/" rel="tag">Judith Plant</a>  <a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/tag/patricia-hill-collins/" rel="tag">Patricia Hill Collins</a>  <a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/tag/vandana-shiva/" rel="tag">vandana shiva</a>  <a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/tag/yayo-herrero/" rel="tag">Yayo Herrero</a></span>

			<div class="entry">
 				<p>As social and economic conditions intertwine with the growth of industrialized areas, environmental  challenges have become a hot button issue in political arenas. Due to dwindling resources and the mounting research on the human impact on the environment, government leaders are faced with the challenge to answer how to strike a balance between human rights and environmental protection with politics of a capitalist global market and power within the system. The <a href="http://www.unep.org/" target="_blank">United Nations Environment Programme</a> has stated that the new future of human rights and environment is the “awareness of how fundamental the environment is as a prerequisite to the enjoyment of human rights.” However, as <a href="http://internationalviewpoint.org/spip.php?article2407&amp;var_recherche=yayo" target="_blank">Yayo Herrero</a> notes, globalization for the sake of profit has lead to “the poverty of women and indigenous cultures, who are the first victims of the destruction of nature,” and globalization and the state of women must be critiqued for the future of gender and environmental equality. This viewpoint is shared by the movement and ideas of ecofeminists, such as Vandana Shiva, Judith Plant, and Carol Adams.</p>
<div id="attachment_24809" style="width: 310px" class="wp-caption alignleft"><a href="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/03/Dr.-Shiva.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-24809 " title="Dr. Shiva" src="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/03/Dr.-Shiva-300x201.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="201" srcset="http://www.genderacrossborders.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/03/Dr.-Shiva-300x201.jpg 300w, http://www.genderacrossborders.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/03/Dr.-Shiva.jpg 500w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Dr. Vandana Shiva photo courtesy of documentary.org</p></div>
<p>According to <a href="http://www.dosomething.org/" target="_blank">Do Something</a>, women bear the brunt of civil and environmental violations, as they account for 75 percent of displaced refugees and 66 percent of the world&#8217;s labor. While global feminism provides an arena to discuss the conditions of women, could ecofeminism provide an inclusive framework of ideas to not only address the impact and significance of globalization on human rights but the intricate environmental consequences? To merely address the state of women is to discount how the treatment of environmental issues sustains the conditions of a global market built around power hierarchies in dualisms (i.e. man/woman, human/nature, human/animal, logic/emotion, etc.).</p>
<p>Although ecofeminism has over 30 years of literature and activism, the academic realm has given more room for the expansion of other areas within feminist theory, especially in Western culture, which has lead to a lack of conversation around the connection of sustainable ideas and feminism as a keystone for global gender equality.  After all, the <a href="http://www.unfpa.org/public/" target="_blank">United Nations Population Fund </a>(UNFPA) explains in the State of the <a href="http://unfpa.org/swp/2009/" target="_blank">World&#8217;s Population report, 2009,</a></p>
<blockquote><p>Women—particularly those in poor countries—will be affected differently than men. They are among the most vulnerable to climate change, partly because in many countries they make up the larger share of the agricultural work force and partly because they tend to have access to fewer income-earning opportunities. Women manage households and care for family members, which often limits their mobility and increases their vulnerability to sudden weather-related natural disasters. Drought and erratic rainfall force women to work harder to secure food, water and energy for their homes. Girls drop out of school to help their mothers with these tasks. This cycle of deprivation, poverty and inequality undermines the social capital needed to deal effectively with climate change.</p></blockquote>
<p>By definition, ecofeminism is a movement and body of research that bridges the gap between environmentalism and feminism, and it draws parallels between the subjugation and social constructed hierarchies around women and nature. Ecofeminism asserts that all systems of oppression are connected and must be critiqued in totality for effective activism. This work aligns closely with the views of <a href="http://www.bsos.umd.edu/socy/people/pcollins.html" target="_blank">Patricia Hill Collins</a> on the links between gender and race. However, as an umbrella term, the research encompasses many subcategories of ecofeminist action and theories, including, but not limited to, vegan ecofeminism, Christian ecofeminism, separatism, Goddess worship, and socialist ecofeminism.</p>
<p>The most well-known positions within ecofeminism are materialist and essentialist. Materialists connect the division of labor, power, and property as the key origin of continuous domination of women and nature, and the contradiction in value behind production (capitalist market) and reproduction (domestic work force). Their school of thought states dichotomies must be deconstructed for equality to be a reality. Essentialist thought states there is an inherent connection between women and nature that is encouraged by biological factors and a shared history of oppression. Essentialist ecofeminism has been criticized for what some feminists consider a reliance on mysticism that reiterates patriarchal views on dichotomies and the feminization of nature. Andy Smith, who wrote the essay<a href="http://www.thepeoplespaths.net/articles/formlife.htm" target="_blank"> “For All Those Who Were Indian in a Former Life,”</a>  criticized some ecofeminists for appropriating indigenous activism and rituals, since some indigenous groups state their work is based on a spiritual connection to nature founded within their cultural heritage – not environmentalism.</p>
<p>Although the movement and theories have limitations, it would be one way to connect the growing interest in connected environmental concerns and global feminist action. Initially, due to focus on women and inherent connections to nature, the role of men in activism was limited and alienating.   <a href="www.lespantheresroses.org/textes/ecology_toward_a_queer_ecofeminism.pdf" target="_blank">Greta Gaard&#8217;s research</a> on queer feminism would allow an inclusive approach to addressing oppressive systems “by interrogating social constructions of the &#8216;natural&#8217;.” Although the research was released in 1997, the theoretical intersections with queer theory has given the material a new platform for discussion as the study of queer theory expands on a university level.</p>
<p>As the movement continues to expand and grow it will grapple with issues that arise from essentialist views, religious tradition, and class privilege, however ecofeminism is capable of inciting academic research and grassroots actions toward an awareness of economic inequality, sustainability issues, environmental welfare, and animal rights on a global scale. In this new scenario, all members of society can work toward reshaping cultures and the value of place and person.
</p>
