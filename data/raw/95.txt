<p>A <b>student</b> or <b>pupil</b> is a learner or someone who attends an educational institution. In Britain those attending <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> are termed "students". In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and more recently also in Britain, the term "student" is applied to both categories. In its widest use, <i>student</i> is used for anyone who is <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a>, including mid-career adults who are taking <a href="/wiki/Vocational_education" title="Vocational education">vocational education</a> or returning to university. When speaking about learning outside an institution, "student" is also used to refer to someone who is learning a topic or who is "a student of" a certain topic or person.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nigeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Nigeria, education is classified into four system known as 6-3-3-4 system of education. It implies six years in primary school, three years in junior secondary, three years in senior secondary and four years in the university. However, the number of years to be spent in university is mostly determined by the course of study. Some courses have longer study length than others. Those in primary school are often referred to as pupils. Those in university as well as those in secondary school are being referred to as students.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore">Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Six years of primary school education in Singapore is compulsory.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Primary School (Primary 1 to 6)</li>
<li>Secondary School ( Secondary 1 to 4 or 5)</li>
<li>Junior College (Junior College 1 to 2 - Optional)</li>
</ul>
<p>There are also schools which have the <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Programme" title="Integrated Programme">integrated programme</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/River_Valley_High_School_(Singapore)" class="mw-redirect" title="River Valley High School (Singapore)">River Valley High School (Singapore)</a>, which mean they stay in the same school from Secondary 1 to Junior College 2, without having to take the "O" level examinations which most students take at the end of Secondary school.</p>
<p>International Schools are subject to overseas curriculums, such as the British, American, Canadian or Australian Boards.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bangladesh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Primary education is compulsory in Bangladesh. It's a near crime to not to send children to primary school when they are of age. But it is not a punishable crime(sending children to work instead of school is a crime). Because of the socio-economic state of Bangladesh, child labour is sometimes legal. But the guardian must ensure the primary education. Everyone who is learning in any institute or even online may be called student in Bangladesh. Sometimes students taking undergraduate education is called undergraduates and students taking post-graduate education may be called post-graduates.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brunei">Brunei</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brunei">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Education is free in Brunei. Darussalam not limited to government educational institutions but also private educational institutions. There are mainly two types of educational institutions: government or public, and private institutions. Several stages have to be undergone by the prospective students leading to higher qualifications, such as <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_Degree" class="mw-redirect" title="Bachelor's Degree">Bachelor's Degree</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>Primary School (Year 1 to 6)</li>
<li>Secondary School (Year 7 to 11)</li>
<li>High School [or also known as the Sixth Form Centers] (Year 12 to 13)</li>
<li>Colleges (Pre-University to Diploma)</li>
<li>University Level (Undergraduate, Postgraduate and Professional)</li>
</ul>
<p>It takes six and five years to complete the primary and secondary levels respectively. Upon completing these two crucial stages, students/pupils have freedom to progress to sixth-form centers, colleges or probably straight to employment. Students are permitted to progress towards university level programs in both government and private university colleges.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Education in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> is free for all the students who study in Primary School, Secondary School or High School.</p>
<ul>
<li>Primary School (Grade 1 to 6)</li>
<li>Secondary School (Grade 7 to 9)</li>
<li>High School (Grade 10 to 12)</li>
<li>College (Year 1 to 3 )</li>
<li>University (Year 1 to 4 or 5)</li>
</ul>
<p>After basic education, students can opt to take a bachelor's (undergraduate) degree at a higher education institution (i.e. a college or university), which normally lasts for four years though the length of some courses may be longer or shorter depending on the institution.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> 12-year school is categorized in three stages: Primary school, Secondary school and Higher Secondary school. For college it averages 4 years for bachelor's degree (except MBBS which is 5 and half years programme) and 2 years master's degree.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistan">Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Most bachelor's degrees span over four years, followed by 2 years master's degree.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Philippines is currently in the midst of a transition to a K-12 (also called K+12) basic education system.<sup id="cite_ref-GovPH_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GovPH-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DepEdPH_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DepEdPH-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SenatePH_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SenatePH-5">[5]</a></sup> Education ideally begins with one year of kinder. Once the transition is complete, elementary or grade school comprises grades 1 to 6. Although the term <i>student</i> may refer to learners of any age or level, the term 'pupil' is used by the Department of Education to refer to learners in the elementary level, particularly in public schools. Secondary level or high school comprises two major divisions: grades 7 to 10 will be collectively referred to as 'junior high school', whereas grades 11 to 12 will be collectively referred to as 'senior high school'. The Department of Education refers to learners in grade 7 and above as <i>students</i>.</p>
<p>After basic education, students can opt to take a bachelor's (undergraduate) degree at a higher education institution (i.e. a college or university), which normally lasts for four years though the length of some courses may be longer or shorter depending on the institution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In Iran 12-year school is categorised in 2 stages: Elementary school and High school. It takes 6 years for a student to graduate from elementary school and 5 years for high school and 1 year for pre-university college( independed on your field) . After graduating from high school(or pre-university if needed), students acquire a diploma. <p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, after preschool, which is not compulsory, children begin primary school, usually after their fifth birthday. In <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> the first year is called 'kindy' (kindergarten), in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> 'prep' (preparatory school), and in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> 'reception'; students then continue on to 'grade one' through to 'grade six', before 2014, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, South Australia and <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, primary school continued on to 'grade seven'. Year seven/eight is generally referred to as the junior school, nine/ten is referred to as 'middle school' and year ten/eleven through to twelve as 'senior school'. After year twelve, students may pursue tertiary education at university or TAFE (<a href="/wiki/Technical_and_further_education" title="Technical and further education">technical and further education</a>). <p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, after kindergarten or pre-school, which is attended from ages three to five, children begin primary school, 'Year One', at five years of age. Then Year Seven and Year Eight are Intermediate, and from Year Nine until Year Thirteen, a student would attend a secondary school or a college. Primary School children are known as Pupils and Intermediate/Secondary School children are known as Students.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>In Finland a student is called "opiskelija" (plural being 'opiskelijat'), though children in compulsory education are called "oppilas" (plural being 'oppilaat'). First level of education is "esikoulu" (literally 'preschool'), which is optional. Children attend esikoulu the year they turn six, and next year they start attending "peruskoulu" (literally "basic school", corresponds to American elementary school, middle school and junior high), which is compulsory. Peruskoulu is divided to "alakoulu" (years 1 through 6) and "yläkoulu" (years 7 through 9). After compulsory education most children attend second level education (toisen asteen koulutus), either lukio (corresponds to high school) or ammattikoulu (Vocational School), at which point they are called students (opiskelija). Some attend "kymppiluokka", which is a retake on some yläkoulu's education.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>To attend ammattikorkeakoulu (University of applied sciences) or a university a student must have a second level education. The recommended graduation time is five years. First year students are called "fuksi" and students that have studied more than five years are called "N:nnen vuoden opiskelija" (Nth year student).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Germany, the German cognate term <i>Student</i> (male) or "Studentin" (female) is reserved for those attending a university. University students in their first year are colloquially called <i>Ersties</i> ("firsties"). Different terms for school students exist, depending on which kind of school is attended by the student. The general term for a person going to school is <i>Schüler</i> or <i>Schülerin</i>. Students attending a university preparatory school are called <i>Gymnasiasten</i>, while those attending other schools are called <i>Hauptschüler</i> or <i>Realschüler</i>. Students who graduate with the <a href="/wiki/Abitur" title="Abitur">Abitur</a> are called <i>Abiturienten</i>. The abbreviation stud. + the abbreviation of the faculty p. e. phil. for <i>philosophiae</i> is a post-nominal for all students of a baccalaureus course. The abbreviation cand. for <i>candidatus</i> + the abbreviation of the faculty is given as a post-nominal to those close to the final exams. <i>First name surname</i>, stud. phil. or <i>First name surname</i>, cand. jur.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, pupils officially start with primary school which consists of eight years: junior infants, senior infants, first class to sixth class (ages 5–11). After primary school, pupils proceed to the secondary school level. Here they first enter the junior cycle, which consists of first year to third year (ages 12–15). At the end of third year, all students must sit a compulsory state examination called the <a href="/wiki/Junior_Certificate" title="Junior Certificate">Junior Certificate</a>. After third year, pupils have the option of taking a "transition year" or fourth year (usually at age 15-16). In transition year pupils take a break from regular studies to pursue other activities that help to promote their personal, social, vocational and educational development, and to prepares them for their role as autonomous, participative and responsible members of society. It also provides a bridge to enable pupils to make the transition from the more dependent type of learning associated with the Junior Cert. to the more independent learning environment associated with the senior cycle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After the junior cycle pupils advance to the senior cycle, which consists of fifth year and sixth year (usually ages between 16 and 18). At the end of the sixth year a final state examination is required to be sat by all pupils, known as the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Leaving_Certificate" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Leaving Certificate">Leaving Certificate</a>. The Leaving Cert. is the basis for all Irish pupils who wish to do so to advance to higher education via a points system. A maximum of 600 points can be achieved. All higher education courses have a minimum of points needed for admission.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Dublin" title="Trinity College, Dublin">Trinity College, Dublin</a> under-graduate students are formally called "junior freshmen", "senior freshmen", "junior sophister" or "senior sophister", according to the year they have reached in the typical four year degree course. <i>Sophister</i> is another term for a <a href="/wiki/Sophomore_year" class="mw-redirect" title="Sophomore year">sophomore</a>, though the term is rarely used in other institutions and is largely limited to Trinity College Dublin.</p>
<p>At university the term "fresher" is used to describe new students who are just beginning their first year. The term, "first year" is the more commonly used and connotation-free term for students in their first year. The week at the start of a new year is called "<a href="/wiki/Orientation_week" class="mw-redirect" title="Orientation week">Freshers' Week</a>" or "Welcome Week", with a programme of special events to welcome new students. An undergraduate in the last year of study before graduation is generally known as a "finalist."</p>

<p>In Italian, a <i>matricola</i> is a first-year student. Some other terms may apply in specific schools, some depending on the <a href="/wiki/Liceo_classico" title="Liceo classico">liceo classico</a> or <a href="/wiki/Liceo_scientifico" title="Liceo scientifico">liceo scientifico</a> attended.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Goliardia" title="Goliardia">goliardic</a> initiation traditions the grades granted (following approximately the year of enrollment at university) are: <i>matricola</i> (freshman), <i>fagiolo</i> (sophomore), <i>colonna</i> (junior), and <i>anziano</i> (senior), but most of the distinctions are rarely used outside Goliardia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Sweden, only those studying at university level are called students (<i>student</i>, plural <i>studenter</i>). To graduate from upper secondary school (<i>gymnasium</i>) is called <i>ta studenten</i> (literally "to take the student"), but after the graduation festivities, the graduate is no longer a student unless he or she enrolls at university-level education. At lower levels, the word <i>elev</i> (plural <i>elever</i>) is used. As a general term for all stages of education, the word <i>studerande</i> (plural also <i>studerande</i>) is used, meaning 'studying [person]'.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Europa-Institut_graduation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Europa-Institut_graduation.jpg/220px-Europa-Institut_graduation.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Europa-Institut_graduation.jpg/330px-Europa-Institut_graduation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Europa-Institut_graduation.jpg/440px-Europa-Institut_graduation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="604" data-file-height="401" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Europa-Institut_graduation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The new graduates of the <a href="/wiki/Europa-Institut" title="Europa-Institut">Europa-Institut</a> in Germany gather to throw their mortar boards in the air as part of a graduation ceremony</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Traditionally, the term "student" is reserved for people studying at university level in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.</p>
<p>At universities in the UK, the term "fresher" is used informally to describe new students who are just beginning their first year. Although it is not unusual to call someone a fresher after their first few weeks at university, they are typically referred to as "first years" or "first year students".</p>
<p>The ancient Scottish <a href="/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews" title="University of St Andrews">University of St Andrews</a> uses the terms "bejant" for a first year (from the French "<i>bec-jaune</i>"&#160;– "yellow beak", "fledgling"). Second years are called "semi-bejants", third years are known as "tertians", and fourth years, or others in their final year of study, are called "magistrands".</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, primary school begins with an optional "nursery" year followed by reception and then move on to "year one, year two" and so on until "year six". In state schools, children join secondary school when they are 11–12 years old in what used to be called "first form" and is now known as "year 7". They go up to year 11 (formerly "fifth form") and then join the sixth form, either at the same school or at a separate <a href="/wiki/Sixth_form_college" title="Sixth form college">sixth form college</a>. A pupil entering a private, fee-paying school (usually at age 13) would join the "third form" — equivalent to year 9. Many schools have an alternate name for first years, some with a derogatory basis, but in others acting merely as a description — for example "shells" (non-derogatory) or "grubs" (derogatory).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and Scotland, it is very similar but with some differences. Pupils start off in nursery or reception aged 3 to 4, and then start primary school in "P1" (P standing for primary) or year 1. They then continue primary school until "P7" or year 7. After that they start secondary school at 11 years old, this is called "1st year" or year 8 in Northern Ireland, or "S1" in Scotland. They continue secondary school until the age of 16 at "5th year", year 12 or "S5", and then it is the choice of the individual pupil to decide to continue in school and (in Northern Ireland) do AS levels (known as "lower sixth") and then the next year to do A levels (known as "upper sixth"). In Scotland, students aged 16–18 take Highers, followed by Advanced Highers. Alternatively, pupils can leave and go into full-time employment or to start in a technical college.</p>
<p>Large increases in the size of student populations in the UK and the effect this has had on some university towns or on areas of cities located near universities have become a concern in the UK since 2000. A report by Universities UK, "<i>Studentification: A Guide to Opportunities, Challenges and Practice</i>" (2006) has explored the subject and made various recommendations.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A particular problem in many locations is seen as the impact of students on the availability, quality and price of rented and owner-occupied property.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Canada" title="Education in Canada">Education in Canada</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Work_in_the_computer_lab.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Work_in_the_computer_lab.JPG/220px-Work_in_the_computer_lab.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Work_in_the_computer_lab.JPG/330px-Work_in_the_computer_lab.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Work_in_the_computer_lab.JPG/440px-Work_in_the_computer_lab.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Work_in_the_computer_lab.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Students of the <i>Cégep de St-Hyacinthe</i> in Quebec working in a computer lab</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Education in Canada is within the constitutional jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">provinces</a>, and the overall curriculum is overseen by the provincial governments. As there is no overall national coordinating authority, the way the educational stages are grouped and named differs from region to region. Education is generally divided into primary education, followed by secondary education, and post-secondary education. Primary and secondary education are generally divided into numbered grades from 1 to 12, although the first grade may be preceded by <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">kindergarten</a> (optional in many provinces). Ontario and Quebec offer a <a href="/wiki/Pre-kindergarten" title="Pre-kindergarten">pre-kindergarten</a>, called a "junior kindergarten" in Ontario, and a "garderie" in Quebec.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Education_in_Ontario" title="Education in Ontario">Education in Ontario</a> once involved an <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Academic_Credit" title="Ontario Academic Credit">Ontario Academic Credit</a> (OAC) as university preparation, but that was phased out in 2007, and now all provinces except Quebec have 12 grades. The OAC was informally known as "grade 13" and the name was also used to refer to the students who took it.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Education_in_Quebec" title="Education in Quebec">Education in Quebec</a> differs from the other provinces in that it has an école primaire (literally "primary school") consisting of grades 1-6, and an école secondaire (literally "secondary school") consisting of secondaries I-V. Secondaries I-V are equivalent to grades 7-11. A student graduating from high school (grade 11) can then either complete a three-year <a href="/wiki/College_education_in_Quebec" title="College education in Quebec">college</a> program or attend a two-year pre-university program required before attending university. In some English High Schools, as well as in most French schools, high school students will refer to secondary 1-5 as year one through five. So if someone in Secondary three is asked "what grade/year are you in?" they will reply "three" or "sec 3". It is presumed that the person asking the question knows that they are not referring to "Grade 3" but rather "Secondary 3". This can be confusing for those outside of Quebec.</p>
<p>In some provinces, grades 1 through 6 are called "elementary school", grades 6 to 8 are called "middle school" or "junior high school", and grades 9 to 12 are considered high school. Other provinces, such as British Columbia, mainly divide schooling into elementary school (Kindergarten to grade 7) and secondary school (grades 8 through 12). In Alberta and Nova Scotia, elementary consists of kindergarten through grade 6. Junior high consists of Grades 7-9. High school consists of Grades 10-12. In English provinces, the high school (known as <i>academy</i> or <i>secondary school</i>) years can be referred to simply as <i>first, second, third and fourth year</i>. Some areas call it by grade such as grade 10, grade 11 and grade 12.</p>
<p>The difference between college and university is significantly different from in the United States or even the United Kingdom. A Canadian <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a> is more similar to an American community college but also the British, French and other European and British Commonwealth such as Australian and New Zealand etc., on the other hand. In contrast, a Canadian university is also quite comparable to an American university as well as many other universities among the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking world</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Francosphere">Francosphere</a>. In Canada, colleges are generally geared for individuals seeking applied careers, while universities are geared for individuals seeking more academic careers.</p>
<p>University students are generally classified as first, second, third or fourth-year students, and the American system of classifying them as "freshmen", "sophomores", "juniors" and "seniors" is seldom used or even understood in Canada. In some occasions, they can be called "senior ones", "twos", "threes" and "fours".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, the first official year of schooling is called <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">kindergarten</a>, which is why the students are called kindergarteners. Kindergarten is optional in most states, but few students skip this level. Pre-kindergarten, also known as "<a href="/wiki/Preschool" title="Preschool">preschool</a>" (and sometimes shortened to "Pre-K") is becoming a standard of education as academic expectations for the youngest students continue to rise. Many public schools offer pre-kindergarten programs.</p>
<p>In the United States there are 12 years of mandatory schooling. The first eight are solely referred to by numbers (e.g. 1st grade, 5th grade) so students may be referred to as 1st graders, 5th graders, then once in middle school before high school you are ratio referred to as 6th, 7th, 8th graders. Upon entering high school, grades 9 through 12 (<a href="/wiki/High_school" class="mw-redirect" title="High school">high school</a>) also have alternate names for students, namely freshman, sophomore, junior and senior. The actual divisions of which grade levels belong to which division (whether elementary, middle, junior high or high school) is a matter decided by state or local jurisdictions.</p>
<p>Accordingly, college students are often called Freshmen, Sophomores, Juniors and Seniors (respectively), unless their undergraduate program calls for more than the traditional 4 years.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_year">First year</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: First year">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first year of college or high school is referred to as Freshman year. A <a href="/wiki/Freshman" title="Freshman">freshman</a> (slang alternatives that are usually derogatory in nature include "fish", "new-g", "fresher", "frosh", "newbie", "freshie", "snotter", "fresh-meat", "skippie", etc.) is a first-year student in <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a>, <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> or <a href="/wiki/High_school" class="mw-redirect" title="High school">high school</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_year">Second year</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Second year">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the U.S., a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sophomore" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:sophomore">sophomore</a>, also called a "soph," is a second-year student. Outside the United States, the term Sophomore is rarely used, with second-year students simply called "second years". <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">Folk etymology</a> indicates that the word means "<a href="/wiki/Wise_fool" title="Wise fool">wise fool</a>"; consequently "sophomoric" means "pretentious, bombastic, inflated in style or manner; immature, crude, superficial" (according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>). It is widely assumed to be formed from Greek "<i>sophos</i>", meaning "wise", and "<i>moros</i>" meaning "foolish", although the etymology suggests an origin from the now-defunct "sophumer", an obsolete variant of "<a href="/wiki/Sophism" title="Sophism">sophism</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-second_year">Post-second year</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Post-second year">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Teamsolvingpuzzle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Teamsolvingpuzzle.jpg/220px-Teamsolvingpuzzle.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Teamsolvingpuzzle.jpg/330px-Teamsolvingpuzzle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Teamsolvingpuzzle.jpg/440px-Teamsolvingpuzzle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Teamsolvingpuzzle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Students from the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the U.S., a Junior is a student in the penultimate (usually third) year and a Senior is a student in the last (usually fourth) year of <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a>, <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a>, or <a href="/wiki/High_school" class="mw-redirect" title="High school">high school</a>. A student who takes more than the normal number of years to graduate is sometimes referred to as a "<a href="/wiki/Super_senior" title="Super senior">super senior</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This term is often used in college, but can be used in high school as well. The term <a href="/wiki/Underclassman" title="Underclassman">Underclassman</a> is used to refer collectively to Freshmen and Sophomores, and Upperclassman to refer collectively to Juniors and Seniors, sometimes even Sophomores. The term Middler is used to describe a third-year student of a school (generally <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a>) that offers five years of study. In this situation, the fourth and fifth years would be referred to as Junior and Senior years, respectively, and the first two years would be the Freshman and Sophomore years.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Graduate_Students">Graduate Students</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Graduate Students">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A graduate student is a student who continues his/her education after graduation. Some examples of graduate programs are: <a href="/wiki/Business_school" title="Business school">business school</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law_school" title="Law school">law school</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medical_school" title="Medical school">medical school</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Veterinary school">veterinary school</a>. Degrees earned in graduate programs include the <a href="/wiki/Master%E2%80%99s_degree" class="mw-redirect" title="Master’s degree">Master’s degree</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">research doctoral</a> degree, or a <a href="/wiki/First_professional_degree" class="mw-redirect" title="First professional degree">first professional degree</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocational_School">Vocational School</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Vocational School">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Students attending <a href="/wiki/Vocational_school" title="Vocational school">vocational school</a> focus on their jobs and learning how to work in specific fields of work. A vocational program typically takes much less time to complete than a four-year degree program, lasting 12–24 months.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Liberal Arts that are required in four-year Universities are less important to these students because the skills necessary for their careers take precedence in order for a timely completion of the program.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Student_politics" class="mw-redirect" title="Student politics">Student politics</a></div>
<p>Students have their own current of politics and activism on and off campus. The <a href="/wiki/Student_rights_in_higher_education" title="Student rights in higher education">student rights</a> movement has centered itself on the empowerment of students similar to the labor movement.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mature_students">Mature students</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Mature students">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Adult_learner" title="Adult learner">Adult learner</a></div>
<p>A mature, non-traditional, or adult student in <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_education" title="Tertiary education">tertiary education</a> (at a university or a college) is normally classified as an (undergraduate) student who is at least 21–23 years old at the start of their <a href="/wiki/Course_of_study" class="mw-redirect" title="Course of study">course</a> and usually having been out of the education system for at least two years. Mature students can also include students who have been out of the education system for decades, or students with no secondary education. Mature students also make up graduate and <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Postgraduate">postgraduate</a> populations by demographic of age.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_pranks">Student pranks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Student pranks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Student_prank" title="Student prank">Student prank</a></div>
<p>University students have been associated with pranks and japes since the creation of universities in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> These can often involve petty crime, such as the theft of <a href="/wiki/Traffic_cone" title="Traffic cone">traffic cones</a> and other public property,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> or hoaxes. It is also not uncommon for students from one school to steal or deface the <a href="/wiki/Mascot" title="Mascot">mascot</a> of a rival school.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In fact, pranks play such a significant part in student culture that numerous books have been published that focus on the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_terms">Other terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Other terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Students who are <a href="/wiki/Grade_retention" title="Grade retention">repeating a grade level of schooling due to poor grades</a> are sometimes referred to as having been "held back" or "kept back". In Singapore they are described as "retained". In the Philippines they are called "repeater".</li>
<li>The term 'pupil' (originally a Latin term for a minor as the ward of an adult guardian, etc.) is used in some Commonwealth <a href="/wiki/Primary_school" title="Primary school">primary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">secondary</a> schools (particularly in England and Wales) instead of "student", but once attending further education (at a <a href="/wiki/Sixth-form" class="mw-redirect" title="Sixth-form">sixth-form</a> college) or higher education (at <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> for example), the term "student" is standard. The term <i>pupil</i> is also used in the Philippines by the Department of Education to refer to learners currently in elementary school; the term <i>student</i> is used for by the Department of Education for learners in high school.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_military_academies" class="mw-redirect" title="United States military academies">United States military academies</a> officially use only numerical terms, but there are colloquial expressions used in everyday speech. In order from first year to fourth year, students are referred to as "fourth-class", "third-class", "second-class", and "first-class" cadets or midshipmen. Unofficially, other terms are used, for example at the United States Military Academy, freshmen are called "plebes", sophomores are called "yearlings" or "yuks", juniors are called "cows", and seniors are called "firsties". Some universities also use numerical terms to identify classes; students enter as "first-years" and graduate as "fourth-years" (or, in some cases, "fifth-years", "sixth-years", etc.).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Idiomatic_use">Idiomatic use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Idiomatic use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Freshman" title="Freshman">Freshman</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Sophomore_year" class="mw-redirect" title="Sophomore year">sophomore</a>" are sometimes used <a href="/wiki/Literal_and_figurative_language" title="Literal and figurative language">figuratively</a>, almost exclusively in the United States, to refer to a first or second effort ("the singer's <i>sophomore</i> <a href="/wiki/Album" title="Album">album</a>"), or to a <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a>'s first or second term in office ("freshman senator") or an <a href="/wiki/Sportsperson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sportsperson">athlete</a>'s first or second year on a <a href="/wiki/Professional_sports_team" class="mw-redirect" title="Professional sports team">professional sports team</a>. "Junior" and "senior" are not used in this figurative way to refer to third and fourth years or efforts, because of those words' broader meanings of "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/younger" class="extiw" title="wikt:younger">younger</a>" and "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/older" class="extiw" title="wikt:older">older</a>." A <a href="/wiki/Junior_senator" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior senator">junior senator</a> is therefore not one who is in a third term of office, but merely one who has not been in the Senate as long as the other senator from their <a href="/wiki/State_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="State (United States)">state</a>. Confusingly, this means that it is possible to be both a "freshman Senator" and a "senior Senator" simultaneously: for example, if a Senator wins election in 2008, and then the other Senator from the same state steps down and a new Senator elected in 2010, the former Senator is both senior Senator (as in the Senate for two years more) and a freshman Senator (since still in the first term).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Students.27_Day">International Students' Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Student&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: International Students' Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/International_Students%27_Day" title="International Students' Day">International Students' Day</a> remembers the anniversary of the 1939 Nazi storming of the <a href="/wiki/Charles_University_in_Prague" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles University in Prague">University of Prague</a> after student demonstrations against the <a href="/wiki/German_occupation_of_Czechoslovakia" title="German occupation of Czechoslovakia">German occupation of Czechoslovakia</a>. Germans closed all Czech universities and colleges, sent over 1200 students to Nazi concentration camps, and had nine student leaders executed (on November 17).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
