					 </div>		<p><strong>Organic Consumers Association</strong> (OCA) led by Ronnie Cummins is listed by the USDA as an &#8220;organic agricultural products marketing and trade resource&#8221; organization.<a id="ref_1_text" href="#ref_1">[1]</a> OCA is one of the most aggressive of North America’s anti-GMO activist groups. It has contributed <a href="http://usrtk.org/donors/" target="_blank">$274.500 to start up and fund the US Right to Know </a>organization, which is targeting scientists and science communicators with controversial Freedom of Information requests in an attempt to tie them to the biotech industry.</p>
<p><strong>See Biotech Gallery: <a href="https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/glp-facts/us-right-to-know/" target="_blank">USRTK</a></strong></p>
<p>Its original name was the “Pure Food Campaign” (in 1988) when anti-technology futurist Jeremy Rifkin at the Foundation on Economic Trends helped launch the initiative as a spin off of their &#8220;Beyond Beef&#8221; initiative with Andrew Kimbrell.</p>
<p>Kimbrell and Cummins spun off their campaign into a new organization called the International Center for Technology Assessment, from which the Pure Food Campaign was later spun off to become the Organic Consumers Association. One of OCA’s stated goals is to achieve 100 percent organic agriculture in the next 50 years<a id="ref_2_text" href="#ref_2">[2]</a> and the group tackles a range of issues which disparage conventional production (including conventional pesticide use, animal welfare and corporate control of farming), its major area of focus for the past 10-plus years has been GMOs.</p>
<h2 id="toc_history">HISTORY</h2>
<p><a href="https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/cummins.jpg" target="_blank" rel="lightbox[279824]" title="cummins"><img class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-279844" src="https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/cummins-300x227.jpg" alt="cummins" width="300" height="227" srcset="https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/cummins-300x227.jpg 300w, https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/cummins.jpg 400w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a>OCA founder Ronnie Cummins got his start campaigning against animal agriculture with Andrew Kimbrell at the Foundation on Economic Trends, then later as part of the <a href="https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/2014/09/29/gmo-farming-abject-failure-claims-center-for-food-safety-is-it/" target="_blank">Center for Food Safety</a>. OCA was formally recognized as a separate 501c3 entity by the IRS in 1998. They added the 501c4 campaign organization Organic Consumers Fund (OCF) in 2002 to engage in political lobbying and ballot initiative campaigns.</p>
<p>OCA’s claims their membership database to contain between 850,000 and 2,000,000 names and email addresses, and OCA “partners” include at least 3,000 food co-ops and organic retailers. The group cloaks its agenda in the guise of food safety, children’s health, fair trade, and environmental sustainability, but its principal aim is to promote the demand for organic retail products.</p>
<p>To raise money, Cummins has cast Monsanto as villain (along with other biotech companies). OCA has also been critical of large-scale organic marketers including Dean Foods and Aurora Organic Dairy. Its stated reason is that these companies have “corporatized” organic in a way that has strayed from the movement’s social justice mission. That criticism generally reflects the fact that larger organic companies’ have been reluctant to contribute money to his cause. OCA also maintains a 501c4 political lobbying arm.</p>
<p>OCA also funds attack NGOs, such as the U.S. Right to Know (USRTK), a group specifically established, with $47,500 in seed money from OCA, to <a href="http://usrtk.org/gmo/u-s-right-to-know-foias-profs-who-wrote-for-gmo-pr-website/" target="_blank">target biotechnology researchers </a>by using the Freedom of Information Act. <a href="https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/2015/02/11/mimicking-climategate-organic-and-anti-gmo-activists-fund-legal-attack-on-biotech/" target="_blank"><em>Science</em></a> magazine likened this tactic to Climategate, in which global warming deniers attempted to smear climate change scientists by abusing the Freedom of Information Act.</p>

<h2 id="toc_campaignsactivities">CAMPAIGNS / ACTIVITIES</h2>
<p>Via OCA and their OCF lobbying arm this group engages in various grassroots campaigns attacking GMOs, pesticides and other non-organic conventional agriculture practices funded by their organic food and alternative health and supplements industry &#8220;sponsors.&#8221; Their claims range from GMOs are killing bees and butterflies to allegations that consumers can protect themselves against Mad Cow Disease by purchasing organic meat. They oppose the pasteurization of milk and promote the raw milk movement.</p>
<p>They promote their campaign claims via partnership with a range of commercial organic, natural product, herbal supplement and alternative health marketers including <a href="https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/2014/07/28/fbi-turns-up-heat-on-mike-adams-as-health-ranger-fiasco-widens-plus-adams-archive/" target="_blank">Mike Adams</a> Natural News, Joe Mercola&#8217;s Mercola.com and via joint advocacy initiatives with groups like the Weston A. Price Foundation and <a href="https://www.geneticliteracyproject.org/glp-facts/greenpeace/" target="_blank">Greenpeace</a> <a id="ref_6_text" href="#ref_6">[6]</a> and frequently repost stories from &#8220;junk&#8221; sites that attack science journalists or science journalists that have critiqued OCA&#8217;s practices or allies. Their anti-GMO campaigns include the &#8220;GE Food Alert&#8221; partnership with Friends of the Earth, Center for Food Safety, Institute for Agriculture Trade Policy, National Environmental Trust, and various state Public Interest Research Groups<a id="ref_7_text" href="#ref_7">[7]</a></p>
<p>OCA leadership also engages in anti-vaccine campaigns promoting claims that vaccines are dangerous and opposing measures to make vaccinating children mandatory<a id="ref_8_text" href="#ref_8">[8]</a> <a id="ref_9_text" href="#ref_9">[9]</a> and claim there are no long term benefits to vaccines.<a id="ref_10_text" href="#ref_10">[10]</a> Cummins <a href="http://inthesetimes.com/article/6330" target="_blank">calls</a> therapeutic antibiotics in animals &#8220;a slippery slope,” writing that its use would “undermine consumer confidence in organics. It’s the same position [I have] as on human vaccines. They are dangerous, and that’s why I didn’t vaccinate my kid.”</p>
<h3 id="toc_pure-food">Pure Food</h3>
<p>Claiming, &#8220;Organic Consumers Association wants a moratorium on genetically engineered food and encouraging organic food. Stop GMO pollution&#8221; this was the first campaign of OCA launched in 2008 with the PureFood.org website set up by OCA consultant Steve Urow of Green People.<a id="ref_11_text" href="#ref_11">[11]</a></p>
<h3 id="toc_toxic-taters">Toxic Taters</h3>
<p>OCA is one of the Spring 2013 founding members of the Toxic Taters coalition with the <a href="http://www.panna.org" target="_blank">Pesticide Action Network North America</a>, <a href="http://www.foodandwaterwatch.org" target="_blank">Food and Water Watch</a>, Campaign for Food Justice Now, Clean Up the River Environment, Corporate Accountability International, Family Farm Defenders, the Green Party of Minnesota, Headwaters Food Sovereignty Council, Indigenous Environmental Network, the Institute for Agriculture and Trade Policy, Land Stewardship Project, Minnesota Public Interest Research Group (MPIRG), Northwest Center for Alternatives to Pesticides, Real Food Challenge and the White Earth Land Recovery Project.<a id="ref_12_text" href="#ref_12">[12]</a></p>
<h3 id="toc_millions-against-monsanto">Millions Against Monsanto</h3>
<p>OCA’s “<a href="https://www.organicconsumers.org/campaigns/millions-against-monsanto" target="_blank">Millions Against Monsanto</a>” campaign initially never drew more than a few hundred supporters to any single protest, but that didn&#8217;t stop Cummins from branding it in a grandiose fashion. OCA was one of the few members of the coalition that cooked up the StarLink corn scare, and it was through this exercise that they demonstrated the effectiveness of discipline in online grassroots organizing. In 2000 when the National Organic Standards began to take shape, Cummins (armed with money from Aveda founder Horst Rechelbacher) organized thousands of organic food devotees to deluge the USDA with political ultimatums. In a later newsletter, he claimed the USDA had “given in to most of the demands of the organic community.”</p>
<h3 id="toc_safeguard-organic-standards-sos-campaign">Safeguard Organic Standards (SOS) Campaign</h3>
<p>The Safeguard Organic Standards (SOS) campaign focuses on attacking large corporations investing in organic production. OCA claims these commercial players want to lower standards to claim their foods are &#8220;organic&#8221; and can market them accordingly and sell them to an unaware public. The campaign targets various companies, including Horizon and Aurora organic dairy products, such as the brand Silk (soy milk).</p>
<p>OCA claimed, &#8220;In late 2005, despite receiving over 350,000 letters and phone calls from OCA members and the organic community, Republican leaders in Congress attached a rider to the 2006 Agricultural Appropriations Bill to weaken the nation&#8217;s organic food standards in response to pressure from large-scale food manufacturers.&#8221;</p>
