	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom firstpar"><strong><span class="srTitle">Black Panther Party</span></strong><strong>, </strong>original name <span class="alternate">Black Panther Party for Self-Defense</span>,  African American revolutionary party, founded in 1966 in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Oakland-California"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/423454/Oakland">Oakland</span></a>, California, by <a name="ref22217" id="ref22217" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Huey-P-Newton"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/413185/Huey-P-Newton">Huey Newton</span></a> and <a name="ref22218" id="ref22218" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Bobby-Seale"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/530908/Bobby-Seale">Bobby Seale</span></a>. The party’s original purpose was to patrol African American neighbourhoods to protect residents from acts of police brutality. The Panthers eventually developed into a <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Marxism"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/367344/Marxism">Marxist</span></a> revolutionary group that called for the arming of all <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/African-American"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/67474/African-Americans">African Americans</span></a>, the exemption of African Americans from the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/conscription"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/133307/conscription">draft</span></a> and from all sanctions of so-called white America, the release of all African Americans from jail, and the payment of compensation to African Americans for centuries of exploitation by white Americans. At its peak in the late 1960s, Panther membership exceeded 2,000, and the organization operated chapters in several major American cities.</p><!--[END-1ST-PARA]--><div class="other-media data-level="1" id="toc314482"><h2 class="h2">Origin and political program</h2><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Despite passage of the 1960s <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/American-civil-rights-movement"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/119368/American-civil-rights-movement">civil rights</span></a> legislation that followed the landmark<a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Supreme-Court-of-the-United-States"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/574815/Supreme-Court-of-the-United-States"> U.S. Supreme Court</span></a> ruling in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Brown-v-Board-of-Education-of-Topeka"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/81780/Brown-v-Board-of-Education-of-Topeka"><em>Brown</em> v. href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/418612/North-America">North America</span></a> continued to suffer economic and social inequality. <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/poverty"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/473136/poverty">Poverty</span></a> and reduced public services characterized these urban centres, where residents were subject to poor living conditions, joblessness, chronic health problems, violence, and limited means to change their circumstances. Such conditions contributed to urban uprisings in the 1960s (such as those in the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/event/Watts-Riots-of-1965"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1962178/Watts-Riots-of-1965">Watts district of Los Angeles in 1965</span></a>, among others) and to the increased use of police violence as a measure to impose order on cities throughout North America.</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="186728" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/23/166123-004-927C64D5.jpg" alt="The Black Panther Party displaying a banner on the steps of the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, D.C., during the Revolutionary People’s Constitutional Convention in 1970. 	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">It was in this <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="context">context</a>, and in the wake of the assassination of <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Malcolm-X"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/359906/Malcolm-X">Malcolm X</span></a> in 1965, that Merritt Junior College students Huey P. Newton and Bobby Seale founded the Black Panther Party for Self-Defense on October 15, 1966, in West Oakland (officially “Western Oakland,” a district of the city of Oakland), California. Shortening its name to the Black Panther Party, the organization immediately sought to set itself apart from African American cultural nationalist organizations, such as the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Universal-Negro-Improvement-Association"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/618122/Universal-Negro-Improvement-Association-UNIA">Universal Negro Improvement Association</span></a> and the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Nation-of-Islam"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/295614/Nation-of-Islam">Nation of Islam</span></a>, to which it was commonly compared. Although the groups shared certain philosophical positions and tactical features, the Black Panther Party and cultural nationalists differed on a number of basic points. For instance, whereas African American cultural nationalists generally regarded all white people as oppressors, the Black Panther Party distinguished between <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/racism"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/488187/racism">racist</span></a> and nonracist whites and allied themselves with progressive members of the latter group. Also, whereas cultural nationalists generally viewed all African Americans as oppressed, the Black Panther Party believed that African American capitalists and elites could and typically did exploit and oppress others, particularly the African American working class. Perhaps most importantly, whereas cultural nationalists placed considerable emphasis on symbolic systems, such as language and imagery, as the means to liberate African Americans, the Black Panther Party believed that such systems, though important, are ineffective in bringing about liberation. It considered symbols as woefully inadequate to <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ameliorate" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="ameliorate">ameliorate</a> the unjust material conditions, such as joblessness, created by <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/capitalism"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/93927/capitalism">capitalism</span></a>.</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="13214" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/07/3007-004-F3818115.jpg" alt="Huey P. Newton." data-width="167" data-height="248"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Huey P. Newton.</div><div class="credit"><em>Camera Press/© Archive Photos</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li><li><figure><span data-id="201299" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/18/4018-004-82D7520B.jpg" alt="Bobby Seale." data-width="169" data-height="250"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Bobby Seale.</div><div class="credit"><em>Camera Press/© Archive Photos</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li></ul></div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">From the outset, the Black Panther Party outlined a Ten Point Program, not unlike those of the Universal Negro Improvement Association and Nation of Islam, to initiate national African American <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="community">community</a> survival projects and to forge alliances with progressive white radicals and other organizations of people of colour. A number of positions outlined in the Ten Point Program address a principle stance of the Black Panther Party: economic exploitation is at the root of all oppression in the United States and abroad, and the abolition of capitalism is a precondition of social <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/justice" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="justice">justice</a>. In the 1960s this socialist economic outlook, informed by a Marxist political philosophy, <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/resonated" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="resonated">resonated</a> with other social movements in the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/United-States"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/616563/United-States">United States</span></a> and in other parts of the world. Therefore, even as the Black Panther Party found allies both within and beyond the borders of North America, the organization also found itself squarely in the crosshairs of the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Federal-Bureau-of-Investigation"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/203351/Federal-Bureau-of-Investigation-FBI">Federal Bureau of Investigation</span></a> (FBI) and its counterintelligence program, COINTELPRO. In fact, in 1969 FBI director <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/J-Edgar-Hoover"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/271410/J-Edgar-Hoover">J. Edgar Hoover</span></a> considered the Black Panther Party the greatest threat to national security.</p></section><section class="gutter-bottom" data-level="1" id="toc314483"><h2 class="h2">Impact and repression</h2><div class="md-sidebar-news-features md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300">

	</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">The Black Panther Party came into the national spotlight in May 1967 when a small group of its members, led by its chair, Seale, marched fully armed into the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/California-state"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/89503/California">California</span></a> state legislature in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Sacramento-California"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/515399/Sacramento">Sacramento</span></a>. Emboldened by the view that African Americans had a <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutional" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="constitutional">constitutional</a> right to bear arms (based on the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Second-Amendment"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/531357/Second-Amendment">Second Amendment</span></a> of the U.S. Constitution), the Black Panther Party marched on the body as a protest against the pending Mulford Act. The Black Panther Party viewed the legislation, a <a name="ref1203461" id="ref1203461" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/technology/gun-control"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/683775/gun-control">gun control</span></a> bill, as a political maneuver to thwart the organization’s effort to combat police brutality in the Oakland community. The images of gun-toting Black Panthers entering the Capitol were supplemented, later that year, with news of Newton’s arrest after a shoot-out with police in which an officer was killed. With this newfound publicity, the Black Panther Party grew from an Oakland-based organization into an international one with chapters in 48 states in North America and support groups in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Japan"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/300531/Japan">Japan</span></a>, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/China"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/111803/China">China</span></a>, France, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/England"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/700965/England">England</span></a>, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Germany"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/231186/Germany">Germany</span></a>, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Sweden"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/576478/Sweden">Sweden</span></a>, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Mozambique"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/395363/Mozambique">Mozambique</span></a>, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/South-Africa"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/555568/South-Africa">South Africa</span></a>, Zimbabwe, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Uruguay"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/620116/Uruguay">Uruguay</span></a>, and elsewhere.</p><a href="/quiz/world-organizations-fact-or-fiction" class="test-your-knowledge md-content-module-left grid-md-shrink visible-lg visible-md">

	</a><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">In addition to challenging police brutality, the Black Panther Party launched more than 35 Survival Programs and provided community help, such as education, tuberculosis testing, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/legal-aid"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/334839/legal-aid">legal aid</span></a>, transportation assistance, ambulance service, and the manufacture and distribution of free shoes to poor people. Of particular note was the Free Breakfast for Children Program (begun in January 1969) that spread to every major American city with a Black Panther Party chapter. The federal government had introduced a similar pilot program in 1966 but, arguably in response to the Panthers’ <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/initiative" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="initiative">initiative</a>, extended the program and then made it permanent in 1975—undoubtedly to the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/chagrin" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="chagrin">chagrin</a> of Hoover.</p><div class="quiz-list-module md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300">

</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Notwithstanding the social services the Black Panther Party provided, the <a name="ref1203463" id="ref1203463" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Federal-Bureau-of-Investigation"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/203351/Federal-Bureau-of-Investigation-FBI">FBI</span></a> declared the group a <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/communism"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/129104/communism">communist</span></a> organization and an enemy of the U.S. government. Hoover had pledged that 1969 would be the last year of the Black Panther Party and devoted the resources of the FBI, through <a name="ref1203462" id="ref1203462" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/COINTELPRO"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1948536/COINTELPRO">COINTELPRO</span></a>, toward that end. In a protracted program against the Black Panther Party, COINTELPRO used agent provocateurs, sabotage, misinformation, and lethal force to <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/eviscerate" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="eviscerate">eviscerate</a> the national organization. The FBI’s campaign culminated in December 1969 with a five-hour police shoot-out at the Southern California headquarters of the Black Panther Party and an Illinois state police raid in which <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Chicago"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/110319/Chicago">Chicago</span></a> Black Panther leader Fred Hampton was killed. The measures employed by the <a name="ref1203464" id="ref1203464" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Federal-Bureau-of-Investigation"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/203351/Federal-Bureau-of-Investigation-FBI">FBI</span></a> were so extreme that, years later when they were revealed, the director of the agency publicly apologized for “wrongful uses of power.” </p><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">In the early 1970s radical scholar and activist <a name="ref1221906" id="ref1221906" class="idxref"></a><a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Angela-Davis"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/152739/Angela-Davis">Angela Davis</span></a> became widely associated with the Black Panthers, though it seems likely that she never actually became a standing member of the party. Davis did, however, have strong connections with the party and taught political education classes for it. She initially gained notoriety in 1970 when then governor of California <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Ronald-Reagan"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/492882/Ronald-Reagan">Ronald Reagan</span></a> led the Board of Regents in refusing to renew Davis’s appointment as lecturer in philosophy at the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/California-University-of-Pennsylvania"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/89655/California-University-of-Pennsylvania">University of California</span></a>, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Los-Angeles-California"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/348286/Los-Angeles">Los Angeles</span></a>, because of her politics and her association with communists. At about the same time, Davis became involved in the case of three African American inmates at Soledad Prison who had been accused of murdering a guard. She became deeply involved with one of the inmates, George Jackson, whose younger brother’s attempt on <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/August" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="August">August</a> 7, 1970, to win Jackson’s release by taking hostages in the Marin county courthouse went violently awry. Four deaths resulted, and when at least one of the guns proved to be registered to Davis, she fled charges of <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conspiracy" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="conspiracy">conspiracy</a>, kidnapping, and murder, going underground and entering the FBI’s Ten Most Wanted Fugitives list before being captured some eight weeks later after becoming a cause célèbre for the radical Left. Ultimately she was acquitted of all the charges against her by an all-white jury.</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="9848" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/24/7924-004-EC7DF3EB.jpg" alt="Angela Davis, 1974." data-width="347" data-height="300"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Angela Davis, 1974.</div><div class="credit"><em>AP</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li></ul></div><div class="connect md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300 text-center ">

</div><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">From the mid-1970s through the ’80s, the activities of the Black Panther Party all but ceased. Although COINTELPRO contributed to its <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/demise" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="demise">demise</a>, the dissolution of the party’s leadership also contributed to the downfall of the organization. Assata Shakur went into exile in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Cuba"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/145542/Cuba">Cuba</span></a>. Kathleen Cleaver earned a law degree and took an appointment as a professor. After returning from exile in Cuba, Newton was killed in a drug dispute in August 1989, perishing in an alley in West Oakland, not far from where he and Seale had founded the first Black Panther Party chapter. <a class="md-crosslink" href="/biography/Eldridge-Cleaver"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/120887/Eldridge-Cleaver">Eldridge Cleaver</span></a> designed clothes in the 1970s and ’80s before joining the anticommunist <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Unification-Church"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/614516/Unification-Church">Unification Church</span></a> en route to becoming a born-again Christian and a registered member of the Republican Party.</p><div class="other-media gutter-bottom"><ul><li><figure><span data-id="9483" data-type="image" class="media-overlay-link middle-xs center-xs"><div class="image-wrapper"><img src="https://media1.britannica.com/eb-media/56/7556-004-CD5CFEFF.jpg" alt="Eldridge Cleaver (left) and his wife, Kathleen." data-width="397" data-height="300"><em class="material-icons" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></div></span><figcaption class="text-center"><div class="description"><div class="description-text">Eldridge Cleaver (left) and his wife, Kathleen.</div><div class="credit"><em>Camera Press/© Archive Photos</em></div></div></figcaption></figure></li></ul></div></section><section class="gutter-bottom" data-level="1" id="toc314484"><h2 class="h2">Legacy</h2><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">From its founding in 1966, the influence of the Black Panther Party assumed a <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/transnational-social-movement"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1977988/transnational-social-movement">transnational</span></a> character that went beyond the creation of support groups for the organization. Activists in Australian urban centres, for example, incorporated the works of Black Panther Party members into their social movements. The oppressed <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/untouchable"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/618508/untouchable">Dalits</span></a> in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/India"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/285248/India">India</span></a> emulated the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/rhetoric" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="rhetoric">rhetoric</a> of the Black Panthers, and the representatives of the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/National-Liberation-Front-political-organization-Vietnam"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/405034/National-Liberation-Front-NLF">Vietnamese National Liberation Front</span></a>, who called themselves Yellow Panthers, also used the organization as a model. Closer to the United States, the Vanguard Party in the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/The-Bahamas"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/48951/The-Bahamas">Bahamas</span></a> closely studied the Black Panther Party, drew on its political philosophy, adopted its use of uniforms and its Ten Point Program, and published the newspaper <em>Vanguard</em>, whose scope and format mirrored the Black Panther Party’s newspaper, <i>Black Panther</i>, to shape its program of activism.</p><p class="md-content-block gutter-bottom">Even decades after the founding of the organization, the Black Panther Party survived in the public imagination in the United States as a result of the publication of a number of memoirs by its members and the use of its rhetoric in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/art/rap"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/665039/rap">rap</span></a> music. In 1990 Milwaukee Alderman Michael McGee, a former Black Panther Party member, sought to resurrect the organization when he formed the Black Panther Militia in response to the neglect of his community by local politicians and business leaders. The militia inspired other chapters and eventually became the <a name="ref1203465" id="ref1203465" class="idxref"></a>New Black Panther Party, under the leadership of community activist Aaron Michaels. By 1998, Khallid Abdul Muhammad, the former national spokesperson for the Chicago-based <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Nation-of-Islam"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/295614/Nation-of-Islam">Nation of Islam</span></a>, had assumed the de facto leadership of the organization when he led a group of shotgun- and rifle-toting New Black Panther Party members to Jasper, <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/Texas-state"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/589288/Texas">Texas</span></a>, in the wake of the murder of James Byrd, Jr., a 49-year-old African American man who had been dragged behind a pickup truck by three members of the <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Ku-Klux-Klan"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/324086/Ku-Klux-Klan">Ku Klux Klan</span></a>. The New Black Panther Party also became known to the public through the Million Youth March it first organized in <a class="md-crosslink" href="/place/New-York-City"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/412352/New-York-City">New York</span></a> in 1998.</p><div class="trending-topics md-content-module-right grid-xs-ad-300">

At the same time, however, the New Black Panther Party embraced a staunchly cultural nationalist orientation, leading some former Black Panther Party leaders to denounce it for using the Black Panther Party name and for appropriating its <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/legacy" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="legacy">legacy</a>. The <a class="md-crosslink" href="/topic/Southern-Poverty-Law-Center"><span property="about" href="https://semantic.britannica.com/accepted_headword/core/1973249/Southern-Poverty-Law-Center-SPLC">Southern Poverty Law Center</span></a> also emphasized the difference between the two groups and labeled the New Black Panther Party a racist and anti-Semitic hate group. Members of the New Black Panther Party, however, were unapologetic and summarily rejected such condemnation, contending that they only took up the struggle for social justice and freedom that the original Black Panther Party had failed to sustain.</p><span class="bps-article-author"><a class="md-author-link" href="/contributor/Garrett-Albert-Duncan/9507521">Garrett Albert Duncan</a></span><span class="bps-article-author"><a class="md-author-link" href="/editor/The-Editors-of-Encyclopdia-Britannica/4419">The Editors of Encyclopædia Britannica</a></span></section></article>
