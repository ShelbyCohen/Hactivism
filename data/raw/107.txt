<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_women%27s_rights_activists&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thelma_Bate" title="Thelma Bate">Thelma Bate</a> (1904–1984) – community leader, advocate for inclusion of Aboriginals in <a href="/wiki/Country_Women%27s_Association" title="Country Women's Association">Country Women's Association</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sandra_Bloodworth" title="Sandra Bloodworth">Sandra Bloodworth</a> – labour historian, socialist activist, co-founder of Trotskyist Socialist Alternative, editor of <i>Marxist Left Review</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eva_Cox" title="Eva Cox">Eva Cox</a> (born 1938) – sociologist and feminist active in politics and social services, member of Women's Electoral Lobby, social commentator on women in power and at work, and social justice</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zelda_D%27Aprano" title="Zelda D'Aprano">Zelda D'Aprano</a> (born 1928) – trade unionist, feminist, in 1969 chained herself to doors of Commonwealth Building over equal pay.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louisa_Margaret_Dunkley" title="Louisa Margaret Dunkley">Louisa Margaret Dunkley</a> (1866–1927) – telegraphist and labour organizer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Evatt" title="Elizabeth Evatt">Elizabeth Evatt</a> (born 1933) – legal reformist, jurist, critic of Australia's Sex Discrimination Act, first Australian in <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Commission_on_Human_Rights" title="United Nations Commission on Human Rights">United Nations Commission on Human Rights</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Miles_Franklin" title="Miles Franklin">Miles Franklin</a> (1879–1954) – writer and feminist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vida_Goldstein" title="Vida Goldstein">Vida Goldstein</a> (1869–1949) – early Australian feminist campaigning for women's suffrage and social reform, first woman in British Empire to stand for national election</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Germaine_Greer" title="Germaine Greer">Germaine Greer</a> (born 1939) – author of <i>The Female Eunuch</i>, academic and social commentator</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bella_Guerin" title="Bella Guerin">Bella Guerin</a> (1858–1923) – first woman to graduate from an Australian university, Guerin was a socialist feminist prominent (although with periods of public dispute) within the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Australian Labor Party</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louisa_Lawson" title="Louisa Lawson">Louisa Lawson</a> (1848–1920)) – feminist, suffragist, author, founder of <a href="/wiki/The_Dawn_(feminist_magazine)" title="The Dawn (feminist magazine)">The Dawn</a>, and pro-republican federalist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eileen_Powell" title="Eileen Powell">Eileen Powell</a> (1913–1997) – trade unionist, women's activist and contributor to the Equal Pay for Equal Work decision</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Millicent_Preston-Stanley" title="Millicent Preston-Stanley">Millicent Preston-Stanley</a> (1883–1955) – first female member of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Legislative_Assembly" title="New South Wales Legislative Assembly">New South Wales Legislative Assembly</a>, campaigner for custodial rights of mothers in divorce and for women's health care</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Anne_Reid" title="Elizabeth Anne Reid">Elizabeth Anne Reid</a> (born 1942) – world's first women's affairs adviser to head of government (<a href="/wiki/Gough_Whitlam" title="Gough Whitlam">Gough Whitlam</a>), active in UN and on HIV</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bessie_Rischbieth" title="Bessie Rischbieth">Bessie Rischbieth</a> (1874–1967)) – earliest female appointee to any court (honorary, Perth Children's Court, 1915), active against Australian government practice of taking Aboriginal children from their mothers (<a href="/wiki/Stolen_Generation" class="mw-redirect" title="Stolen Generation">Stolen Generation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jessie_Street" title="Jessie Street">Jessie Street</a> (1889–1970) – Australian suffragette, feminist and human rights campaigner influential in labour rights and early days of UN</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anne_Summers" title="Anne Summers">Anne Summers</a> (born 1945) – women's rights activist in politics and media, women's advisor to Labor premier <a href="/wiki/Paul_Keating" title="Paul Keating">Paul Keating</a>, editor of <i>Ms.</i> magazine (NY)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rosie_Batty" title="Rosie Batty">Rosie Batty</a> (born 1962) – 2015 <a href="/wiki/Australian_of_the_Year" title="Australian of the Year">Australian of the Year</a> and family violence campaigner</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fiona_Patten" title="Fiona Patten">Fiona Patten</a> (born 1964) – leader of Australian Sex Party, lobbyist for personal freedoms and progressive lifestyles</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Michelle_Payne" title="Michelle Payne">Michelle Payne</a> (born 1985) – first female winner of <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Cup" title="Melbourne Cup">Melbourne Cup</a> and an advocate of increased presence of women in sport</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://margotfink.com/">Margot Fink</a> (born 1994) – Prominent <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a>IQ activist and nominee for <a href="/wiki/Young_Australian_of_the_Year" class="mw-redirect" title="Young Australian of the Year">Young Australian of the Year</a> (2016)</li>
</ul>

<li><a href="/wiki/Catherine_Winkworth" title="Catherine Winkworth">Catherine Winkworth</a> (1827–1878) – translator and women's rights activist, secretary of the Clifton Association for Higher Education for Women</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malala_Yousafzai" title="Malala Yousafzai">Malala Yousafzai</a> (born 1997) – see Pakistan.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alice_Zimmern" title="Alice Zimmern">Alice Zimmern</a> (1855–1939) – writer and suffragist</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_women%27s_rights_activists&amp;action=edit&amp;section=54" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jane_Addams" title="Jane Addams">Jane Addams</a> (1860–1935) – major social activist, president <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_International_League_for_Peace_and_Freedom" title="Women's International League for Peace and Freedom">Women's International League for Peace and Freedom</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">Susan B. Anthony</a> (1820–1906) – prominent opponent of slavery, played a pivotal role in the 19th-century women's rights movement to introduce women's <a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">suffrage</a> into the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helen_Valeska_Bary" title="Helen Valeska Bary">Helen Valeska Bary</a> (1888–1973) – suffragist, researcher, and social reformer<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alice_Stone_Blackwell" title="Alice Stone Blackwell">Alice Stone Blackwell</a> (1857–1950) – feminist and journalist, editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Woman%27s_Journal" title="Woman's Journal">Woman's Journal</a></i>, a major women's rights publication</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antoinette_Brown_Blackwell" title="Antoinette Brown Blackwell">Antoinette Brown Blackwell</a> (1825–1921) – founded <a href="/wiki/American_Woman_Suffrage_Association" title="American Woman Suffrage Association">American Woman Suffrage Association</a> with <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Stone" title="Lucy Stone">Lucy Stone</a> in 1869</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Henry_Browne_Blackwell" title="Henry Browne Blackwell">Henry Browne Blackwell</a> (1825–1909) – businessman, abolitionist, journalist, suffrage leader and campaigner</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amelia_Bloomer" title="Amelia Bloomer">Amelia Bloomer</a> (1818–1894) – advocate of women's issues, suffragist, publisher and editor of <i>The Lily</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helen_Gurley_Brown" title="Helen Gurley Brown">Helen Gurley Brown</a> (1922–2012) – author of <i><a href="/wiki/Sex_and_the_Single_Girl" title="Sex and the Single Girl">Sex and the Single Girl</a></i>, long-time editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_(magazine)" title="Cosmopolitan (magazine)">Cosmopolitan</a></i>, advocate of women's self-fulfillment</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lucy_Burns" title="Lucy Burns">Lucy Burns</a> (1879–1966) – suffragist and women's rights activist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Ceballos" title="Jacqueline Ceballos">Jacqueline Ceballos</a> – feminist and founder of <a href="/wiki/Veteran_Feminists_of_America" title="Veteran Feminists of America">Veteran Feminists of America</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carrie_Chapman_Catt" title="Carrie Chapman Catt">Carrie Chapman Catt</a> (1859–1947) – suffragist leader, president of <a href="/wiki/National_American_Woman_Suffrage_Association" title="National American Woman Suffrage Association">National American Woman Suffrage Association</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/League_of_Women_Voters" title="League of Women Voters">League of Women Voters</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Alliance_of_Women" title="International Alliance of Women">International Alliance of Women</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Channing" title="William Henry Channing">William Henry Channing</a> (1810–1884) – minister, author</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grace_Julian_Clarke" title="Grace Julian Clarke">Grace Julian Clarke</a> (1865–1938) – suffragist, journalist, author</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frederick_Douglass" title="Frederick Douglass">Frederick Douglass</a> (1818–1895) – abolitionist, writer, speaker</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carol_Downer" title="Carol Downer">Carol Downer</a> (born 1933) – founder of women's self-help movement, feminist, and attorney</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Freeman" title="Elisabeth Freeman">Elisabeth Freeman</a> (1876–1942) – suffragist, civil rights activist, participated in <a href="/wiki/Suffrage_Hikes" title="Suffrage Hikes">Suffrage Hikes</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nancy_Friday" title="Nancy Friday">Nancy Friday</a> (born 1933) – writer and activist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Betty_Friedan" title="Betty Friedan">Betty Friedan</a> (1921–2006) – writer, activist, feminist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Margaret_Fuller" title="Margaret Fuller">Margaret Fuller</a> (1810–1850) – Transcendentalist, advocate of women's education, author of <i><a href="/wiki/Woman_in_the_Nineteenth_Century" title="Woman in the Nineteenth Century">Woman in the Nineteenth Century</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matilda_Joslyn_Gage" title="Matilda Joslyn Gage">Matilda Joslyn Gage</a> (1826–1898) – suffragist, editor, writer, organizer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Lloyd_Garrison" title="William Lloyd Garrison">William Lloyd Garrison</a> (1805–1879) – abolitionist, journalist, organizer, advocate</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ruth_Bader_Ginsburg" title="Ruth Bader Ginsburg">Ruth Bader Ginsburg</a> (born 1933) – academic and lawyer for several women's rights cases before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a>. She herself became a Supreme Court Justice in 1993.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emma_Goldman" title="Emma Goldman">Emma Goldman</a> (1869–1940) – campaigner for birth control and other rights</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Judy_Goldsmith" title="Judy Goldsmith">Judy Goldsmith</a> (born 1938) – feminist activist, President of <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Women" title="National Organization for Women">National Organization for Women</a> (NOW)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helen_M._Gougar" title="Helen M. Gougar">Helen M. Gougar</a> (1843–1907) – lawyer, temperance and women's rights advocate</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sara_Jane_Lippincott" title="Sara Jane Lippincott">Grace Greenwood</a> (1823–1904) – first woman reporter on <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>, advocate of social reform and women's rights</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wentworth_Higginson" title="Thomas Wentworth Higginson">Thomas Wentworth Higginson</a> (1828–1911) – abolitionist, minister, author</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isabella_Beecher_Hooker" title="Isabella Beecher Hooker">Isabella Beecher Hooker</a> (1822–1907) – leader, lecturer and activist in the American Suffragist movement</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Julia_Ward_Howe" title="Julia Ward Howe">Julia Ward Howe</a> (1818–1910) – suffragist, writer, organizer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jane_Hunt" title="Jane Hunt">Jane Hunt</a> (1812–1889) – philanthropist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rosalie_Gardiner_Jones" title="Rosalie Gardiner Jones">Rosalie Gardiner Jones</a> (1883–1978) – suffragist and organizer of the <a href="/wiki/Suffrage_Hikes" title="Suffrage Hikes">Suffrage Hikes</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mary_Livermore" title="Mary Livermore">Mary Livermore</a> (1820–1905) – women's rights journalist, suffragist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kate_Kelly_(feminist)" title="Kate Kelly (feminist)">Kate Kelly</a> (born 1980) – feminist and human rights lawyer, founder of <a href="/wiki/Ordain_Women" title="Ordain Women">Ordain Women</a>, works for <a href="/wiki/Planned_Parenthood" title="Planned Parenthood">Planned Parenthood</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abby_Kelley" title="Abby Kelley">Abby Kelley</a> (1811–1887) – opponent of slavery, women's rights activist, one of the first women to voice views in public speeches</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inez_Milholland" title="Inez Milholland">Inez Milholland</a> (1886–1916) – suffragist, key participant in <a href="/wiki/National_Woman%27s_Party" title="National Woman's Party">National Woman's Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woman_Suffrage_Parade_of_1913" class="mw-redirect" title="Woman Suffrage Parade of 1913">Woman Suffrage Parade of 1913</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robin_Morgan" title="Robin Morgan">Robin Morgan</a> (born 1941) – poet, political theorist, journalist and lecturer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lucretia_Mott" title="Lucretia Mott">Lucretia Mott</a> (1793–1880) – abolitionist, women's rights activist, and social reformer, who helped write <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Sentiments" title="Declaration of Sentiments">Declaration of Sentiments</a> during 1848 <a href="/wiki/Seneca_Falls_Convention" title="Seneca Falls Convention">Seneca Falls Convention</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pauli_Murray" title="Pauli Murray">Pauli Murray</a> (1910–1985) – civil and women's rights activist, lawyer, and Episcopal priest<sup id="cite_ref-test_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-test-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diane_Nash" title="Diane Nash">Diane Nash</a> (born 1938) – Civil Rights Movement leader and organizer, voting rights exponent</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zelda_Kingoff_Nordlinger" title="Zelda Kingoff Nordlinger">Zelda Kingoff Nordlinger</a> (born 1932) – instigator of first rape-reform laws</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maud_Wood_Park" title="Maud Wood Park">Maud Wood Park</a> (1871–1955) – founder <a href="/wiki/College_Equal_Suffrage_League" title="College Equal Suffrage League">College Equal Suffrage League</a>, first president <a href="/wiki/League_of_Women_Voters" title="League of Women Voters">League of Women Voters</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alice_Paul" title="Alice Paul">Alice Paul</a> (1885–1977) – One of the leaders of the 1910s Women's Voting Rights Movement for 19th Amendment, founder of <a href="/wiki/National_Woman%27s_Party" title="National Woman's Party">National Woman's Party</a>, initiator of <a href="/wiki/Silent_Sentinels" title="Silent Sentinels">Silent Sentinels</a> and <a href="/wiki/1913_Women%27s_Suffrage_Parade" class="mw-redirect" title="1913 Women's Suffrage Parade">1913 Women's Suffrage Parade</a>, author of the proposed <a href="/wiki/Equal_Rights_Amendment" title="Equal Rights Amendment">Equal Rights Amendment</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9rique_Petrides" title="Frédérique Petrides">Frédérique Petrides</a> (1903–1983) – see Belgium.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wendell_Phillips" title="Wendell Phillips">Wendell Phillips</a> (1811–1884) – abolitionist, orator, lawyer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Margaret_Sanger" title="Margaret Sanger">Margaret Sanger</a> (1879–1966) – writer, nurse, founder <a href="/wiki/American_Birth_Control_League" title="American Birth Control League">American Birth Control League</a>, founder president of <a href="/wiki/Planned_Parenthood" title="Planned Parenthood">Planned Parenthood</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/May_Wright_Sewall" title="May Wright Sewall">May Wright Sewall</a> (1844–1920) – educator, feminist, president of National Council of Women for the United States, president of the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_of_Women" title="International Council of Women">International Council of Women</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anna_Howard_Shaw" title="Anna Howard Shaw">Anna Howard Shaw</a> (1847–1919) – president of <a href="/wiki/National_Women%27s_Suffrage_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="National Women's Suffrage Association">National Women's Suffrage Association</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Smeal" title="Eleanor Smeal">Eleanor Smeal</a> (born 1939) – organizer, initiator, president of <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Women" title="National Organization for Women">NOW</a>, founder and president of the <a href="/wiki/Feminist_Majority_Foundation" title="Feminist Majority Foundation">Feminist Majority Foundation</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Cady_Stanton" title="Elizabeth Cady Stanton">Elizabeth Cady Stanton</a> (1815–1902) – social activist, abolitionist, suffragist, organizer of <a href="/wiki/1848_Women%27s_Rights_Convention" class="mw-redirect" title="1848 Women's Rights Convention">1848&#160;Women's Rights Convention</a>, co-founder of <a href="/wiki/National_Woman_Suffrage_Association" title="National Woman Suffrage Association">National Woman Suffrage Association</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Council_of_Women" title="International Council of Women">International Council of Women</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gloria_Steinem" title="Gloria Steinem">Gloria Steinem</a> (born 1934) – writer, activist, feminist, women's rights journalist</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Doris_Stevens" title="Doris Stevens">Doris Stevens</a> (1892–1963) – organizer for National American Women Suffrage Association and National Woman's Party, <a href="/wiki/Silent_Sentinels" title="Silent Sentinels">Silent Sentinels</a> participant, author of <i>Jailed for Freedom</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pauline_Agassiz_Shaw" title="Pauline Agassiz Shaw">Pauline Agassiz Shaw</a> (1841–1917) – founder president of <a href="/wiki/Boston_Equal_Suffrage_Association_for_Good_Government" title="Boston Equal Suffrage Association for Good Government">Boston Equal Suffrage Association for Good Government</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lucy_Stone" title="Lucy Stone">Lucy Stone</a> (1818–1893) – orator, one of the initiators of the first <a href="/wiki/National_Women%27s_Rights_Convention" title="National Women's Rights Convention">National Women's Rights Convention</a>, founder of <i><a href="/wiki/Woman%27s_Journal" title="Woman's Journal">Woman's Journal</a></i>, force behind the <a href="/wiki/American_Woman_Suffrage_Association" title="American Woman Suffrage Association">American Woman Suffrage Association</a>, noted for retaining her surname after marriage</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roshini_Thinakaran" title="Roshini Thinakaran">Roshini Thinakaran</a> – film-maker focussing on lives of women in post-conflict zones</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Thompson" title="Dorothy Thompson">Dorothy Thompson</a> (1893–1961) – Buffalo and New York suffragist, later influential journalist and radio broadcaster</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sojourner_Truth" title="Sojourner Truth">Sojourner Truth</a> (c. 1797 – 1883) – <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">abolitionist</a> and women's rights activist and speaker</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mabel_Vernon" title="Mabel Vernon">Mabel Vernon</a> (1883–1975) – suffragist, member of <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Union_for_Women_Suffrage" class="mw-redirect" title="Congressional Union for Women Suffrage">Congressional Union for Women Suffrage</a>, organizer for <a href="/wiki/Silent_Sentinels" title="Silent Sentinels">Silent Sentinels</a></li>
<li>Harry S. Weeks – suffragist, civil rights activist, founder of Wheeling, WV's Democratic-Socialist Union</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ida_B._Wells" title="Ida B. Wells">Ida B. Wells</a> (1862–1931) – civil rights and anti-<a href="/wiki/Lynching" title="Lynching">lynching</a> activist, <a href="/wiki/Suffragist" class="mw-redirect" title="Suffragist">suffragist</a> noted for refusal to avoid media attention as an African American</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frances_Willard_(suffragist)" title="Frances Willard (suffragist)">Frances Willard</a> (1839–1898) – long-time president of the <a href="/wiki/Woman%27s_Christian_Temperance_Union" title="Woman's Christian Temperance Union">Woman's Christian Temperance Union</a>, which, under her leadership, supported women's suffrage</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Victoria_Woodhull" title="Victoria Woodhull">Victoria Woodhull</a> (1838–1927) – suffragist, organizer, first woman to run for U.S. presidency</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rose_O%27Neill" title="Rose O'Neill">Rose O'Neill</a> (1874–1944) famous illustrator (Kewpie creator) who worked for women's right to vote by creating posters and advertising material to promoting the women's movement. Worked with Eleanor Roosevelt.</li>
</ul>
