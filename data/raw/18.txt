<p>The <b>Brights Movement</b> is an international intellectual movement. The members of that movement refer to themselves as "Brights". They hold a <a href="/wiki/Naturalism_(philosophy)" title="Naturalism (philosophy)">naturalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Worldview" class="mw-redirect" title="Worldview">worldview</a>.</p>
<p>Most Brights believe that public policies should be based on science (a body of knowledge obtained and tested by use of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>). Brights are likely to oppose the practice of basing public policies on <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> doctrines. Brights may therefore be described as <a href="/wiki/Secularists" class="mw-redirect" title="Secularists">secularists</a>.</p>
<p>The most politically active Brights frequently and openly advocate <a href="/wiki/Scientocracy" title="Scientocracy">scientocracy</a>, the practice of basing public policies on science. They look forward to living in an era when the best available scientific evidence provides a foundation for the <a href="/wiki/Humane" class="mw-redirect" title="Humane">humane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compassionate" class="mw-redirect" title="Compassionate">compassionate</a> operation of human societies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brights_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bright movement has proposed the following terminology.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>super</b> (noun): someone whose worldview includes supernatural and/or mystical elements.</li>
<li><b>bright</b> (noun): someone whose worldview is naturalistic (no supernatural and mystical elements).</li>
<li><b>Bright</b>: a bright who has registered on the Bright website as a member of the movement.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brights_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Paul Geisert, who coined the term bright and co-founded the bright movement<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a one-time Chicago biology teacher, <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a>, entrepreneur, writer, he co-developed learning materials.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In deciding to attend the "<a href="/wiki/Godless_Americans_March_on_Washington" title="Godless Americans March on Washington">Godless Americans March on Washington</a>" in 2002, Geisert disliked the label "godless" as he saw it as synonymous with evil, he thought this would alienate the public. He sought a new, positive word that might become well-accepted and improve the image of those who didn't believe in the supernatural, in the same way that the term "<a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a>" did. A few weeks later, Geisert came up with the noun "bright", after brainstorming lots of ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He then ran into another room and told his wife "I've got the word, and this is going to be big!"<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was also co-founded by his wife, Mynga Futrell.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Futrell and Geisert remain co-directors of the organization to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After coming up with the term they pitched their idea to friends and decided to unveil their idea at an <a href="/wiki/Atheist_Alliance_International" title="Atheist Alliance International">Atheist Alliance International</a> conference in <a href="/wiki/Tampa" class="mw-redirect" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. They called the organizers and got permission to present the idea.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Geisert and Futrell made their proposal in Spring 2003 at an atheist conference in Florida which was attended by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> They launched the brights' Net website on June 4, 2003. The movement gained early publicity through articles by Richard Dawkins in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-guardian_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-11">[11]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Wired magazine">Wired</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Dennett" title="Daniel Dennett">Daniel Dennett</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dennett_Defense_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennett_Defense-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The movement continued to grow and experienced accelerated registrations following media debate around <a href="/wiki/New_Atheism" title="New Atheism">New Atheism</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> prompted by a series of book releases in late 2006 including <i><a href="/wiki/The_God_Delusion" title="The God Delusion">The God Delusion</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Breaking_the_Spell:_Religion_as_a_Natural_Phenomenon" title="Breaking the Spell: Religion as a Natural Phenomenon">Breaking the Spell</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/God_Is_Not_Great" title="God Is Not Great">God Is Not Great</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_End_of_Faith" title="The End of Faith">The End of Faith</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Letter_to_a_Christian_Nation" title="Letter to a Christian Nation">Letter to a Christian Nation</a></i>. The movement has grown to be a constituency of over 78,000 brights in 204 nations and territories.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A website has been set up by Quebecan Brights titled brightsquebec.org.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brights">Brights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brights_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Brights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many, but not all, brights also identify as <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antitheism" title="Antitheism">antitheist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a> (specifically <a href="/wiki/Secular_humanism" title="Secular humanism">secular humanist</a>), <a href="/wiki/Freethought" title="Freethought">freethinker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Objectivism_(Ayn_Rand)" title="Objectivism (Ayn Rand)">Objectivist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irreligion" title="Irreligion">irreligionist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphysical_naturalism" title="Metaphysical naturalism">naturalist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Physicalism" title="Physicalism">physicalist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnostic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skeptic</a>, or even <a href="/wiki/Naturalistic_pantheism" title="Naturalistic pantheism">naturalistic pantheist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FAQ1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ1-17">[17]</a></sup> Even so, the "movement is not associated with any defined beliefs". The website Brights' Net says its goal is to include the umbrella term <i>bright</i> in the vocabulary of this existing "community of reason".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, "the broader intent is inclusive of the many-varied persons whose worldview is naturalistic" but are in the "general population", as opposed to associating solely with the "community of reason". So persons who can declare their naturalistic worldview using the term <i>bright</i> extend beyond the familiar secularist categories, as long as they do not hold theistic worldviews.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Registrations even include some members of the clergy, such as <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> ministers and a Church History Professor and ordained priest.</p>
<p>Dawkins compares the coining of <i>bright</i> to the "triumph of <a href="/wiki/Consciousness-raising" class="mw-redirect" title="Consciousness-raising">consciousness-raising</a>" from the term <i>gay</i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Gay is succinct, uplifting, positive: an "up" word, where homosexual is a down word, and <a href="/wiki/Queer" title="Queer">queer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faggot_(epithet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Faggot (epithet)">faggot</a> and pooftah are insults. Those of us who subscribe to no religion; those of us whose view of the universe is natural rather than supernatural; those of us who rejoice in the real and scorn the false comfort of the unreal, we need a word of our own, a word like "gay" ... a noun hijacked from an adjective, with its original meaning changed but not too much. Like gay, it should be catchy: a potentially prolific meme. Like gay, it should be positive, warm, cheerful, bright.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Despite the explicit difference between the noun and adjective, there have been comments on the comparison. In his <i>Wired</i> article Dawkins stated, "Whether there is a statistical tendency for brights [noun] to be bright [adjective] is a matter for research."</p>
<p>Notable people who have self-identified as brights at one time or another include: biologists Richard Dawkins and <a href="/wiki/Richard_J._Roberts" title="Richard J. Roberts">Richard J. Roberts</a>; cognitive scientist <a href="/wiki/Steven_Pinker" title="Steven Pinker">Steven Pinker</a>; philosophers Daniel Dennett and <a href="/wiki/Massimo_Pigliucci" title="Massimo Pigliucci">Massimo Pigliucci</a>; stage magicians and <a href="/wiki/Debunker" title="Debunker">debunkers</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Randi" title="James Randi">James Randi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penn_%26_Teller" title="Penn &amp; Teller">Penn &amp; Teller</a>; <a href="/wiki/Amy_Alkon" title="Amy Alkon">Amy Alkon</a>; <a href="/wiki/Sheldon_Lee_Glashow" title="Sheldon Lee Glashow">Sheldon Lee Glashow</a>; <a href="/wiki/Babu_Gogineni" title="Babu Gogineni">Babu Gogineni</a>; <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Kagin" title="Edwin Kagin">Edwin Kagin</a>; <a href="/wiki/Mel_Lipman" title="Mel Lipman">Mel Lipman</a>; <a href="/wiki/Piergiorgio_Odifreddi" title="Piergiorgio Odifreddi">Piergiorgio Odifreddi</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Air_America_Radio" class="mw-redirect" title="Air America Radio">Air America Radio</a> talk show host <a href="/wiki/Lionel_(radio_personality)" title="Lionel (radio personality)">Lionel</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasted_with_.22supers.22">Contrasted with "supers"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brights_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Contrasted with &quot;supers&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Daniel Dennett, in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Breaking_the_Spell:_Religion_as_a_Natural_Phenomenon" title="Breaking the Spell: Religion as a Natural Phenomenon">Breaking the Spell: Religion as a Natural Phenomenon</a></i>, suggests that if non-naturalists are concerned with connotations of the word <i>bright</i>, then they should invent an equally positive sounding word for themselves, like <i>supers</i> (i.e., one whose world view contains supernaturalism).<sup id="cite_ref-Dennett_2006_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennett_2006-20">[20]</a></sup> He also suggested this during his presentation at the Atheist Alliance International '07 convention.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Geisert and Futrell maintain that the <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> has always had a kinship with <a href="/wiki/The_Enlightenment" class="mw-redirect" title="The Enlightenment">the Enlightenment</a>, an era which celebrated the possibilities of science and a certain amount of free inquiry; they have endorsed the use of <i>super</i> as the antonym to <i>bright</i>, although this term makes the assumption that anyone not a "bright" necessarily subscribes to notions of supernaturalism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbol">Symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brights_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Brights' avatar represents a celestial body viewed from space. As there is no up or down or right or left in outer space, the arrangement of planet and darkness and starlight is changeable.<sup id="cite_ref-symbology_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-symbology-23">[23]</a></sup> Although the symbol is open to the viewer's interpretation, it is generally meant to invoke transition and a sense of gradual illumination. The intentional ambiguity of the avatar is meant to symbolically reflect an important question: Is the future of humankind becoming luminous or more dim? The Brights aspire "to take the promising route, whereby the imagery brings to mind a gradually increasing illumination for this earth of ours, an escalation of enlightenment".<sup id="cite_ref-symbology_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-symbology-23">[23]</a></sup> This optimistic interpretation of the Brights' symbol is summarized by the <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a> "Embrightenment Now!"</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brights_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The movement has been criticised by some (both religious and non-religious) who have objected to the adoption of the title "bright" because they believe it suggests that the individuals with a naturalistic <a href="/wiki/Worldview" class="mw-redirect" title="Worldview">worldview</a> are more intelligent ("brighter") than non-naturalists, such as <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_skepticism" title="Philosophical skepticism">philosophical skeptics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealists</a>, believers in the <a href="/wiki/Paranormal" title="Paranormal">paranormal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_theism" title="Philosophical theism">philosophical theists</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Religiosity_and_intelligence" title="Religiosity and intelligence">religious</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-not_so_bright_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-not_so_bright-24">[24]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Committee_for_Skeptical_Inquiry" title="Committee for Skeptical Inquiry">Committee for Skeptical Inquiry</a> published an article by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Mooney_(journalist)" title="Chris Mooney (journalist)">Chris Mooney</a> titled "Not Too 'Bright<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>" in which he stated that, although he agreed with the movement, Richard Dawkins's and Daniel Dennett's "campaign to rename religious unbelievers 'brights' could use some rethinking" because of the possibility that the term would be misinterpreted.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The journalist and noted atheist <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hitchens" title="Christopher Hitchens">Christopher Hitchens</a> likewise found it a "cringe-making proposal that atheists should conceitedly nominate themselves to be called 'brights.<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>"<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to this, Daniel Dennett has stated:<sup id="cite_ref-Dennett_2006_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennett_2006-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There was also a negative response, largely objecting to the term that had been chosen [not by me]: <i>bright</i>, which seemed to imply that others were dim or stupid. But the term, modeled on the highly successful hijacking of the ordinary word "gay" by homosexuals, does not have to have that implication. Those who are not gays are not necessarily glum; they're <i>straight</i>. Those who are not brights are not necessarily dim.</p>
</blockquote>
