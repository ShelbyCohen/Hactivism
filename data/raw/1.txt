			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Adverse Childhood Experiences Study</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>The <b>Adverse Childhood Experiences Study</b> (<b>ACE Study</b>) is a research study conducted by the American <a href="/wiki/Health_maintenance_organization" title="Health maintenance organization">health maintenance organization</a> <a href="/wiki/Kaiser_Permanente" title="Kaiser Permanente">Kaiser Permanente</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ACE_Study_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACE_Study-1">[1]</a></sup> Participants were recruited to the study between 1995 and 1997 and have been in long-term follow up for health outcomes. The study has demonstrated an association of adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) with health and social problems as an adult. The study is frequently cited as a notable landmark in <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiological</a> research,<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks2012_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks2012-2">[2]</a></sup> and has produced more than 50 scientific articles and more than 100 conference and workshop presentations that look at the prevalence and consequences of ACEs.<sup id="cite_ref-ACE_Study_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACE_Study-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (February 2017)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1980s, the dropout rate of participants at <a href="/wiki/Kaiser_Permanente" title="Kaiser Permanente">Kaiser Permanente</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> clinic in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, was about 50%; despite all of the dropouts successfully <a href="/wiki/Weight_loss" title="Weight loss">losing weight</a> under the program.<sup id="cite_ref-Stevens_October_2012_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens_October_2012-3">[3]</a></sup> Vincent Felitti, head of Kaiser Permanente’s Department of Preventive Medicine in San Diego, conducted interviews with people who had left the program, and discovered that a majority of 286 people he interviewed had experienced <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">childhood sexual abuse</a>. The interview findings suggested to Felitti that weight gain might be a coping mechanism for <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fear" title="Fear">fear</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stevens_October_2012_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens_October_2012-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Felitti and Robert Anda from the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> (CDC) went on to survey childhood <a href="/wiki/Psychological_trauma" title="Psychological trauma">trauma</a> experiences of over 17,000 Kaiser Permanente patient volunteers.<sup id="cite_ref-Stevens_October_2012_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens_October_2012-3">[3]</a></sup> The 17,337 participants were volunteers from approximately 26,000 consecutive Kaiser Permanente members. About half were female; 74.8% were white; the average age was 57; 75.2% had attended college; all had jobs and good health care, because they were members of the Kaiser <a href="/wiki/Health_maintenance_organization" title="Health maintenance organization">health maintenance organization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prevalence_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prevalence-4">[4]</a></sup> Participants were asked about 10 types of childhood trauma that had been identified in earlier research literature:<sup id="cite_ref-Anda200304_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anda200304-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Physical_abuse" title="Physical abuse">Physical abuse</a></li>
<li>Sexual abuse</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emotional_abuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Emotional abuse">Emotional abuse</a></li>
<li>Physical <a href="/wiki/Neglect" title="Neglect">neglect</a></li>
<li>Emotional neglect</li>
<li>Mother treated violently</li>
<li>Household <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a></li>
<li>Household <a href="/wiki/Mental_illness" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental illness">mental illness</a></li>
<li>Parental <a href="/wiki/Marital_separation" title="Marital separation">separation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">divorce</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Imprisonment" title="Imprisonment">Incarcerated</a> household member</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Findings">Findings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Findings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_ACE_Pyramid.gif" class="image"><img alt="Pyramid with horizontal layers representing, in ascending order, Adverse Childhood Experiences; Social, Emotional, and Cognitive Impairment; Adoption of Health-risk Behaviors; Disease, Disability, and Social Problems; and Early Death. A vertical arrow represents the course of life beginning at the base and moving upward. Smaller arrows depict gaps in scientific knowledge about the links between Adverse Childhood Experiences and latter risk factors." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/The_ACE_Pyramid.gif/330px-The_ACE_Pyramid.gif" width="330" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/The_ACE_Pyramid.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="369" data-file-height="224" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_ACE_Pyramid.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The ACE Pyramid represents the conceptual framework for the ACE Study, which has uncovered how adverse childhood experiences are strongly related to various risk factors for disease throughout the lifespan, according to the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ACE_Pyramid_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACE_Pyramid-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-About_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About-7">[7]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to the United States' <a href="/wiki/Substance_Abuse_and_Mental_Health_Services_Administration" title="Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration">Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration</a>, the ACE study found that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Adverse childhood experiences are common. For example, 28% of study participants reported physical abuse and 21% reported sexual abuse. Many also reported experiencing a divorce or parental separation, or having a parent with a mental and/or <a href="/wiki/Substance_use_disorder" title="Substance use disorder">substance use disorder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SAMHSA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SAMHSA-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Adverse childhood experiences often occur together. Almost 40% of the original sample reported two or more ACEs and 12.5% experienced four or more. Because ACEs occur in clusters, many subsequent studies have examined the cumulative effects of ACEs rather than the individual effects of each.<sup id="cite_ref-SAMHSA_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SAMHSA-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Adverse childhood experiences have a <a href="/wiki/Dose%E2%80%93response_relationship" title="Dose–response relationship">dose–response relationship</a> with many health problems. As researchers followed participants over time, they discovered that a person's cumulative ACEs score has a strong, graded relationship to numerous health, <a href="/wiki/Social_problem" class="mw-redirect" title="Social problem">social</a>, and behavioral problems throughout their lifespan, including substance use disorders. Furthermore, many problems related to ACEs tend to be <a href="/wiki/Comorbid" class="mw-redirect" title="Comorbid">comorbid</a>, or co-occurring.<sup id="cite_ref-SAMHSA_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SAMHSA-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>About two-thirds of individuals reported at least one adverse childhood experience; 87% of individuals who reported one ACE reported at least one additional ACE.<sup id="cite_ref-Anda200304_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anda200304-5">[5]</a></sup> The number of ACEs was strongly associated with adulthood high-risk health behaviors such as smoking, alcohol and drug abuse, promiscuity, and severe obesity, and correlated with ill-health including depression, <a href="/wiki/Heart_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_lung_disease" class="mw-disambig" title="Chronic lung disease">chronic lung disease</a><sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://dispenser.homenet.org/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn"><span title="Link needs disambiguation. (November 2016)">disambiguation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and shortened lifespan.<sup id="cite_ref-Anda200304_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anda200304-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Felitti1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Felitti1998-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Middlebrooks2008_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Middlebrooks2008-10">[10]</a></sup> Compared to an ACE score of zero, having four adverse childhood experiences was associated with a seven-fold increase in <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>, a doubling of risk of being diagnosed with cancer, and a four-fold increase in <a href="/wiki/Emphysema" class="mw-redirect" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a>; an ACE score above six was associated with a 30-fold increase in attempted suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks2012_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks2012-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ACE study's results suggest that maltreatment and <a href="/wiki/Dysfunctional_family" title="Dysfunctional family">household dysfunction</a> in childhood contribute to health problems decades later. These include <a href="/wiki/Chronic_condition" title="Chronic condition">chronic</a> diseases—such as heart disease, cancer, <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" class="mw-redirect" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>—that are the most common causes of death and <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WorldHealth_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorldHealth-11">[11]</a></sup> The study's findings, while relating to a specific population within the United States, might reasonably be assumed to reflect similar trends in other parts of the world, according to the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WorldHealth_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorldHealth-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_surveys">Subsequent surveys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subsequent surveys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ACE Study has produced more than 50 articles that look at the prevalence and consequences of ACEs.<sup id="cite_ref-Publications_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Publications-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary.2C_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (February 2017)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It has been influential in several areas. Subsequent studies have confirmed the high frequency of adverse childhood experiences, or found even higher incidences in urban or youth populations.</p>
<p>The original study questions have been used to develop a 10-item <a href="/wiki/Screening_(medicine)" title="Screening (medicine)">screening</a> questionnaire.<sup id="cite_ref-CDC2009_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDC2009-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Score_Test_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Score_Test-14">[14]</a></sup> Numerous subsequent surveys have confirmed that adverse childhood experiences are frequent.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:293px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ACEs_Study_State_Data_2009-2013.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/ACEs_Study_State_Data_2009-2013.jpg/291px-ACEs_Study_State_Data_2009-2013.jpg" width="291" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/ACEs_Study_State_Data_2009-2013.jpg/437px-ACEs_Study_State_Data_2009-2013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/ACEs_Study_State_Data_2009-2013.jpg/582px-ACEs_Study_State_Data_2009-2013.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1460" data-file-height="815" /></a>

</div>
<p>The CDC runs the <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_Risk_Factor_Surveillance_System" title="Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System">Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System</a> (BRFSS),<sup id="cite_ref-Gudmunson2012_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gudmunson2012-15">[15]</a></sup> an annual survey conducted by individual state health departments in all 50 states. An expanded survey instrument in several states found each state to be similar.<sup id="cite_ref-Bynum2009_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bynum2009-16">[16]</a></sup> Some states have collected additional local data.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Adverse childhood experiences were even more frequent in studies in urban Philadelphia<sup id="cite_ref-PhilAceStudy_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PhilAceStudy-19">[19]</a></sup> and in a survey of young mothers (mostly younger than 19).<sup id="cite_ref-Stevens2012_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens2012-20">[20]</a></sup> Internationally, an ACE International Questionnaire (ACE-IQ) is undergoing <a href="/wiki/Verification_and_validation" title="Verification and validation">validation testing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Surveys of adverse childhood experiences have been conducted in Romania,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> the Czech Republic,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> the Republic of Macedonia,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Norway, the Philippines, the United Kingdom, Canada, China and Jordan.<sup id="cite_ref-About_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The citation is not specific enough to facilitate source verification (March 2017)">not specific enough to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> Child Trends used data from the 2011/12 National Survey of Children's Health (NSCH) to analyze ACEs prevalence in children nationally, and by state. The NSCH's list of "adverse family experiences" includes a measure of economic hardship and shows that this is the most common ACE reported nationally.<sup id="cite_ref-childtrends_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-childtrends-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurobiology_of_stress">Neurobiology of stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neurobiology of stress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Social_stress" title="Social stress">Social stress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stress_in_early_childhood" title="Stress in early childhood">Stress in early childhood</a></div>
<p>Cognitive and neuroscience researchers have examined possible mechanisms that might explain the negative consequences of adverse childhood experiences on adult health.<sup id="cite_ref-Weiss_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss-26">[26]</a></sup> Adverse childhood experiences can alter the structural <a href="/wiki/Human_development_(biology)" title="Human development (biology)">development</a> of neural networks and the <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Neuroendocrine_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuroendocrine system">neuroendocrine systems</a><sup id="cite_ref-Anda2006_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anda2006-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Danese2012_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danese2012-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Teicher_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Teicher-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kolassa_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kolassa-30">[30]</a></sup> and may have long-term effects on the body, including speeding up the processes of disease and aging and compromising immune systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Sorrow_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sorrow-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Moffitt_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moffitt-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rogosch2011_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rogosch2011-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Epigenetics" title="Epigenetics">epigenetic</a> transmission may occur due to stress during pregnancy or during interactions between mother and newborns. Maternal stress, depression, and exposure to partner violence have all been shown to have epigenetic effects on infants.<sup id="cite_ref-Kolassa_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kolassa-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementing_practices">Implementing practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Implementing practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As knowledge about the prevalence and consequences of adverse childhood experiences increases, trauma-informed and resilience-building practices based on the research is being implemented in communities, education, public health departments, social services, faith-based organizations and criminal justice. A few states are considering legislation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communities">Communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As knowledge about the prevalence and consequences of ACEs increases, more communities seek to integrate trauma-informed and resilience-building practices into their agencies and systems. Tarpon Springs, Florida, became the first trauma-informed community in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Stevens_CP_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens_CP-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tarpon_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tarpon-35">[35]</a></sup> Trauma-informed initiatives in Tarpon Springs include trauma-awareness training for the local housing authority, changes in programs for ex-offenders, and new approaches to educating students with learning difficulties.<sup id="cite_ref-Tarpon_ACE_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tarpon_ACE-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Children who are exposed to adverse childhood experiences may become overloaded with stress hormones, leaving them in a constant state of arousal and alertness to environmental and relational threats. Therefore, they may have difficulty focusing on school work, and consolidating new memory, making it harder for them to learn at school.<sup id="cite_ref-Making_SPACE_for_Learning_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Making_SPACE_for_Learning-37">[37]</a></sup> Approximately one in three or four children have experienced significant ACEs.<sup id="cite_ref-Spokane_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spokane-38">[38]</a></sup> A study by the Area Health Education Center of Washington State University found that students with at least three ACEs are three times as likely to experience academic failure, six times as likely to have behavioral problems, and five times as likely to have attendance problems.<sup id="cite_ref-Spokane_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spokane-38">[38]</a></sup> These students may have trouble trusting teachers and other adults, and may have difficulty creating and maintaining relationships.<sup id="cite_ref-Toxic_stress_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toxic_stress-39">[39]</a></sup> The trauma-informed school movement aims to train teachers and staff to help children self-regulate, and to help families that are having problems that result in children's normal response to trauma, rather that simply jumping to punishment. It also seeks to provide behavioral consequences that will not re-traumatize a child. Punishment is often ineffective, and better results can often be achieved with positive reinforcement.<sup id="cite_ref-Mass_Wash_Schools_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mass_Wash_Schools-40">[40]</a></sup> Out-of-school suspensions can be particularly bad for students with difficult home lives; forcing students to remain at home may increase their distrust of adults.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Trauma-sensitive, or compassionate, schooling has become<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> increasingly popular in Washington, Massachusetts, and California. Lincoln High School in Walla Walla, Washington, adapted a trauma-informed approached to discipline and reduced its suspensions by 85%.<sup id="cite_ref-Stevens_April_2012_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens_April_2012-41">[41]</a></sup> Rather than standard punishment, students are taught to recognize their reaction to stress and learn to control it. Spokane, Washington, schools conducted a research study that demonstrated that academic risk was correlated with students’ experiences of traumatic events known to their teachers.<sup id="cite_ref-Spokane_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spokane-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Spokane2_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spokane2-42">[42]</a></sup> The same school district has begun a study to test the impact of trauma-informed intervention programs, in an attempt to reduce the impact of toxic stress. In Brockton, Massachusetts, a community-wide meeting led to a trauma-informed approach being adopted by the Brockton School District.<sup id="cite_ref-Mass_Wash_Schools_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mass_Wash_Schools-40">[40]</a></sup> So far, all of the district's elementary schools have implemented trauma-informed improvement plans, and there are plans to do the same in the middle school and high school. About one-fifth of the district teachers have participated in a course on teaching traumatized students. Police alert schools when they have arrested someone or visited at a student's address. Massachusetts state legislation has sought to require all schools to develop plans to create "safe and supportive schools".<sup id="cite_ref-Mass_Wash_Schools_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mass_Wash_Schools-40">[40]</a></sup> At El Dorado, an elementary school in San Francisco, California, trauma-informed practices were associated with a suspension reduction of 89%.<sup id="cite_ref-El_Dorado_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-El_Dorado-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_services">Social services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Social services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Social service providers—including welfare systems, housing authorities, homeless shelters, and <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence" title="Domestic violence">domestic violence</a> centers – are adopting trauma-informed approaches that help to prevent ACEs or minimize their impact. Utilizing tools that screen for trauma can help a social service worker direct their clients to interventions that meet their specific needs.<sup id="cite_ref-Common_Ground_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Common_Ground-44">[44]</a></sup> Trauma-informed practices can also help social service providers look at how trauma impacts the whole family.<sup id="cite_ref-FIT_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FIT-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Trauma-informed approaches can improve child welfare services by 1) openly discussing trauma and 2) addressing parental trauma.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2015)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-Starve_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Starve-46">[46]</a></sup> The New Hampshire Division for Children Youth and Families (DCYF) is taking a trauma-informed approach to their foster care services by educating staff about childhood trauma, screening children entering foster care for trauma, using trauma-informed language to mitigate further traumatization, mentoring birth parents and involving them in collaborative parenting, and training foster parents to be trauma-informed.<sup id="cite_ref-Common_Ground_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Common_Ground-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Albany, New York the HEARTS Initiative has led to local organizations developing trauma-informed practice.<sup id="cite_ref-Starve_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Starve-46">[46]</a></sup> Senior Hope Inc., an organization serving adults over the age of 50, began implementing the 10-question ACE survey and talking with their clients about childhood trauma. The LaSalle School, which serves orphaned and abandoned boys, began looking at delinquent boys in from a trauma-informed perspective and began administering the ACE questionnaire to their clients.</p>
<p>Housing authorities are also becoming trauma-informed. Supportive housing can sometimes recreate control and power dynamics associated with clients’ early trauma.<sup id="cite_ref-Bebout_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bebout-47">[47]</a></sup> This can be reduced through trauma-informed practices, such as training staff to be respectful of clients' space by scheduling appointments and not letting themselves into clients' private spaces, and also understanding that an aggressive response may be trauma-related coping strategies.<sup id="cite_ref-Bebout_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bebout-47">[47]</a></sup> The housing authority in Tarpon Springs provided trauma-awareness training to staff so they could better understand and react to their clients' stress and anger resulting from poor employment, health, and housing.<sup id="cite_ref-Tarpon_ACE_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tarpon_ACE-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>A survey of 200 homeless individuals in California and New York demonstrated that more than 50% had experienced at least four ACEs.<sup id="cite_ref-ACEs_360_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACEs_360-48">[48]</a></sup> In Petaluma, California, the Committee on the Shelterless (COTS) uses a trauma-informed approach called Restorative Integral Support (RIS) to reduce intergenerational homelessness.<sup id="cite_ref-Larkin_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin-49">[49]</a></sup> RIS increases awareness of and knowledge about ACEs, and calls on staff to be compassionate and focus on the whole person. COTS now consider themselves ACE-informed and focus on resiliency and recovery.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_care_services">Health care services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Health care services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Screening for or talking about ACEs with parents and children can help to foster healthy physical and psychological development and can help doctors understand the circumstances that children and their parents are facing. By screening for ACEs in children, pediatric doctors and nurses can better understand behavioral problems. Some doctors have questioned whether some behaviors resulting in <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder" title="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder">attention deficit hyperactivity disorder</a> (ADHD) diagnoses are in fact reactions to trauma. Children who have experienced four or more ACEs are three times as likely to take ADHD medication when compared with children with less than four ACEs.<sup id="cite_ref-ADHD_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADHD-50">[50]</a></sup> Screening parents for their ACEs allows doctors to provide the appropriate support to parents who have experienced trauma, helping them to build resilience, foster attachment with their children, and prevent a family cycle of ACEs.<sup id="cite_ref-Pediatrics_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pediatrics-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PrimaryHealth_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PrimaryHealth-52">[52]</a></sup> Trauma-informed pediatric care also allows doctors to develop a more trusting relationship with parents, opening the lines of communication.<sup id="cite_ref-Gottlieb_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gottlieb-53">[53]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Montefiore_Medical_Center" title="Montefiore Medical Center">Montefiore Medical Center</a> ACEs screenings will soon be implemented in 22 pediatric clinics. In a pilot program any child with one parent who has an ACE score of four or higher is offered enrollment and receive a variety of services. For families enrolled in the program parents report fewer ER visits and children have healthier emotional and social development, compared with those not enrolled.<sup id="cite_ref-Pediatrics_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pediatrics-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Montefiore_Medical_Group_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montefiore_Medical_Group-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health">Public health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Public health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most American doctors as of 2015 do not use ACE surveys to assess patients. Objections to doing so include that there are no <a href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" title="Randomized controlled trial">randomized controlled trials</a> that show that such surveys can be used to actually improve health outcomes, there are no standard protocols for how to use the information gathered, and that revisiting negative childhood experiences could be emotionally traumatic.<sup id="cite_ref-fear_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fear-55">[55]</a></sup> Other obstacles to adoption include that the technique is not taught in medical schools, is not billable, and the nature of the conversation makes some doctors personally uncomfortable.<sup id="cite_ref-fear_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fear-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some public health centers see ACEs as an important way (especially for mothers and children)<sup id="cite_ref-Hellerstedt_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hellerstedt-56">[56]</a></sup> to target health interventions for individuals during sensitive periods of development early in their life, or even in utero.<sup id="cite_ref-Hellerstedt_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hellerstedt-56">[56]</a></sup> For example, Jefferson Country Public Health clinic in Port Townsend, Washington, now screens pregnant women, their partners, parents of children with special needs, and parents involved with CPS for ACEs.<sup id="cite_ref-Public_Health_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Public_Health-57">[57]</a></sup> With regard to patient counseling, the clinic treats ACEs like other health risks such as smoking or alcohol consumption.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_justice">Criminal justice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criminal justice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since research suggests that incarcerated individuals are much more likely to have been exposed to violence and suffer from <a href="/wiki/Posttraumatic_stress_disorder" title="Posttraumatic stress disorder">posttraumatic stress disorder</a> (PTSD),<sup id="cite_ref-Corrections_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corrections-58">[58]</a></sup> a trauma-informed approach may better help to address some of these criminogenic risk factors and can create a less traumatizing criminal justice experience.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2015)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> Programs, like Seeking Safety, are often used to help individuals in the criminal justice system learn how to better cope with trauma, PTSD, and substance abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-Seeking_Safety_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seeking_Safety-59">[59]</a></sup> Juvenile courts better help deter children from crime and delinquency when they understand the trauma many of these children have experienced.<sup id="cite_ref-Juvenile_Courts_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Juvenile_Courts-60">[60]</a></sup> The criminal justice system itself can also retraumatize individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawaii_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawaii-61">[61]</a></sup> This can be prevented by creating safer facilities where correctional and police officers are properly trained to keep incidents from escalating.<sup id="cite_ref-Corrections_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corrections-58">[58]</a></sup> Partnerships between police and mental health providers can also reduce the possible traumatizing effects of police intervention and help provide families with the proper mental health and social services.<sup id="cite_ref-Law_Enforcement_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law_Enforcement-62">[62]</a></sup> The Women’s Community Correctional Center of Hawaii began a Trauma-Informed Care Initiative that aims to train all employees to be aware and sensitive to trauma, to screen all women in their facility for trauma, to assess those who have experienced trauma, and begin providing trauma-informed mental health care to those women identified.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawaii_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawaii-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Faith-based_organizations">Faith-based organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Faith-based organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some faith-based organizations offer spiritual services in response to traumas identified by ACE surveys. For example, the founder of ACE Overcomers<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> combined the epidemiology of ACEs, the neurobiology of toxic stress and principles of the Christian <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> into a workbook and 12-week course used by <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a> in several states.<sup id="cite_ref-pastor_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pastor-64">[64]</a></sup> Faith-based organizations also participate in the online group ACES Connection Network.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The Faith and Health Connection ministry<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> also applies principles of <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">Christian theology</a> to address childhood traumas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adverse_Childhood_Experiences_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vermont has proposed a bill, H. 762, The Adverse Childhood Experience Questionnaire, which seeks wide use of ACE screening by health providers and aims to educate medical and health school students about ACEs.<sup id="cite_ref-Vermont_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vermont-67">[67]</a></sup> Previously Washington State passed legislation to set up a public-private partnership to further community development of trauma-informed and resilience-building practices that had begun in that state; but it was not adequately funded.<sup id="cite_ref-Vermont_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vermont-67">[67]</a></sup> On August 18, 2014, California lawmakers unanimously passed ACR No. 155, which encourages policies reducing children's exposure to adverse experiences.<sup id="cite_ref-CASenate_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CASenate-68">[68]</a></sup> Recent Massachusetts legislation supports a trauma-informed school movement as part of The Reduction of Gun Violence bill (No. 4376). This bill aims to create "safe and supportive schools" through services and initiatives focused on physical, social, and emotional safety.<sup id="cite_ref-Mass_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mass-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
