<p>Investors should not “obsess about the wave of anti-globalization” projected by many politicians across the western world, says Nigel Green, the founder and chief executive of deVere Group, a U.K. based financial advisory.</p>
<p>“Populism is an increasingly important force in global politics.&nbsp; Understandably, people are worrying about their jobs, their wages, and stagnating economic growth...and are seeking alternatives,&quot; he says.</p>
<p>The phenomen is most obviously evidenced by two high profile votes: Brexit and the election of Donald Trump on Tuesday. What's next? Looking at the stock market, the Russell 2000 Index has gained 8.6% since Trump's election, beating the S&amp;P 500's 1.2% gain. MSCI World is up 1.7% while the MSCI Emerging Markets is taking it on the chin, down 8.46% in expectations for higher rates in the U.S. and an America First economic policy that likely means a stronger dollar. If Trump can get tax laws passed that lower the corporate tax rate and bring in billions of off-shore capital being held by U.S. multinationals headquartered in fiscal paradises, inflows could easily strengthen the dollar, raise inflation, and increase interest rates. Money will leave the low rate investment grade markets (Europe and Japan) and go to the U.S.</p>
<div id="inread" article-ad-inbody="inread" class="inread"></div>
<p>Consider that a hint of what's to come.</p>
<p>Trump has made several proposals that could benefit the U.S. economy, notes Richard Barkham, global chief economist of real estate firm CBRE. These include increased defense and infrastructure spending; lower taxes for businesses; reduced business regulations; repealing and replacing Obamacare; tighter border security and one of the scariest ones for the market -- the imposition of trade tariffs and the uncertainty of existing trade deals.</p>
<p>Both Mexico and Canada have signalled that they were willing to look at the North American Free Trade Agreement again, and revise some of its rules.</p>
<p>With Republicans leading Congress, Trump &quot;can bring his agenda up for votes and use procedural maneuvering to further it along&quot; says Barkham. This bodes well for a lot of his ideas to pass, though many in the markets doubt that he will get anywhere near a tariff on Made in China and can scapegoat Republicans for that. &quot;Trump's proposals are likely to be tempered as they move through Congress.&quot;</p>
