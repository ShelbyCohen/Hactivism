		<div class="top-image"><img typeof="foaf:Image" src="http://www.georgiaencyclopedia.org/sites/default/files/styles/article-top/public/m-10520.jpg?itok=ycO7xoHp" width="650" height="488" alt="" /></div><div class="moduleContainer articleExplore"><h2 class="block-title">Explore This Article</h2><div class="articleContents"><div class="moduleTitleBlock"><div class="block-title">Contents</div></div><div class="articleSubhead articleSubhead-first"><a href="#Civil-Rights-Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a></div><div class="articleSubhead "><a href="#Activism-at-UGA">Activism at UGA</a></div><div class="articleSubhead "><a href="#Antiwar-Protests">Antiwar Protests</a></div><div class="articleSubhead "><a href="#Atlanta-Counterculture">Atlanta Counterculture</a></div><div class="articleSubhead "><a href="#Women's-Rights-and-Gay-Liberation-Movements">Women's Rights and Gay Liberation Movements</a></div></div></div> <div class="p">During the 1960s Georgia and the rest of the country experienced an increase in student activism on its college campuses and in its cities. Opposed to U.S. political leadership and dissatisfied with American culture, student <div class="inlineWrapper media-right"><div class="articleImage"><a href="#" class="" title="View the Full Gallery" onclick="nge.gallery.load(0, this)"><img src="http://www.georgiaencyclopedia.org/sites/default/files/styles/article-body/public/m-10520.jpg?itok=9GQBScJu" width="150" height="150" alt="In 1970 demonstrators in downtown Atlanta protest U.S. involvement in the Vietnam War (1964-73). Most antiwar marches in the city, which took place from the late 1960s to the early 1970s, followed a route down Peachtree Street to Piedmont Park." /></a><a href="#" class="" title="Vietnam War Protest" onclick="nge.gallery.load(0, this)">Vietnam War Protest</a></div></div>activists held demonstrations across the state and experimented with lifestyle changes in the hope of effecting fundamental change in American life.</div>
<div class="p">The student movement, also called the New Left because it represented the latest manifestation of left-leaning political activism, gained converts on campuses across the nation throughout the decade. In Georgia several schools maintained chapters of national and regional student organizations, such as Students for a Democratic Society and the Southern Student Organizing Committee (SSOC). Georgia student leaders often looked to the national New Left for guidance and inspiration. Georgia student activists who joined the <a href="/articles/history-archaeology/civil-rights-movement">civil rights</a> and student movements routinely risked arrest and physical harm, as well as alienation from their more conservative friends and families.</div>
<h3><a name="Civil-Rights-Movement"></a>Civil Rights Movement</h3>
<div class="p">The civil rights movement motivated many of Georgia's New Left leaders to become involved in political activism. <a href="/articles/education/morehouse-college">Morehouse College</a> student Lonnie King, inspired by restaurant sit-ins in Greensboro, North Carolina, organized a protest campaign by drawing three student leaders from each of <a href="/articles/counties-cities-neighborhoods/atlanta">Atlanta</a>'s six historically black colleges and universities: Atlanta University and Clark College (later <a href="/articles/education/clark-atlanta-university">Clark Atlanta University</a>), the <a href="/articles/arts-culture/interdenominational-theological-center">Interdenominational Theological Center</a>, Morehouse, <a href="/articles/education/morris-brown-college">Morris Brown College</a>, and <a href="/articles/education/spelman-college">Spelman College</a>) and talking to the schools' presidents. The resulting coalition, called the Committee on Appeal for Human Rights (COAHR), organized ten sit-ins by 200 students in downtown Atlanta on March 15, 1960. (In fear of such demonstrations, the <a href="/articles/government-politics/georgia-general-assembly">Georgia legislature</a> had recently made sit-ins a misdemeanor.) The <a href="/articles/history-archaeology/student-nonviolent-coordinating-committee-sncc">Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC)</a> continued the sit-ins with COAHR in October, and <a href="/articles/arts-culture/martin-luther-king-jr-1929-1968">Martin Luther King Jr.</a> was arrested along with other protestors on October 19. Because, in part, of the economic stress brought on by such protests, Atlanta outlawed <a href="/articles/history-archaeology/segregation">segregation</a> in public facilities in the fall of 1961.  </div>
<div class="p">In 1964 <a href="/articles/education/emory-university">Emory University</a> student Gene Guerrero became the first chairman of SSOC; he turned to activism after being arrested at a 1963 civil rights <a href="/articles/history-archaeology/atlanta-sit-ins">sit-in</a> in Atlanta.</div>
<h3><a name="Activism-at-UGA"></a>Activism at UGA</h3>
<div class="p">The <div class="inlineWrapper "><div class="articleImage"><a href="#" class="" title="View the Full Gallery" onclick="nge.gallery.load(1, this)"><img src="http://www.georgiaencyclopedia.org/sites/default/files/styles/article-body/public/m-10486.jpg?itok=lZ6_XBuh" width="150" height="150" alt="Graffiti left by antiwar protestors marks the military building at the University of Georgia in Athens during the Vietnam War (1964-73). Student activists at UGA attempted to burn down the building five times between 1968 and 1972. The slogan &quotChe Lives&quot is a reference to Che Guevara, a leader of the socialist revolution in Cuba and an icon of the American New Left. He was captured and executed in Bolivia in 1967." /></a><a href="#" class="" title="UGA Military Building" onclick="nge.gallery.load(1, this)">UGA Military Building</a></div></div><a href="/articles/education/university-georgia">University of Georgia</a> (UGA) in <a href="/articles/counties-cities-neighborhoods/athens">Athens</a> possessed a dedicated community of activists during the 1960s. In April 1968 students conducted a three-day sit-in at the Academic Building to protest the unequal treatment of female students. (The dress code and curfew rules were stricter for women than for men, and women, unlike men, were banned from living off campus and from drinking.) In the wake of the killing of four students by National Guardsmen at Kent State University in Ohio in May 1970, 3,000 students demonstrated on the UGA campus, leading the Board of Regents to close all schools in the <a href="/articles/education/university-system-georgia">University System of Georgia</a> for two days. UGA did not escape the growing commitment to violence that characterized the national New Left in the late 1960s. On five separate occasions from 1968 to 1972, student activists attempted unsuccessfully to burn down the military building on campus.</div>
<h3><a name="Antiwar-Protests"></a>Antiwar Protests</h3>
<div class="p">The growing sentiment among Americans against the Vietnam War (1964-73) generated numerous protests in Georgia. The largest antiwar demonstrations occurred during the fall of 1969, as part of the national "moratorium" campaign. In October and again in November, thousands of students gathered on campuses across the state to remember those killed in Southeast Asia. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, Atlanta witnessed many antiwar demonstrations, most of which followed a route down Peachtree Street to Piedmont Park.</div>
<h3><a name="Atlanta-Counterculture"></a>Atlanta Counterculture</h3>
<div class="p">As <div class="inlineWrapper "><div class="articleImage"><a href="#" class="" title="View the Full Gallery" onclick="nge.gallery.load(2, this)"><img src="http://www.georgiaencyclopedia.org/sites/default/files/styles/article-body/public/m-10518.jpg?itok=SHEaUVVZ" width="150" height="150" alt="Staff members of the Great Speckled Bird, an underground newspaper in Atlanta published from 1968 to 1976, meet to plan a forthcoming issue. The paper, founded by students from several Georgia colleges, advocated New Left issues." /></a><a href="#" class="" title="Great Speckled Bird" onclick="nge.gallery.load(2, this)"><i>Great Speckled Bird</i></a></div></div>the state's capital and one of the South's largest cities, Atlanta was home to numerous colleges and universities, and the city became a gathering point for many activists. It also became home to a countercultural community. Commonly called "hippies," counterculture followers advocated illegal drug use, communal living, relaxed sexual norms, and other behaviors that went against mainstream American culture. A movement that shocked and confused many Americans, the counterculture seemed even more bizarre to many conservative southerners. The Atlanta counterculture centered mainly on Peachtree Street, between Eighth and Fourteenth streets. There, hippies found businesses that catered to their lifestyle, including music shops, coffeehouses, nightclubs, and health food stores.</div>
<div class="p">The success of the <a href="/articles/arts-culture/great-speckled-bird"> <i>Great Speckled Bird</i></a>, an underground newspaper that operated out of a house on Fourteenth Street in Atlanta, symbolized the considerable size and longevity of the 1960s student movement in Georgia. From 1968 until it ceased operation in 1976, writers for the <i>Bird</i> filled each weekly issue with stories about New Left causes. Founded by students from several Georgia colleges, the newspaper kept activists outside the metropolitan area in touch with the student movement. At its height in the early 1970s, activists distributed the paper throughout Georgia and the Southeast.</div>
<h3><a name="Women's-Rights-and-Gay-Liberation-Movements"></a>Women's Rights and Gay Liberation Movements</h3>
<div class="p">The <div class="inlineWrapper media-right"><div class="articleImage"><a href="#" class="" title="View the Full Gallery" onclick="nge.gallery.load(3, this)"><img src="http://www.georgiaencyclopedia.org/sites/default/files/styles/article-body/public/m-10504.jpg?itok=vogSriBE" width="150" height="150" alt="A student demonstrates for gay rights at the University of Georgia in Athens, circa 1971. In 1972 the Committee on Gay Education at the university successfully sued for the right to hold a dance on campus." /></a><a href="#" class="" title="Gay Activists" onclick="nge.gallery.load(3, this)">Gay Activists</a></div></div>commitment to social change that motivated the student movement in the 1960s did not end with the coming of the 1970s. In Georgia, as across the nation, new organizations formed to address the concerns and fight for the rights of previously ignored or marginalized groups of people. Two of the most important movements focused on women's and gay rights. Many female students who had protested for civil rights and against the Vietnam War began fighting for the equality of women. These women worked hard to change abortion laws and tried unsuccessfully to get the Equal Rights Amendment (ERA) approved by the Georgia legislature; the state house voted against the ERA in January 1974, and the state senate voted against it in February 1975. Homosexual men and women also began speaking out. At UGA the Committee on Gay Education successfully sued the university and in late 1972 won the right to hold a dance for gays and lesbians on university grounds.</div>
<div class="p">Georgia student activists participated fully in the social and political upheaval that overtook the nation in the 1960s. Although often overshadowed by events in other parts of the nation, the Georgia student movement played an integral part in the story of the twentieth century's most turbulent decade.</div>
