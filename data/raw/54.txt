<p><b>Idle No More</b> is an ongoing <a href="/wiki/Protest" title="Protest">protest</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">movement</a>, founded in December 2012 by four women: three <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> women and one non-Native ally. It is a grassroots movement among the <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_peoples_in_Canada" title="Aboriginal peoples in Canada">Aboriginal peoples in Canada</a> comprising the <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Métis people (Canada)">Métis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> peoples and their non-Aboriginal supporters in Canada, and to a lesser extent, internationally. It has consisted of a number of political actions worldwide, inspired in part by the <a href="/wiki/Liquid_diet" title="Liquid diet">liquid diet</a> <a href="/wiki/Hunger_strike" title="Hunger strike">hunger strike</a> of <a href="/wiki/Attawapiskat_First_Nation" title="Attawapiskat First Nation">Attawapiskat</a> Chief <a href="/wiki/Theresa_Spence" title="Theresa Spence">Theresa Spence</a><sup id="cite_ref-Raveena_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raveena-1">[1]</a></sup> and further coordinated via <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a>. A reaction to alleged legislative abuses of Indigenous <a href="/wiki/Treaty_rights" title="Treaty rights">treaty rights</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Harper" title="Stephen Harper">Stephen Harper</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Conservative Party of Canada">Conservative</a> federal government, the movement takes particular issue with the <a href="/wiki/Omnibus_bill" title="Omnibus bill">omnibus bill</a> <a href="/wiki/Jobs_and_Growth_Act" title="Jobs and Growth Act">Bill C-45</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The popular movement has included round dances in public places and blockades of rail lines.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2011" title="Canadian federal election, 2011">May 2, 2011 Canadian Federal election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Conservative Party of Canada">Conservative</a> federal government, led by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Harper" title="Stephen Harper">Stephen Harper</a>, proposed a number of <a href="/wiki/Omnibus_bill" title="Omnibus bill">omnibus</a> bills introducing sweeping legislative changes. While omnibus bills had been presented to parliament by previous governments, the removal of protections for forests and waterways proposed in Bill C-45 led to concern among Indigenous communities and environmentalist. Of particular concern is the removal of the term "absolute surrender" in Section 208.</p>
<p>A number of these measures drew fire from environmental and First Nations groups. In particular, Bill C-45 overhauled the <a href="/wiki/Navigable_Waters_Protection_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Navigable Waters Protection Act">Navigable Waters Protection Act</a> (NWPA) of 1882, renaming it the Navigation Protection Act (NPA). The NWPA had mandated an extensive approval and consultation process before construction of any kind could take place in or around any water which could in principle be navigated by any kind of floating craft. Under the new NPA, the approval process would only be required for development around one of a vastly circumscribed list of waterways set by the Minister of Transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many of the newly deregulated waterways passed through traditional First Nations land.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the NWPA had originally been intended to facilitate actual navigation, the ubiquity of waterways in the Canadian wilderness has given it the effect of strong environmental legislation by presenting a significant barrier to industrial development, especially to projects such as pipelines which crossed many rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The government had by this time been engaged for some years in a campaign for approval of the Enbridge Northern Gateway Pipelines Project, a proposal to build a pipeline for bitumen condensate connecting the Athabasca tar sands with the Pacific Ocean, facilitating unprocessed bitumen exports to China.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many bills affecting First Nations people have failed to be passed. Numerous attempts to introduce bills have failed due to their low priority for past federal governments, eventually dying on the <a href="/wiki/Order_Paper" title="Order Paper">order paper</a> without being debated or passed. <a href="/wiki/35th_Canadian_Parliament" title="35th Canadian Parliament">In 1996</a> Bill C-79, the Indian Act Optional Modification Act died on the order paper. <a href="/wiki/37th_Canadian_Parliament" title="37th Canadian Parliament">In 2002</a>, Bill C-7, the First Nations Governance Act, attempted to reform reserve administration. It died in 2003. <a href="/wiki/39th_Canadian_Parliament" title="39th Canadian Parliament">In 2008</a>, there was Bill C-47, the Family Homes on Reserves and Matrimonial Interests or Rights Act, to redress inequity in the treatment of women. That one died on the order paper three times and is returned before Parliament, now called Bill S-2.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The cancellation of the <a href="/wiki/Kelowna_Accord" title="Kelowna Accord">Kelowna Accords</a> by the current federal government was seen as a betrayal by natives.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Further background to this is the feeling that the federal government has repeatedly acted in bad faith with Aboriginal people's interests, and have violated treaties when it suited them. The feeling that the traditional tactics of negotiating with the federal government have become meaningless has caused support for new tactics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vision_and_goals">Vision and goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vision and goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The founders of Idle No More outlined the vision and goals of the movement in a January 10, 2013 press release as follows:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Vision [...] revolves around Indigenous Ways of Knowing rooted in Indigenous Sovereignty to protect water, air, land and all creation for future generations.</p>
<p>The Conservative government bills beginning with Bill C-45 threaten Treaties and this Indigenous Vision of Sovereignty. The movement promotes environmental protection and indigenous sovereignty. It plans to accomplish these goals by: (A) Implementing leadership structure and councils (Such as the Council of Women) (B) Taking training in coordinating rallies, media, messaging and safety issues as well as in identifying provocateurs, misinformation shills, and propaganda. (C) Placing key spokespeople and connecting with experienced experts in different areas; i.e. treaty research, indigenous rights and governance, environmental activism, writers, international spokesperson, national etc. (D) Creating chapters across Turtle Island under the umbrella of the main INM. (E) Requesting regular meetings with First Nations leadership to have ongoing discussions regarding third party agreements between the Government of Canada and industry corporations</p>
<p>To date the movement has been particularly focused on:</p>
<p>(A) The education and the revitalization of indigenous peoples through awareness and empowerment. (B) Encouraging knowledge sharing about indigenous sovereignty and environmental protections.<sup id="cite_ref-Official_Idle_No_More_Website_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Official_Idle_No_More_Website-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The press release also notes that "As a grassroots movement, clearly no political organization speaks for Idle No More". Furthermore, this is not just an Aboriginal Canadian movement. These pipeline projects will be stretching beyond borders carving through critical ecosystems and landscapes in the states. Canada’s large oil reserves have attracted industry to exploit, and profit, “The tar sands industry aims to create an extensive web of pipelines to deliver increasing amounts of this Canadian tar sands sludge to refineries in the United States.” (Glick, page 2). Reports say that some 900,000 barrels of oil per day will be traveling from Canada tar sands through these pipelines. According to the National Wildlife Federation report, these pipelines will stretch thousands and thousands of miles through Canada and into the states and will leave devastation along its paths, “This pipeline system would virtually assure the destruction of swaths of one of the world’s most important forest ecosystems, produce lake-sized reservoirs of toxic waste, import a thick, tarlike fuel that will release vast quantities of toxic chemicals into our air when it is refined in the U.S., and emit significantly more global warming pollutants into the atmosphere than fuels made from conventional oil.” (Glick, page 3). These impacts are already being seen in Canada’s peoples and wildlife, “Communities that live near the tar sands are already experiencing health problems linked to the pollution, and dozens of wildlife species are at risk, including millions of migrating cranes, swans, and songbirds.” (Glick, page 3). With the magnitude and power of this project, these negative impacts will not end soon, and will not end in Canada. These pipelines will stretch across borders and come into our own backyards here in the states.<sup id="cite_ref-Official_Idle_No_More_Website_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Official_Idle_No_More_Website-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Idle No More's vision has been linked by some commentators in the press with longstanding leftist political theories of <a href="/wiki/Indigenism" title="Indigenism">indigenism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> During the protests of late 2012 and early 2013, the theoretical framework of Idle No More has been frequently articulated in the Canadian press by Pamela Palmater. Palmater has denounced what she perceives as the federal government's "assimilationist agenda".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It has been suggested by others that the definition of "nation" is itself problematic.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sylvia McAdam, a co-founder of the movement, has said that she does not condone the rail or road blockade tactics that some demonstrators have used, and has spoken in support of peaceful protest "within the legal boundaries".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The movement was initiated by activists <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nina_Wilson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nina Wilson (page does not exist)">Nina Wilson</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sheelah_Mclean&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sheelah Mclean (page does not exist)">Sheelah Mclean</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvia_McAdam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sylvia McAdam (page does not exist)">Sylvia McAdam</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jessica_Gordon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jessica Gordon (page does not exist)">Jessica Gordon</a> in November 2012, during a <a href="/wiki/Teach-in" title="Teach-in">teach-in</a> at Station 20 West in <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon" title="Saskatoon">Saskatoon</a> called "Idle No More", held in response to the <a href="/wiki/Harper_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Harper government">Harper government</a>'s introduction of <a href="/wiki/Bill_C-45_Jobs_and_Growth_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill C-45 Jobs and Growth Act">Bill C-45</a>.</p>
<p>C-45 is a large <a href="/wiki/Omnibus_bill" title="Omnibus bill">omnibus bill</a> implementing numerous measures, many of which activists claim weaken <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental protection laws</a>. In particular, laws protecting all of the country's navigable waterways were limited in scope to protect only a few waterways of practical importance for navigation. Many of the affected waterways pass through land reserved to First Nations.</p>
<p>Law blog writer/observer Lorraine Land,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and Idle No More itself,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> have identified the following current bills as affecting natives or native sovereignty:</p>
<ul>
<li>Bill C-38 (Budget Omnibus Bill #1)</li>
<li>Bill C-45 (Budget Omnibus Bill #2)</li>
<li>Bill C-27 First Nations Financial Transparency Act<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>Bill S-2 Family Homes on Reserve and Matrimonial Interests or Right Act</li>
<li>Bill S-6 First Nations Elections Act</li>
<li>Bill S-8 Safe Drinking Water for First Nations</li>
<li>Bill C-428 Indian Act Amendment and Replacement Act</li>
<li>Bill S-207 An Act to amend the Interpretation Act</li>
<li>Bill S-212 First Nations Self-Government Recognition Bill</li>
<li>“First Nations” Private Ownership Act</li>
</ul>
<p>This led to a series of teach-ins, rallies and protests that were planned by the founders in a National Day Of Action on Dec 10 which coincided with Amnesty International's Human Rights Day.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> These coincided with similar protests already underway in British Columbia over the <a href="/wiki/Enbridge_Northern_Gateway_Pipelines" title="Enbridge Northern Gateway Pipelines">Northern Gateway</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Trails_pipeline&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pacific Trails pipeline (page does not exist)">Pacific Trails pipelines</a> as well as a march and protest organized by students at the Native Education College.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The protests were timed to coincide with the announcement that Chief <a href="/wiki/Theresa_Spence" title="Theresa Spence">Theresa Spence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Attawapiskat" class="mw-redirect" title="Attawapiskat">Attawapiskat</a> was launching a <a href="/wiki/Liquid_diet" title="Liquid diet">liquid diet</a> to demand a meeting with Prime Minister Harper and the Governor General of Canada to discuss Aboriginal rights. The <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_First_Nations" title="Assembly of First Nations">Assembly of First Nations</a> then issued an <a href="/wiki/Open_letter" title="Open letter">open letter</a> December 16 to Governor General <a href="/wiki/David_Johnston" title="David Johnston">David Johnston</a>, calling for a meeting to discuss Spence's demands.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also on December 17 the <a href="/wiki/Confederacy_of_Treaty_No._6_First_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Confederacy of Treaty No. 6 First Nations">Confederacy of Treaty No. 6 First Nations</a> issued a press release saying that they did not recognize the legality of any laws passed by the federal parliament, "including but not limited to Bill C-45, which do not fulfill their constitutionally recognized and affirmed treaty and Aboriginal rights; as well as the Crown's legal obligations to meaningfully consult and accommodate First Nations."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of January 4, 2013, the main goals have been narrowed down to (1) the establishment of a nation-to-nation relationship between First Nations and the Government of Canada, rather than a relationship as defined in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Act" title="Indian Act">Indian Act</a> to address issues and (2) social and environmental sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_exploitation">Resource exploitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Resource exploitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Idle No More movement generally opposes certain types of resource exploitation, particularly on First Nations territory.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The movement takes this stance against resource exploitation, as attributed to First Nations sovereignty and environmental sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The position is supported by many groups including non-governmental and grassroots organizations. In a human rights report on Canada, Amnesty International suggested that the government should have “respect for indigenous rights when issuing licences for mining, logging and petroleum and other resource extraction.”<sup id="cite_ref-Schultz_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schultz-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Idle No More movement outlines ways by which it opposes resource exploitation, although these views are contested within the First Nations population itself. It communicates the need for treaty modernization as well as increased land claims. It also advocates against resource exploitation on First Nations land without any benefit to the First Nations.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Furthermore, the movement argues that First Nations communities do not get an equitable share of the profits from natural resource exploitation and encourages the government to address this issue.<sup id="cite_ref-Schultz_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schultz-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>One demonstration reflecting opposition to resource exploitation was held at Barriere Lake in Quebec by the Algonquin people. It closed Highway 117 and was reported “to draw attention to forestry operations that they oppose on their lands."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In British Columbia (B.C.), the specific focus within resource exploitation is opposition to oil pipeline construction. Frank Brown, organizer of a B.C. Idle No More protest and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Heiltsuk_First_Nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Heiltsuk First Nation">Heiltsuk First Nation</a>, said the B.C. group opposed proposed pipelines such as the Enbridge Northern Gateway pipeline. Moreover, several First Nations communities, the BC Métis Federation, the Union of British Columbian Municipalities, and several local governments have passed resolutions banning pipelines in B.C. and opposing Enbridge specifically.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests_in_Canada">Protests in Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protests in Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Flash_mobs" class="mw-redirect" title="Flash mobs">flash mobs</a> performing <a href="/wiki/Round_dance" title="Round dance">round dances</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shopping_malls" class="mw-redirect" title="Shopping malls">shopping malls</a> became a recurring theme of the protest during the pre- and post-Christmas shopping season in 2012. On December 17 a <a href="/wiki/Flash_mob" title="Flash mob">flash mob</a> performed a <a href="/wiki/Round_dance" title="Round dance">round dance</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Centre_(Regina,_Saskatchewan)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornwall Centre (Regina, Saskatchewan)">Cornwall Centre</a> shopping mall in Regina.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The following day a similar flash mob round dance occurred at <a href="/wiki/West_Edmonton_Mall" title="West Edmonton Mall">West Edmonton Mall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> This tactic was also used at the <a href="/wiki/Rideau_Centre" title="Rideau Centre">Rideau Centre</a> in Ottawa<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=St.Vital_Centre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="St.Vital Centre (page does not exist)">St.Vital Centre</a> in Winnipeg. It spread internationally with a similar protest at the <a href="/wiki/Mall_of_America" title="Mall of America">Mall of America</a> in Minnesota. Members of the <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Bay_First_Nation" title="Sandy Bay First Nation">Sandy Bay First Nation</a> in Manitoba blocked the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Canada_Highway" title="Trans-Canada Highway">Trans-Canada Highway</a> on December 15, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Members of the <a href="/wiki/Driftpile_First_Nation" title="Driftpile First Nation">Driftpile First Nation</a> also blocked a road on December 18.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The group executed their second <a href="/wiki/Round_dance" title="Round dance">round dance</a> <a href="/wiki/Flash_mob" title="Flash mob">flash mob</a> in Saskatchewan on December 20, this time at <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Plaza_(Saskatoon)" title="Midtown Plaza (Saskatoon)">Midtown Plaza</a> in Saskatoon. Two thousand people filled the upper and lower levels of the mall for the 10-minute dance of the flash mob. Also on this day, organizers started gathering people around the Vimy war memorial on the Saskatoon riverbank.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> On December 27 an online source reported that there had been 100 protests in Canada to date<sup id="cite_ref-mediacoop1_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mediacoop1-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 30, as part of a day of nationwide actions, a group believed to be involved<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> with Idle No More blocked the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian National">Canadian National</a> <a href="/wiki/Main_line_(railway)" title="Main line (railway)">main railway line</a> between the country's two largest cities of Toronto and Montreal at a point near <a href="/wiki/Belleville,_Ontario" title="Belleville, Ontario">Belleville, Ontario</a> for approximately three hours.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> On January 2, Waterfront Station in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> was packed for a demonstration drawing hundreds of participants. Protestors danced and chanted.<sup id="cite_ref-ctvnews1_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctvnews1-40">[40]</a></sup> Protestors filled <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Eaton_Centre" title="Toronto Eaton Centre">Toronto's Eaton Centre</a>, while a simultaneous protest occurred in the <a href="/wiki/West_Edmonton_Mall" title="West Edmonton Mall">West Edmonton Mall</a>, 50 Protestors at Clifton Hill in Niagara Falls, and other locations in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-sunnewsnetwork1_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sunnewsnetwork1-41">[41]</a></sup> On January 5, 2013, protests shut down multiple border crossings throughout Canada, including Blue Water Bridge in Sarnia, Ontario, International Bridge in Cornwall, Ontario, the Peace Arch crossing in Surrey, B.C., The Peace Bridge between Fort Erie and Buffalo in the Niagara region, and NWT's Deh Cho Bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The International Bridge in Cornwall had not been closed since a month-long dispute between <a href="/wiki/Akwesasne" title="Akwesasne">Akwesasne</a> Nation and <a href="/wiki/Canada_Border_Services_Agency" title="Canada Border Services Agency">CBSA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 11, 2013, thousands of people participated in Idle No More demonstrations in all regions of Canada. In Vancouver, British Columbia, a mass march and rally was held at Vancouver City Hall. It was estimated over 1000 people attended the march which started at the Native Education College.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>

<p>The protests have also spread outside of Canada. On December 27 an online source reported that there had been 30 Idle No More protests in the United States, and solidarity protests in Stockholm, Sweden, London, UK, Berlin, Germany, Auckland, New Zealand, and Cairo, Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-mediacoop1_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mediacoop1-38">[38]</a></sup> On December 30, approximately 100 people from <a href="/wiki/Walpole_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Walpole Island">Walpole Island</a> marched to <a href="/wiki/Algonac,_Michigan" title="Algonac, Michigan">Algonac, Michigan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sunnewsnetwork1_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sunnewsnetwork1-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> CBS reported that "hundreds" attended a flash mob at the Mall of America in Minneapolis, Minnesota.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The <i>Twin Cities Daily Planet</i> called it a crowd of "over a thousand" and stated that it followed a similar protest a week earlier where <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Bellecourt" title="Clyde Bellecourt">Clyde Bellecourt</a> had been arrested, as well as another flash mob at the <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bunyan_Mall" title="Paul Bunyan Mall">Paul Bunyan Mall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bemidji,_Minnesota" title="Bemidji, Minnesota">Bemidji</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On January 5, the International Bridge was closed again due to <a href="/wiki/Mohawk_people" title="Mohawk people">Mohawk</a> protests from New York.<sup id="cite_ref-indiancountrytodaymedianetwork.com_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indiancountrytodaymedianetwork.com-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the United States, protests have been reported in many states: Michigan,<sup id="cite_ref-sunnewsnetwork1_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sunnewsnetwork1-41">[41]</a></sup> Minnesota, Ohio,<sup id="cite_ref-Idle_no_More_Columbus_Flash_mob_2.2F16.2F13_at_Easton_Mall_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Idle_no_More_Columbus_Flash_mob_2.2F16.2F13_at_Easton_Mall-49">[49]</a></sup> New York,<sup id="cite_ref-indiancountrytodaymedianetwork.com_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indiancountrytodaymedianetwork.com-48">[48]</a></sup> Arizona,<sup id="cite_ref-indiancountrytodaymedianetwork1_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indiancountrytodaymedianetwork1-50">[50]</a></sup> Colorado,<sup id="cite_ref-indiancountrytodaymedianetwork1_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indiancountrytodaymedianetwork1-50">[50]</a></sup> Maine,<sup id="cite_ref-bangordailynews.com_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bangordailynews.com-51">[51]</a></sup> New Mexico,<sup id="cite_ref-occupynewmexico.org_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-occupynewmexico.org-52">[52]</a></sup> Vermont,<sup id="cite_ref-vtcommons.org_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vtcommons.org-53">[53]</a></sup> South Carolina,<sup id="cite_ref-CoSC_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CoSC-54">[54]</a></sup> Washington State,<sup id="cite_ref-IdleNoMore_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IdleNoMore-55">[55]</a></sup> Washington, D.C., Indiana and Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-ctvnews1_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctvnews1-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Support">Support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Former <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal</a> Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Paul_Martin" title="Paul Martin">Paul Martin</a>, who had supported the <a href="/wiki/Kelowna_Accords" class="mw-redirect" title="Kelowna Accords">Kelowna Accords</a>, said that Teresa Spence's efforts made her "an inspiration to all Canadians". Former <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Canada">Progressive Conservative</a> Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Joe_Clark" title="Joe Clark">Joe Clark</a> also met Spence and made a speech afterward saying that Canada and First Nations were on a dangerous path.<sup id="cite_ref-Stechyson_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stechyson-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In solidarity with the movement, <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Canadians" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of Canadians">Council of Canadians</a> National Chairperson <a href="/wiki/Maude_Barlow" title="Maude Barlow">Maude Barlow</a>, author <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Klein" title="Naomi Klein">Naomi Klein</a>, and singer <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Slean" title="Sarah Slean">Sarah Slean</a> have returned their <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II_Diamond_Jubilee_Medal" title="Queen Elizabeth II Diamond Jubilee Medal">Queen Elizabeth II Diamond Jubilee Medals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Criticism of the Idle No More movement has appeared in Canada's mainstream media, which has covered related high-profile protests such as road blockades and Spence's fast. Several newspapers, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Calgary_Herald" title="Calgary Herald">Calgary Herald</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">The Globe and Mail</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> have published editorials critical of the movement or of Spence's hunger strike. The <i><a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Journal" title="Edmonton Journal">Edmonton Journal</a></i> questioned Idle No More organizer Tanya Kappo's opposition to individual property rights on reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> An editorial in the <i><a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_Free_Press" title="Winnipeg Free Press">Winnipeg Free Press</a></i> called for a halt to Idle No More protests, and pointed to what it views as an incorrect interpretation of legislation by some protestors:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Changes to the Navigable Waters Protection Act, for example, are not a threat to aboriginal resources. The government is reducing its jurisdiction to 97 lakes, 62 rivers and three oceans, while leaving the environmental management of minor streams and water bodies to municipalities and provinces. It means more local control over projects without the excessive delays and red tape of the federal government.</p>
<p>An amendment to a law that allows First Nations to lease their land has been described falsely as an act to allow for the sale of aboriginal land, but all it does is speed up the process for possible lease deals that aboriginal leaders themselves have sought so they can compete for economic development.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The parliamentary secretary to the Minister of Aboriginal Affairs stated to media that "With respect to Bill C-45, the changes to property leasing provisions, these changes respond directly to the request of a number of First Nations Chiefs to provide them with more flexibility".<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Conservative Senator <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Brazeau" title="Patrick Brazeau">Patrick Brazeau</a>, who is Aboriginal and a former chief, has told media that the demonstrations lacked focus in protesting "a whole gamut of issues".<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> He also said that democratic processes were available for participating in decision-making affecting First Nations.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> He criticized Teresa Spence's tactics, saying she should have used the "proper parliamentary process."<sup id="cite_ref-Stechyson_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stechyson-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The founders of the Idle No More movement have emphasized their intention for the movement to remain at its core a "grassroots" movement, led by Indigenous women; they have released a statement to say that they have a different vision than that of the "leadership" of First Nation Chiefs, saying "we have been given a clear mandate … to work outside of the systems of government."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> By early January 2013, <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Atleo" title="Shawn Atleo">Shawn Atleo</a>, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_First_Nations" title="Assembly of First Nations">Assembly of First Nations</a>, and other Chiefs, were beginning to "use the protests' momentum to press Ottawa on treaty rights and improved living standards."<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The protests have been noted for creating a stream of polarized debate online, bringing out both supporters and detractors.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="January_11.2C_2013_meeting">January 11, 2013 meeting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Idle_No_More&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: January 11, 2013 meeting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Idle_No_More_2013_b.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Idle_No_More_2013_b.jpg/220px-Idle_No_More_2013_b.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Idle_No_More_2013_b.jpg/330px-Idle_No_More_2013_b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/Idle_No_More_2013_b.jpg/440px-Idle_No_More_2013_b.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="960" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Idle_No_More_2013_b.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Idle No More supporters occupying the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Hill" title="Parliament Hill">Parliament Hill</a> on January 11</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On January 4, 2013, Prime Minister Harper announced a meeting with a delegation of First Nation leaders coordinated by the Assembly of First Nations (AFN), to follow up on the issues discussed during the Crown-First Nations Gathering on January 24, 2012. His statement announcing the meeting made no mention of Idle No More.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The meeting was held on January 11, 2013.</p>
<p>The preparations were the subject of intense negotiation and debate within the AFN membership, until late in the evening of the eve of the meeting. Some chiefs voted not to participate, choosing to boycott the meeting for various reasons, including demands that more chiefs be included in the AFN delegation, questions over what to include on the meeting's agenda, and the fact that the Governor General would not be present throughout the meeting, and would be limited to a shorter ceremonial meeting after the meeting with Prime Minister Harper.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The day of the meeting, members of the Idle No More movement organized protests on Parliament Hill (drawing an estimated 3000 demonstrators),<sup id="cite_ref-CTV_News_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTV_News-71">[71]</a></sup> and in cities across Canada. Idle No More spokesperson Pam Palmater, who ran against Atleo for the position of National Chief with the AFN, said in a CBC discussion that the diverse positions expressed among the AFN chiefs "doesn't mean good things for the AFN".<sup id="cite_ref-CBC_News_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_News-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>The January 11 meeting was attended by National Chief Shawn Atleo and a delegation of chiefs from several provinces and the Yukon, AFN representatives from its Youth Council, Women's Council and Elders Council (Ontario and Manitoba chiefs boycotted the meeting). While it had previously been announced that Harper would only attend portions of the meeting, he attended the entire meeting. The meeting was also attended by Aboriginal Affairs Minister John Duncan, his Parliamentary Secretary <a href="/wiki/Greg_Rickford" title="Greg Rickford">Greg Rickford</a>, Cabinet Ministers <a href="/wiki/Tony_Clement" title="Tony Clement">Tony Clement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leona_Aglukkaq" title="Leona Aglukkaq">Leona Aglukkaq</a>, and senior officials from the Privy Council Office, Aboriginal Affairs and Northern Development Canada, and several other federal departments. The ceremonial meeting with Governor General Johnston was attended by around 100 chiefs, including Chief <a href="/wiki/Theresa_Spence" title="Theresa Spence">Theresa Spence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CBC_News_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_News-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the meeting, Aboriginal Affairs Minister John Duncan stated that it would be followed by "high-level dialogue" between Harper and Atleo,<sup id="cite_ref-CTV_News_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTV_News-71">[71]</a></sup> including follow-up meetings and more frequent reporting on Aboriginal matters by the federal government. <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Coon_Come" title="Matthew Coon Come">Matthew Coon Come</a>, a former AFN National Chief who has been confronted by protestors, attended the meeting, stated after the meeting that the "prime minister had moved a couple of posts forward", furthering discussions on treaty process and specific land claims, and that the prime minister indicated a willingness to consult with First Nations on environmental issues and legislative matters that impact Aboriginal territories.<sup id="cite_ref-CBC_News_72-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_News-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
