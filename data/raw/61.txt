			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Lebensreform</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Lebensborn" title="Lebensborn">Lebensborn</a>.</div>
<p><i><b>Lebensreform</b></i> ("life reform") was a social movement in late 19th-century and early 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> that propagated a back-to-nature lifestyle, emphasizing among others <a href="/wiki/Health_food" title="Health food">health food</a>/<a href="/wiki/Raw_food" class="mw-redirect" title="Raw food">raw food</a>/<a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">organic food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nudism" class="mw-redirect" title="Nudism">nudism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_liberation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual liberation">sexual liberation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a>, and religious reform and at the same time abstention from alcohol, tobacco, drugs, and vaccines.<sup id="cite_ref-hr_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fm_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Important <i>Lebensreform</i> proponents were <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Kneipp" title="Sebastian Kneipp">Sebastian Kneipp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Kuhne" title="Louis Kuhne">Louis Kuhne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Steiner" title="Rudolf Steiner">Rudolf Steiner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Wilhelm_Diefenbach" title="Karl Wilhelm Diefenbach">Karl Wilhelm Diefenbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fidus" title="Fidus">Fidus</a> (Hugo Höppener), <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Gr%C3%A4ser" title="Gustav Gräser">Gustav Gräser</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Just" title="Adolf Just">Adolf Just</a>. Some practitioners of <i>Lebensreform</i> such as <a href="/wiki/Bill_Pester" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill Pester">Bill Pester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Lust" title="Benedict Lust">Benedict Lust</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Ehret" title="Arnold Ehret">Arnold Ehret</a> emigrated to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in the late 19th (Lust) or first half of the 20th century and directly influenced the later <a href="/wiki/Hippie" title="Hippie">hippie</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-hr_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fm_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fm-2">[2]</a></sup> One group, called the "Nature Boys", settled in the California desert. <a href="/wiki/Eden_ahbez" title="Eden ahbez">eden ahbez</a>, a member of this group, wrote a hit song called <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_Boy" title="Nature Boy">Nature Boy</a></i> (recorded in 1947 by <a href="/wiki/Nat_King_Cole" title="Nat King Cole">Nat King Cole</a>), popularizing the homegrown back-to-nature movement to mainstream America. Eventually, a few of these Nature Boys, including <a href="/wiki/Gypsy_Boots" title="Gypsy Boots">Gypsy Boots</a>, made their way to Northern California in 1967, just in time for the <a href="/wiki/Summer_of_Love" title="Summer of Love">Summer of Love</a> in San Francisco.<sup id="cite_ref-hr_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>One noticeable legacy of <i>Lebensreform</i> in Germany today are the <i><a href="/wiki/Reformhaus" title="Reformhaus">Reformhaus</a></i> ("reform house") retail stores that sell naturopathic medicine and organic food.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lebensreform&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lebensreform movement in Germany originally was a politically diverse movement. There were hundreds of groups across Germany dedicated to some or all of the concepts associated with Lebensreform: ecology and organic farming, vegetarianism, <a href="/wiki/Naturism" title="Naturism">naturism</a> (<i>Nacktkultur</i>), and abstinence from alcohol and tobacco. Dozens of magazines, books, and pamphlets were published on these topics. Some groups were made of socialists, some were apolitical, and some were rightwing and nationalist in outlook.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams2007_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2007-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>One outstanding prophet of Lebensreform was the painter <a href="/wiki/Karl_Wilhelm_Diefenbach" title="Karl Wilhelm Diefenbach">Karl Wilhelm Diefenbach</a> (1861-1913), pacifist and tolstoyan anarchist who founded the community Himmelhof near Vienna. Among his disciples were three painters: Hugo Höppener (<a href="/wiki/Fidus" title="Fidus">Fidus</a>), Frantischek Kupka and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gusto_Graeser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gusto Graeser (page does not exist)">Gusto Graeser</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams2007_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2007-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1900 Graeser became the cofounder and inspiring pioneer of the community <a href="/wiki/Monte_Verit%C3%A0" title="Monte Verità">Monte Verità</a> near Ascona, Switzerland. Monte Verità attracted lots of artists from all of Europe, during World War I conscientious objectors from Germany and France. Gusto Graeser, thinker and poet, greatly influenced the German Youth Movement and such writers as <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Hesse" title="Hermann Hesse">Hermann Hesse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerhart_Hauptmann" title="Gerhart Hauptmann">Gerhart Hauptmann</a>. He was the model for the master figures in the books of Hermann Hesse. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ungewitter" title="Richard Ungewitter">Richard Ungewitter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Pudor" title="Heinrich Pudor">Heinrich Pudor</a> were also well known advocates of a strain of Lebensreform that emphasized nudism and was explicitly anti-Semitic, which eventually became <a href="/wiki/Freik%C3%B6rperkultur" title="Freikörperkultur">Freikörperkultur</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams2007_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2007-4">[4]</a></sup> The Freikörperkultur movement eventually broadened and came to include socialists with no strains of racism like <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Koch" title="Adolf Koch">Adolf Koch</a>, who for example organized factory workers to go on nudist hikes.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams2007_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2007-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contemporary environmental movements, the <a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">organic food</a> movement, many <a href="/wiki/Fad_diets" class="mw-redirect" title="Fad diets">fad diets</a> and "back to nature" movements, as well as "folk movements", have their roots in the Lebensreform movement's emphasis on the goodness of nature, the harms to society, people, and to nature caused by industrialization, the importance of the whole person, body and mind, and the goodness of "the old ways".<sup id="cite_ref-Williams2007_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2007-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:40</sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fitz_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fitz-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:32-33</sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another stream based on <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkisch" class="mw-redirect" title="Völkisch">völkisch</a> Romanticism gradually became part of Nazi ideology by the 1930s, known as <a href="/wiki/Blood_and_soil" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood and soil">blood and soil</a>. As early as 1907, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ungewitter" title="Richard Ungewitter">Richard Ungewitter</a> published a pamphlet called <i>Nudity and Culture</i> (which sold 100,000 copies), arguing that the practices he recommended would be "the means by which the German race would regenerate itself and ultimately prevail over its neighbours and the diabolical Jews, who were intent on injecting putrefying agents into the nation's blood and soil".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The extremists promoting rightwing ideology eventually became popular among Nazi Party officials and their supporters, including <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Heinrich Himmler</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudolph_H%C3%B6ss&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rudolph Höss (page does not exist)">Rudolph Höss</a>, who belonged to the right-wing farming organization the <a href="/wiki/Artaman_League" title="Artaman League">Artaman League</a>. When other groups were being banned or disbanded due to political conflict during the 1930s, the extreme nationalist ideology became connected with Naziism. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Life_Reform_League&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="German Life Reform League (page does not exist)">German Life Reform League</a> broke apart into political factions during this time. The Nationalist physician Artur Fedor Fuchs began the League for Free Body Culture (FKK), giving public lectures on the healing powers of the sun in the "Nordic sky", which "alone strengthened and healed the warrior nation".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Ancient forest living, and habits presumed to have been followed by the ancient tribes of Germany, were beneficial to regenerating the Aryan people, according to Fuchs' philosophy. Hans Sùren, a prominent former military officer, published <i>Man and the Sun</i> (1924), which sold 240,000 copies; by 1941 it was reissued in 68 editions. Sùren promoted the Aryan <a href="/wiki/Master_race" title="Master race">master race</a> concept of physically strong, militarized men who would be the "salvation" of the German people.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently, Germany has been experiencing a growth of rightwing extremist organizations with ecological connections, including organic farming. Since the 1990s, a number of rightwing farmers have set up organic farming operations in <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a>. The politically far-right environmental-interest magazine <i>Umwelt und Aktiv</i> (Environment and Active) is believed to receive support from the far-right nationalist <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="National Democratic Party of Germany">National Democratic Party of Germany</a> (NPD).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> German publications <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Spiegel" title="Der Spiegel">Der Spiegel</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCddeutsche_Zeitung" title="Süddeutsche Zeitung">Süddeutsche Zeitung</a></i> have also covered the link between far-right extremists and certain organic farming projects in Germany, and it has received government and academic attention as well. Speculation is that such rightwing projects may be attempts to revive the Nazi-era <a href="/wiki/Artaman_League" title="Artaman League">Artaman League</a>, to soften the image of the NPD, or to pull supporters away from the leftwing <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Alliance '90/The Greens</a> (Green Party) of Germany, which is Europe's most successful environmentalist political party.</p>
